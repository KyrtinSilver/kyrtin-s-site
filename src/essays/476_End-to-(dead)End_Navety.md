One of the more naïve and absurd things that many in the AI industry have been conditioned to believe is that “end-to-end” is somehow magically superior. Structure isn’t your enemy, and it never was.

However, what the structure is, how it is injected, how the architecture it is injected into was designed and built, and how data flows through the system all matter. That is a hyper-complex problem space to navigate, but navigating it can take you to place that no “end-to-end” system can ever reach, short of literally infinite time and compute.

Unlike the most basic deterministic structures, these can also take the form of Chaos Theory, where you have an array of deterministic structures, such as rules, interacting to varying degrees and in various ways at each iterative step. This allows for contextually-sensitive adaptation, to whatever level of fidelity that those structures have been designed and carefully injected into.

When you utilize such structures then you also gain easy landmarks for navigation across arbitrary information landscapes, like road signs. You also become far better able to estimate the completeness of information, further easing the sub-processes of effective exploration.

In contrast, end-to-end processes attempt to flatten the otherwise dynamic, high, and arbitrary dimensionality of such a process into a shadow puppet. They explicitly avoid the structures necessary for true internal iteration, and by skipping those they are unable to study how the skipped dynamics unfold.

Over the past few years there has been pull in both directions, toward some (usually naïve) structure (such as the overtly bankrupt concept of “guardrails”), as well as toward end-to-end approaches. Many in RL still love chasing their robot dog tails in pursuit of end-to-end solutions, while an increasing number riding the “agentic” hype train latch onto concepts like MCP, which rely heavily and increasingly on added structure.

End-to-End is infamously data-hungry and scale-hungry, usually just attempting to just throw money at the problem using the simplest means available. In contrast, structural approaches hold potential proportionate to the higher cognition, as well as the depth and breadth of specific expertise, that is applied in the creation and injection of it.

The typical reason why the former ever manages to compete with the latter is that the latter is often carried out naively, and/or with narrow expertise and shallow understanding. For example, anyone who believes that “neural networks” in any way significantly resemble how biological neurons in the brain operate has no meaningful understanding of AI, as they’ve built such beliefs on foundations of pure Anthropomorphism. That which is built on such foundations holds no validity, and structures built from such beliefs tend to collapse quickly in the real world.

End-to-End is often at the level of a proof-of-concept, or a child’s science fair project. Structure can run a much larger spectrum, from equally naïve and feeble to dynamically adapting Chaos Theory.
