Planck’s Principle (Max Planck) states: “A new scientific truth does not triumph by convincing its opponents and making them see the light, but rather because its opponents eventually die and a new generation grows up that is familiar with it …”

However, this dynamic isn’t limited to new scientific truths, but rather it applies to any new and similarly disruptive change. It can apply to matters of scientific progress, but it can also apply to (Higher) Cognitive Offloading, “brain rot”, or any other net-negative dynamic being normalized in the world today.

This is also by no means new, as even war crimes have been normalized, for a time, when committed by specific countries. History has not produced steady progress in this respect by any stretch of the imagination, but rather it has tended to take 4 steps forward, and 3 steps back, before toppling over drunk and passing out for a few decades.

Despite Euro-centric history remembering a few hundred years as “The Dark Ages”, the myth of human progress’s consistency can be a particularly persistent delusion. Likewise, there are no guarantees that humanity won’t slide into another deep pit, at an even larger scale, if a sufficiently idiotic choice is made at a large enough scale, and to the tune of significant enough consequences.

The new “Hold my Beer” is “cognitive offloading” to AI. In the business world, it has become the “(shrug) F^&\* it” option on every boardroom table.

Planck’s Principle also takes on a much darker reality when considered in the context of Population Dynamics that carry intentionality. That is, when the “opponents” of some activity, such as humanity’s current widespread and degenerative “higher cognitive offloading”, are selectively targeted and removed by any variety of malevolent means.

One of the greatest mistakes that anyone can make is to assume that there is no opponent with malicious intentions, generally speaking. In the AI domain, such bad actors with malicious intentions and means are a known quantity, to the point that many of them are household names, like Sam Altman, Dario Amodei, or Jensen Huang. They are often even praised for their “success”, not unlike Bernie Madoff before them.

Planck’s Principle cuts both ways, noting a resistance to change, irrespective of whether that change is a net-positive or net-detriment to science and/or society. When facing bad actors, the acceleration and targeting of when “opponents eventually die” can quickly become a deciding factor for the end result. Likewise, bad actors can be made to fall in financial terms, which tends to quickly lead to prison sentences for them thereafter.

In scientific advancement, as well as many measures of societal progress, Sun Tzu has a far greater role to play than many people seem to realize. Humanity doesn’t advance based on some value of merit, in part because nobody calculates it. Rather, it advances through time and crushing victories.
