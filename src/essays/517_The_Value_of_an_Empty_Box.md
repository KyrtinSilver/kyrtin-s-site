People often underestimate (or overlook) the value of an empty box in a schematic. That is to say, a designated place where something may be added to it.

This came to mind during a nice discussion with a professor recently, as we discussed practical opportunities for adding value to the various AI systems people are building today.

The thing about such an empty box in particular is that designating that place where something new may be added offers several distinct kinds of value:

1\. It casually reduces the cognitive effort required for adding new value, by designating a place for it, removing a layer of uncertainty and ambiguity that would otherwise tend to be present.

2\. This can also remove bottlenecks where some domain knowledge is required to recognize the places where such additional factors and features may be added.

3\. The empty box itself cultivates a creative motivation to answer the question of “What new kind of value can we add here?”

This combination of reducing the required level and kinds of cognitive effort and domain knowledge while encouraging creative construction pairs very well together in practice.

When working with systems that hold degrees of modularity and scalability, this becomes even more potent. A modular system that gets scaled up might become subject to a new set of dominant dynamics, such as some new market forces or types of data, where a strong incentive to fill such an empty box with a corresponding capacity suddenly emerges.

This also illustrates how such empty boxes can serve the function of risk mitigation, and timely adaptation, conveying flexibility to systems that might otherwise break down under such pressures.

Given the value that such empty boxes can add to almost any diagram, they should also be considered a resource worth “keeping in stock” whenever possible. When those boxes are filled, the next set should be added. This keeps the system that they’re attached to prepared for adaptation, with down-payments on the required cognition, motivating creativity and removing bottlenecks of domain knowledge as well as preparation time.

For example, something from Google Deepmind just recently came out, “Vision Banana” ([https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.20329](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.20329)), claiming distinctly new capacities for taking images and “reading” them for segmentation, metric estimation, and other value that might be extracted from such visual data. Companies who’ve built up what they thought to be “moats” around this not being the case are now scrambling to adapt.

Companies that build with those empty boxes in mind, as well as keeping them in stock, may much more quickly, easily, and intelligently adapt under such pressures, integrating new capacities utilizing such tools into their products and services as the winds change.
