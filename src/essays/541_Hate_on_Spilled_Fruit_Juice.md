Building on my prior post related to unconscious sensory perception, it is also worth noting both that the “experiencing self” and “remembering self” diverge sharply, and that emotional associations aren’t necessarily subject to the same influences as those of the conscious experience and memory. This also plays into the subtle differences that separate utterly mediocre products from the exceptional few that have benefited from systematic care being applied to their design.

Take the simple and every-day example of two cartons of fruit juice. Two brands, “Malee” (a Thai brand), and “Vinamilk”, use the same plastic cap design, but a difference in the shape of the cartons themselves causes Malee’s to pour out smoothly (complex design, partial octagon), whereas Vinamilk’s quickly succumbs to liquid turbulence (square design), making the latter vastly more likely to spill out from either side than the former.

Most people might not explicitly choose to remember this difference, but their emotional preferences will reliably be impacted any such experienced sources of predictable irritation, even those that aren’t consciously remembered (or for that matter, even consciously experienced). This is because the emotional processes of prediction span both unconscious and conscious, as well as guiding decision-making processes (Damasio).

Personally, that predictable source of irritation, the liquid turbulence of a poorly designed carton, evokes a small but meaningful degree of hatred every single time it is experienced, and that never truly goes away, it is only mitigated by the quality of the product as it is consumed, effectively “making amends” for the poor carton design. However, having to compensate for that shortcoming does render the resulting product mediocre as a whole, because even if the contents are good, the turbulence subtracts from that value.

A more potent example of this difference is found in calculating the air turbulence (and subsequent noise) of fans, with Dyson’s well known (and extremely expensive) products carefully factoring that consideration into their engineering. Similar companies like Noctua, in the computer fan market, are known for doing the same thing, specifically to reduce noise while still delivering performance. Of course, “luxury” products also have their effective value strongly degraded proportionate to how overpriced they are, so while I might buy a Noctua fan, I wouldn’t buy a Dyson.

The same principles can apply to any given product or service. That extremely sleazy “AI button” that couldn’t be removed from Adobe software merited a much stronger hatred response every single time I saw it, with no real positive value to mitigate that loss, leading to all of Adobe’s malware now being uninstalled from my systems.

Likewise, the principles of how emotional experience persists, accumulates, and is used for decision-making factor into products and services through association, when that association is rendered predictable. For example, if a platform offering some products or services integrates advertising, even if that advertising is unrelated to the products and services offered by the platform, then the presence of those advertisements merits only the strongest hatred response, and that emotional response is tied directly to the platform and whatever it offers.

When designing products and services, as well as the platforms hosting them, the entire process must be considered both systematically and mindfully, from start to finish. Quality Control issues where some products are defective can introduce highly harmful uncertainties, but it can be every bit as bad to have the complete certainty of something that you truly hate.

I fully expect new markets to emerge related to this, given time, as many solutions to common pain points for products and services can spread far and wide, once the need is recognized. That adoption of solutions may in turn be incentivized and accelerated by systems that replace traditional “marketing” and “advertising”, by instead testing, rating, reviewing, and recommending (even proactively) with far less statistical noise, no bribery, and reduced cognitive bias relative to existing review and recommender systems today.
