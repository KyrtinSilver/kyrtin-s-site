Beyond the “Gaping holes where a product should be” that I mentioned previously, the computer hardware market is a curious example of a place where, at any given moment, a portion of the critical components are nearing their next generations, respectively. In practice this means that there is always something to wait for the release of, and never a truly ideal time to buy.

Within such expansive and dynamic systems, companies making the same kind of product may operate on similar timelines, or alternating ones, depending on the conditions of competition between them. However, a computer case manufacturer doesn’t necessarily coordinate product releases with a CPU or GPU manufacturer, let alone the extremely long cycle products like generations of RAM.

In years previous, this was largely mitigated by having only a couple of major technology showcases each year, putting pressure on companies to coordinate the launch of new products (or at least the revealing of their launch dates) at those fixed times. However, the past 4 years have changed this significantly, as even the annual showcase with “Consumer” in the name (CES) virtually abandoned consumers, with major companies announcing zero new products for consumers, and talking a bunch of easily debunked bullshit about AI. Beyond infamous bad actors like Jensen, the PC hardware market is a good example of a space that requires centralized orchestration, absent centralized profit/incentive. The technology showcase conferences are a good, but very weak, example of limited coordination, but limited is far from the limit. Overcoming it requires overcoming hyper-complexity, but that too is an option.

A central platform could be constructed specifically for the coordination of:

-   manufacturing (product releases),
    
-   third-party testing (like GamersNexus),
    
-   compatibility testing,
    
-   build showcasing (such as overclocking and custom aesthetic designs),
    
-   design assistance (such as tiered recommendations for each type of build, integrating the results of third-party testing),
    
-   and so on.  
    

To be credible that platform would need to operate with precisely ZERO “ad-revenue”, and if it was used to actually order the parts in question then it could coordinate with various other platforms like Amazon or Newegg, utilizing referral programs at an industrial scale. Eventually, a better solution for this might become feasible, but global supply chains are high-cost and generally long-term infrastructure, in addition to their complexity, making coordination among selling platforms the low-hanging fruit for the fulfillment of orders.

There are many benefits that may be extracted from centralization, just as the same is true of decentralization in other respects. The key to optimal dynamics is recognizing the value in both, and integrating centralized and decentralized systems for their respective optimal purposes.

In the example of such a PC market hardware platform, as noted above, a central benefit is a dramatic reduction in redundancy of efforts, where 100,000 people may each perform the same laborious comparisons, or they may access a platform that automatically handles the comparison process. It isn’t difficult to imagine which choice the overwhelming majority of people will make, when given the option.

A good PC build may require a solid week or more of research, which might be reduced to an hour given such tools. The same is true of many other markets across numerous domains, where many of the same principles and dynamics are shared.
