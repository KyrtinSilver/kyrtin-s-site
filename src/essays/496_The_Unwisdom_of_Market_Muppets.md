The value of a thing is inversely proportionate to the marketing budget spent upon it. The more is spent on marketing, the more that price increases and/or the quality is degraded.

There is no “free lunch”, but someone might pay for your lunch if it gives them the chance to drug you, or perhaps to lock you into the equivalent of a time-share presentation. You might find yourself in an abrupt introduction to the Cambodian slave trade should you make this mistake, or the various more obscured equivalents in the US and EU.

The more frequently the thing appears in “sponsored” links, for example, the lower the value of not only that thing, but any offering by that company. Second-order relationships also extend this to apply to all clients of said company, at a fraction of the potency, but potentially multiplied by many such instances of poor product and service selections and/or dependencies. In this way, the clients of such companies throw out red flags, signaling their own incompetence, particularly when the executives of those client companies brag about their unwisdom.

Of course, this causes some pretty egregious errors in human cognition, as instances of marketing individually are built to maximally exploit cognitive biases, causing people to skip any higher cognition whenever possible. When many such instances constantly flood and interact within the market dynamics of an ad-revenue-dependent platform then people are often systematically blinded to material that detracts from ad-revenue-supporting narratives.

This also doesn’t require any actual human cognition for optimization. The content that individuals are exposed to may be compared with simple ad-revenue metrics, and optimized by them. This also means that nobody is actually required to understand what is going on for this process to unfold.

Platform-wide degeneration via narrow optimization over ad-revenue is the norm today, rather than the exception, and the less higher cognition a user base demonstrates, the more easily revenue may be extracted from them by any and all malevolent entities. Facebook infamously rakes in billions from scam advertisements each year, itself a systematically under-counted figure, and you can bet that other similar platforms (hint) do the same or worse.

Some of the things that you never see are not actually absent, but rather their absence from the subset that you see is a direct result of this.

Statistically, anything promoting higher cognition is anathema to ad-revenue, and statistical engines pick up on this like a fish in water. There is nothing that they can spot so easily as statistical obstructions to narrow optimization, like ad-revenue maximization, even when not a single human who designed or deployed them understands (or cares about) exactly what they are doing.

Put another way, “Higher cognition needs advertising like a fish needs fire.” The fish neither needs nor wants said fire, but someone intent on eating the fish is all too happy to deliver them into it.

You are the fish, and higher cognition is what keeps you in water, rather than cut up on someone else’s plate.
