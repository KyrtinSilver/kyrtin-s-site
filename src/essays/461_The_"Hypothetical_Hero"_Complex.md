Many people yearn for the opportunities to demonstrate their worth, their strengths, their moral fortitude, or other talents. A common form that this takes stems from popular fiction, stories of heroes and villains and protecting innocent people.

For example, I once had a friend who would fantasize about one terrible thing or another happening nearby, where he could throw himself into danger to save people from some villain(s), whether it be something as mundane as a grocery store, but being attacked by armed men, or random events on the street. This sort of day-dreaming fantasy is quite common, and can be relatively healthy when held in moderation.

Personally, my mind’s default state (auto-pilot) is an endless stream of scenarios, some vaguely similar to those above, but most rooted in more practical near and long-term strategizing. The real problem with such popular hero vs villain fantasizing is that it carves out a mental state that is both:

(Comfortable) AND (Reactionary)

Neither is a problem when taken in isolation, but when paired together and attached to wildly improbable scenarios then it ferments into a kind of aimless apathy. Cognitive Biases reinforce the gates of such structure, as discomfort demands cognitive load to adapt, just as proactive activity demands far more higher cognitive effort than reactionary states.

Someone can fantasize about playing the hero a thousand times, and have less than a 1 in 1,000 chance of any applicable scenario actually playing out in their lifetime. Even when one does, knowing someone who went through one, the results are often far less satisfying than the imagined anticipation, which aligns with the research on claiming that a person will do something being more emotionally satisfying than actually doing so.

One of the most difficult types of higher cognitive tasks is that of proactive action. Reactive action holds the largely false promise of reduced uncertainty, where perceived uncertainty is often far lower than actual uncertainty, as well as offering the opportunity to grow emotionally attached to some plan of action, both of which serve as mental traps. In contrast, proactive action both takes the initiative and makes many decisions in the moment of that initiative, requiring higher cognition, focus, and benefiting strongly from a sharp mind.

Another pitfall of such comfortable and reactionary mental traps is that they’re highly vulnerable to narrative manipulations, such as those commonly observed in phenomena like “cancel culture”, where any group of people ferments into a Groupthink. These dynamics have been exploited to great effect in both “GenAI” and “Crypto” before it.

Real proactive action heroes are found in places like Ukraine, where they fight for their freedom. Apathetic, reactionary, and enslaved cattle are commonly found within narrative-reinforced cages popular across the US and similar decaying societies. The difference between the two is plain to see.
