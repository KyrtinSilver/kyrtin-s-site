Many “AI Benchmarks” today make one particularly simple mistake that wholly invalidates any claims that they might make. This is separate from the requirements to make a genuinely good benchmark, leaving that a much higher bar than baseline (and increasingly uncommon) success today. The problem is this:

-   If you use another “Deep Learning” based algorithm to create “synthetic (fake) data”, or to curate real data from a dataset, then you are inherently placing the stamp of that algorithm on the data, carving a predictable and new path of least resistance through it.  
      
    
-   In the case of “synthetic data” this means that the algorithm will often follow that carved path of least resistance through the data back to some approximation of the original algorithm.  
      
    
-   In the case of data curation, the resulting dataset will predictably be weakened, with a new path of algorithmic predictability carved through the metaphorical landscape.  
      
    
-   For both cases, the problem is generally going to be both far easier and very different relative to the original stated intention of the benchmark.  
      
    

This is an increasingly severe problem, as people claiming to be researchers have become very prolific, pushing out what amounts to fake benchmarks and research papers, which major tech companies are happy to endorse, as each plays into the various flavors of PR (BS) that they use, lacking almost any real technical value for their products.

You can use many simple and deterministic algorithms, or statistically “noisy” humans, to create the datasets for real benchmarks, as neither one can predictably cause the kind of human-incomprehensible curve-fitting necessary to carve these new paths of least resistance through the data. It doesn’t matter in the slightest that the paths aren’t human-comprehensible for common “AI models” today, as they were never anything remotely like human thought or cognitive architecture to begin with.

The technology that they’re based on is built to find those kinds of breadcrumbs, absent the faintest shred of “understanding” or “reasoning”, but the trail of breadcrumbs makes it much more trivial to do so over any terrain.

The models that excel at such Faux-AI-Benchmarks are solving a problem, it is just a completely different problem than the claims made, and usually a far easier one, with little or no real-world value. Look under the hood and you’ll find some variation on “Cognitive Dissonance Man”, not what you wanted.

As noted in months previous, SWE-Bench Verified was itself “verified” in research to be fraudulent, debunking all claims of value from it. This should come as no surprise, given that OpenAI had some involvement in that variation on SWE-Bench.

Many other benchmarks have faired no better, and some even worse, like the exposed fraud of FrontierMath, which actually received news coverage.

Today, the “Tech Companies” peddling scamware still frequently cite SWE-Bench Verified scores, pretending that it isn’t already debunked, and it serves as a litmus test to highlight bad actors in AI.
