The damage of Bureaucracy is both severely and systematically underestimated, every day, and around the world.

1\. Baseline: What is the baseline volume of utterly wasted time? Does that time pressure compete with or compound upon other factors?

2\. Uncertainty: What happens when one digit is wrong on a form?

(which is itself nearly guaranteed to be redundant and/or entirely worthless)

\*Also, don’t forget to account for the added Stress + Time Wasted in attempts to be “extra careful”, due to this being recognized by those who are ultimately impacted by any such errors.

3\. Remediation: When something does goes wrong, how difficult and/or expensive might it be to correct?

(Many forms of bribery and ideological discrimination tend to appear when this happens)

4\. Severity: How severe are these combined costs and consequences?

(...including the long-tail of the probability distribution, where the consequences can become truly absurd in their severity)

5\. Cascade: How can a problem in this one event cascade into subsequent failures? What are those second and third-order effects?

6\. Scale: At what scale is this particular crime against humanity being committed? Does it pointlessly waste this time and cause all of these other factors for 100 people, or 100 million?

7\. Adversarial Optimization: Is a particular system adversarially designed and/or optimized to negatively impact some particular group(s), that is(are) otherwise unrelated to the system in question?

(In the US, this set of methods are commonly observed in voting regulations aimed at suppressing the voting of various minority groups)

Even the above list is likely incomplete, but sufficient to make the point.

Some countries, and some domains, appear to be moving toward more (and more damaging) Bureaucracies, as has been repeatedly observed in the US for decades. However, others do genuinely appear to be automating away many of the places where it serves a net-detrimental purpose.

The Kuala Lumpur International Airport has, for example, automated passport gates, taking only seconds to walk up and scan one, rather than hours waiting on an immigration officer, as well as automated kiosks for check-in and luggage tags. There remains much more that could be automated with no losses to integrity, but it puts them well ahead of the curve.

The archaic and barbaric practices of bureaucracy, like “notaries” and passport stamps, are gradually being digitized, rendered interoperable and instantaneous, before getting automated away. Even as some places double down on the Combinatorial Absurdities of Bureaucracy, others move forward.
