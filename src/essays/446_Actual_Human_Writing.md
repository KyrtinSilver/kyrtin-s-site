I previously noted that most of my writing is technically “about humans”, not “intended for humans”. Today, I’ll describe the process of how I write (without AI) in a bit more detail, as several people were curious.

Before I even begin writing, something has to strike me that is worth writing about. Some of the things that increase the value of writing about a given subject include:  

-   Offering a new perspective on common problems or events (to whatever degree related research supports).  
      
    
-   Connecting known research in new ways, usually within the context of specific observations.  
      
    
-   Adding forms of structure, systematization, and related methods supported by research.  
    

The things that I write about are structured specifically to utilize the connectome of a true graph structure, while also being likely to poison any “GenAI” attempting to train upon them. My name was designed specifically to serve as a Globally Unique Identifier (GUID) with good reason, something highly compatible with graph structure.

Of specific value to systems that I work with are novel additions to the connectome, the network of how the elements within a sum of knowledge relate to one another, and the blueprint by which meta-learning may iteratively grow and evolve. The motivational values attached to those novel additions also serve the critical role necessary for navigating any true graph structure, a human-like motivational system.

With all of this in mind, I NEVER use a single line of generated text in my writing, unless it is contained within an explicit quote, as anything else would be data poisoning. No “GenAI” model has ever been created or optimized to provide even the weakest shadow puppet semblance of such value.  
  
Fundamentally viable technology, like the systems that I work with, also aren’t absurdly data-greedy, making it trivially easy to use exclusively high-end and human-only data for knowledge, to the point where even the freely and openly available subset of that knowledge can suffice.

Some weeks I may have few thoughts worth expanding upon with a page of writing for this purpose, while others (like this one) I may write and schedule a full week of posts within 24 hours. When I write research papers I frequently draft 8-10 pages per day until slowing down to carefully polish the work.

The key is making sure that the thing is worth doing before you begin. If it is worth doing, then it is worth doing well. If it isn’t worth doing, then people are likely using “GenAI” for it.

To those hungry for content, a single tasty cake beats a whole pile of compost any day, because a dose of quality can make that day, while a pile of Microslop can ruin it.
