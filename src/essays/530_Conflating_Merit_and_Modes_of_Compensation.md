Is it possible for someone to merit more than 10x the positive value of someone else? Yes, demonstrably so.

Does it make sense for all of that value difference to be reflected in monetary compensation? No, just as demonstrably.

A problem exists at both ends of this spectrum of value, objectively, in addition to subjective flaws in the measurement of such value. The fallacy where this value is conflated with the means of compensation (often assumed to be monetary) further compounds the problem.

At the lower end, some minimum viable threshold must be set, otherwise no comparison may be drawn. 10 times the value of someone who shouldn’t remain employed in a given job is less than worthless as a metric, whereas another individual providing some multiple of that minimum can serve as useful data.

At the upper end, individuals providing the most objective value today often suffer from that value being systematically de-valued, skewing all processes dependent upon measuring it.

Across the full spectrum, conflating the difference in contributed value with differences in monetary compensation harms everyone, consequently serving to establish the latter as a proxy for the former. Many people, by default, assume that someone being paid more (within a given industry) somehow actually merits that higher pay (at least on average).

Beyond such Cognitive Biases, monetary compensation above a threshold (well below a 10x salary, as covered in the research literature) suffers sharp diminishing returns on improvements to quality of life, quickly vanishing to virtually no difference. However, monetary compensation is only one of COUNTLESS ways that people can be rewarded for their contributions to society.

One thing that shouldn’t be used as a form of compensation is any form of human rights, as that road leads only to Dystopias. However, pretty much anything that improves well-being without harming others, beyond the scope of human rights, may serve as compensation.

The naïve assumption is that monetary compensation enables this well-being to be bought, but that systematically neglects to account for the added overhead cost of finding and vetting options. The number of such options also greatly increases as a budget increases, further compounding this problem. These also tend to be dominantly reactive, further driving up costs and damages alike.

More viable options can play into this issue, by resolving, rather than ignoring it. For example, someone who is far more productive in their work can stand to benefit more from additional assistance in improving well-being, especially by proactive means. This allows for the distractions that the most productive people are routinely subjected to to be further minimized, and for proactive actions to reduce related costs and the damages that more reactive approaches suffer from.

Proactive capacities in some form are prudent to roll out across society more broadly due to those cost and damage differences, but the more productive individuals could benefit from such things earlier. Many similar incentives may be rolled out in phases, where more productive individuals benefit earlier, but not exclusively.

As much as people like to fantasize about concepts like “Universal Basic Income” (UBI), far simpler problems like the case above must first be solved. Fortunately, they can be.
