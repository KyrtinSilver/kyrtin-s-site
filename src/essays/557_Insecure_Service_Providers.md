I recently woke up at an odd hour and noticed an unspecified app attempting to download Text-to-speech updates, that were blocked by some customizations to my Wi-Fi router, and after disabling, nuking, and uninstalling a few apps it was indeed the usual culprit, Google, attempting to download entirely unauthorized content. I was consequently reminded of more ways that Asymmetric Warfare could be applied to mobile networks.

Extending the previous example of (directly) bankrupting bad actors, immediately after that bankruptcy several limited opportunities appear, during that phase of panic following an unexpected bank account balance of zero. The question then becomes, who among their accomplices in crime do they reach out to first, and how do you track the activity?

The marginally more intelligent bad actors will act under the assumption that their devices and/or other accounts have been compromised, so direct tracking and tampering is less likely to work against them, but direct is far from the limit. Mobile phone carriers and Internet Service Providers (ISPs) are generally no more secure at the infrastructure level than the average bank, so if a bad actor’s bank account may be emptied, so too may the network providers handling their calls, messages, and emails to their closest accomplices.

This is an example of a far more reliable method of stirring the nest to flush out a broader criminal network from one panicked bad actor. If you operate in bulk, such as at the state-sponsored level, and the methods can’t trace back to you (a very big “if”) then you can exploit the larger number of less competent bad actors at scale via direct methods, but to catch the big fish in that septic pond might require more advanced methods.

If many such bad actors are bankrupted within the span of an hour or two, then an additional opportunity comes into play to kick one predictable mass-accomplice in the pants, FDIC. The basic principle is that the bad actors will all attempt to recover $250,000 each, and should FDIC do so then it becomes an accomplice to the crimes of those bad actors, multiplied by the number of bad actors.

If FDIC awarded $250,000 each to 100 different bad actors then they’d instantly become a criminal accomplice to each of them, to the tune of $25m. Whatever additional penalties this might invoke from Ethics, the immediately predictable scope of retaliation for aiding such criminals would be to double that drain, extracting an additional $25m from them to further fund the war on fraud.

This would be deeply ironic, of course, but FDIC has no protections in place to prevent them funneling massive sums of money to known frauds like the employees of OpenAI or Anthropic, and so this would fall squarely within expectations.

The ultra-wealthy bad actors take a wide variety of less conventional means to shelter, hide, and shuffle around large sums of money beyond banks, but the Cybersecurity of such methods only tends toward rock-bottom for most of those less conventional means. Instead, those methods rely more on being little-known financial tricks rather than robust Cybersecurity measures, often officially operating out of little island nations, or Switzerland.

For example, one trick a financial analyst told me about some years back was someone making more than $200,000 per year as a consultant, but paying themselves via loans from temporary companies that they’d never pay back, then collapsing the companies they were tied to each year. In this way, that individual avoided paying any taxes for a whopping 15 years, and was never caught. Rather, they asked the analyst if they should stop while they were ahead.

People raking in millions or even billions have similar tricks, often just operating at larger scales, like the various “Foundations” that bad actors like Jensen Huang use to write off their PR and R&D expenses as “charitable donations”, effectively avoiding billions in taxes, as was highlighted in the news recently.

When a major blow is landed on such criminals, within that moment where they panic and start checking all of their other (often hidden) assets, is where all of their assets and accomplices are most easily located, and before they can ramp up security on them. For example:

1.  Bad Actor A has an emptied bank account.  
    
2.  They panic, checking their other assets and reaching out to accomplices.  
    
3.  A system monitors their traffic as they do this, preparing each additional discovered asset for exfiltration, to be executed in quick succession once the bad actor pauses in revealing new assets and accomplices. This doesn’t need to be absolute, but may instead focus on maximizing irreversible damage, balanced for ease of execution.  
    
4.  Accomplices, once discovered, may be dealt with at leisure, leaving only the newly revealed assets to exfiltrate before additional measures may be taken to secure or move them.
    

The first blow may be significant, but the follow through on subsequent opportunities that it opens up determine if such bad actors are indeed shut down.
