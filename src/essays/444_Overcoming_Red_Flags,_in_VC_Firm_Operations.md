**Screaming Red Flags within VC Firm Operations, number 1:**

“A partner’s internal reputation can affect whether a deal gets approved. Investment committees do not only evaluate the startup. They also evaluate the conviction and credibility of the partner bringing it forward.”

“Conviction” and “credibility” are extremely messy proxies, with no inherent relation to the real value, risks, or uncertainties of a given investment opportunity. The process described is also bureaucratic, further compounding these critical flaws. The best startup approaching the worst partner gets heavily penalized.

This is a strong contributor to statistics like “75% of VC-backed startups fail”, as compared to ~90% of all startups. Added funds likely account for the overwhelming majority of that 15% difference, as the overall process for many VC firms remains closely approximated by Tetlock’s famous “dart-throwing chimpanzees”. This is also why around half of VC firms never turn a profit, or have a second round. They’re a literal coin flip.

**Solution:**

Daniel Kahneman came up with a method of evaluating candidates to reduce cognitive bias and integrate the structure necessary for stable grounding and comparison. The structure is as follows:

-   Designate a number of fixed criteria that are each to be graded on some scale, such as a score of 1 to 10.
    
-   Briefly describe why you gave each score.
    
-   Add notes for any material that doesn’t fit into those categories to the end of the evaluation.
    
-   Compare any two or more candidates jointly, using their scores, and discussing any added material.
    

This structure allows for strong and categorical grounding to any relevant pillars for consideration, while making it easier to compare any two or more candidates. This strong structural anchoring effect can then be pulled a more appropriate distance by any factors that fall outside of these categories.

This is a method of cognitive bias reduction using structure to mitigate many of the commonly exploited cognitive vulnerabilities of the human mind. Nowhere are those vulnerabilities more commonly exploited than wherever there is money on the table, so VCs need it far more than most.

Back in 2022 I was faced with the challenge of trying to estimate our startup’s actual value, based on comparison to a variety of other startups in the industry, and all metrics for what they offer the industry. I applied Kahneman’s method to this task, producing overall scores for every startup considered, allowing calculations to easily be run in a finance-friendly spreadsheet format.

The weakness of this method is that if one person using it grades on an exponential scale, and another on a logarithmic scale, then their scores won’t be compatible. For example, our team’s systems are 5+ orders of magnitude more data efficient and compute efficient than OpenAI, which only translates into scale of 1 to 10 if that scale is exponential. Keep this in mind when choosing your methods.
