In the past decade, most job seekers have come to recognize that 99.9% of time spent on “job applications” is entirely wasted effort. What many haven’t yet realized is that the same is equally true of every single “pitch deck” and other document or application with a similar one-time purpose whose performance is in no way anchored to reality.

In both cases, it is vastly more important “who you know” than “what you can do”, or even “what you’ve already done”. Dreams of meritocracy fall flat and promptly die a fiery death within this space, where nothing that you’ve done or can do truly matters, only that someone on the other side of the door props it open (or in most cases, doesn’t).

Put another way, current systems are more like qualifying a chef by how well they can code, or vice versa. The two things aren’t related, and many of these unrelated things tend to hold strong inverse correlations, meaning that you get worse results by using them than if you’d simply rolled dice to select a random decision.

For example, the strongest 10x coders are generally going to have an equally STRONG inverse correlation with the ability to market anything, meaning that they’ll likely have the worst pitch decks, all else being equal. These are delegated to other roles, like UX, communication, etc., who are technology-agnostic, having nothing to do with the innovation itself. These technology-agnostic roles are matters of funding, not innovation, and so funding for such entities is often determined by entirely unrelated factors, such as marketing polish.

This problem quickly compounds under selection pressure, since those who compete on marketing polish will tend to have little or no actual technology beneath that polish, while those who focus on technology and innovation will just as strongly tend to have little or no polish.

\*See Altman for an example of high polish, sh\*t technology, and $0 in subsequent profit.

This is what happens when Cognitive Biases maximally govern any hyper-complex process, weak and simple proxies with weak or inverse correlations to actual value are increasingly prioritized, as bad actors pander to them, up until a system collapses.

Of course, the problem is actually much worse than missed opportunities. Wasted time taken at such large scales quickly results in crimes against humanity being committed at those same scales. Those who fail to live up to their responsibilities are fully liable for this activity in ethical terms, if not legal terms.

One of the use cases I look forward to our technology resolving is this, putting a permanent end to that wasted time by deploying systems that handle the hyper-complex higher cognitive labor that we see so flagrantly neglected today. Neither job markets nor the investment space will have any excuse to maintain the status quo, and those who try will face bankruptcy, and legal recourse, in due time.

Not one minute of that wasted time will ever be forgiven, they will pay for it all.
