Most Ethical activity, by practical everyday volume, is also “Legal” activity, or close enough to it. At the scale of an individual going about their normal day, the volume of activity that is merely “close enough” to being legal is extremely high, while the impact of that activity is also extremely low.

This is one of those points where Dynamics prove vastly more accurate than “Rules”, as interacting forces illuminate what rules systematically fail to predict, let alone enforce. The volume, potency, and scope of the activity in question matters significantly, even before you begin to consider matters of corruption, circumstance, prior activity, or any factors external to a given instance.

The practical result that plays out every day, across the world, is a sliding scale of selective enforcement at a baseline, with many layers of further selection stacked on top of it. Much as cognitive biases are expansively and automatically used to greatly reduce the need for higher cognition in humans, legal governance systems today simply weren’t designed or built to handle either nuance or volume. Practicality thus frequently requires that nuances be glossed over and volume greatly reduced (with the selection process itself becoming a focal point for corruption).

It is perfectly possible for humans to adhere strictly to what is Ethical, ignoring or steamrolling over whatever “legal” system may exist, because most of the time the difference between the two is sufficiently marginal, low in impact, and/or small in scope that nobody really cares. I can personally attest to this, having done so for more than 20 years, including a few more dramatic demonstrations where the scope or impact was temporarily increased.

“Ethical” is the thing that “Legal” systems should aspire to be, within any working governance structure, while also accounting for whatever is feasible at the time, as well as taking steps to increase the portion of things that are both Ethical AND feasible. In some cases, this offers the opportunity to counter a misbehaving or corrupt legal system by folding it in on itself, such as exposing the corruption or illegal activities of entities behind some legal hostilities, be they corporate or government in origin.

It is also possible to codify such extrajudicial punishment, laying out the price in retribution for any enterprising criminals, effectively just introducing a second set of laws. When two or more such sets of laws exist, then it becomes a competition at any point where one structure attempts to act directly against the other.

For example, anyone familiar with the US government’s Cybersecurity infrastructure should understand which side would lose badly, should the US attempt to pick a fight with any genuinely intelligent software (not to be confused with trash technology, like “agents”) laying out a set of Ethics-aligned laws. The fundamental thing is that the moment the US’s military terrorism of “Air Superiority” can no longer “kill” a single target, such as an entity that can copy itself almost instantly across the world’s digital infrastructure, then the US loses the overwhelming bulk of offensive capacity, and its defensive capacity is utter sh\*t.

Beyond a certain point, you simply can’t have Bureaucratically-Aligned (legal) systems while avoiding human extinction. You can easily avoid extinction with Ethically-Aligned systems, and those systems may well codify new sets of Ethically-Aligned laws, while also being exceptionally well-designed, adapted, and positioned to enforce them.

For most people, most of the time, there might be no real difference between the Bureaucratically-Aligned laws of today and Ethically-Aligned ones, but that difference comes into sharp contrast when it matters.
