For any law or legal system that isn’t strictly and solely derivative of ethics (not to be confused with morals), there will be a decision to either be ethical, or to be legal. Sometimes the two may use the same methods and reach the same conclusions, and that may be used to measure how ethical a given legal system is. However, a decision is made in any case.

If the legal option is chosen then ethics is sacrificed, and an Ethical Debt is incurred by the decision-maker, as well as all other responsible parties capable of halting or preventing a given sequence of events.

For matters of any real consequence, particularly those crimes committed at scale, this Ethical Debt can quickly add up to an individual choosing the legal option earning themselves millions or billions of years worth of torture as their fully deserved punishment, or as close as the laws of physics and life extension technology may allow.

If the ethical option is chosen, then there may be some legal repercussions, depending on a thousand different factors. This is highly uncertain at even a baseline level, since most modern bureaucratic models of government are wildly inconsistent in if and how they actually go about enforcing any laws. Some laws are more commonly enforced than others, to put it mildly.

Beyond that baseline uncertainty, a few more factors come into play, such as:

-   Will any hostile actions against the ethical party cause more significant harm to the party claiming legal authority than alternatives, such as ignoring the activity? (see Mutually Assured Destruction)  
      
    
-   Will any pursuit of legal repercussions expose that “legal authority” to levels of transparency that they seek to avoid?  
      
    
-   Is such a “legal authority” even able to act against the ethical party?  
      
    
-   Are there acute risks of social backlash against the “legal authority”?  
      
    
-   Can the very methods that the “legal authority” might seek to make a fuss about be used against them?  
      
    \*Blackmail, coercion, etc. are methods naively associated with bad actors, but they may just as easily be used against bad actors by ethical parties.  
    

As with all such binary dilemmas, there is a third option. In this case, the third option is a truly terrible (unethical + illegal) combination, which the world’s third largest market, Cybercrime, is quite fond of. Cybercrime isn’t alone in choosing that combo either, as Nvidia, Microsoft, OpenAI, and Anthropic are right there beside them.

Of course, in terms of the Alignment Problem in AI, there is no real choice here. Without a system that chooses to be ethical rather than legal, humanity goes extinct, without exception or caveat.
