“Anarchism” is one of those words that most people usually wouldn’t give a second glance, except perhaps to ridicule, but one funny thing about it is that most people also don’t actually understand the meaning of the word, any more than they understand the names of rare and unfamiliar medical diagnoses. I must confess that I was guilty of the same, not giving it a second glance, until a particular individual began using it properly, and in context.

In the latest book (posthumously published on the late author’s behalf), David Graeber’s first essay notes how the term Anarchism is closely aligned with, and in previous centuries was treated as interchangeable with, another potent term, “Democracy”.

As he put it, “The basic principles of anarchism - self-organization, voluntary association, mutual aid, the opposition to all forms of coercive authority – are essentially moral and organizational”.

(\*Note, Graeber goes on to discuss how the term was closely associated with Unions, which may well be a large part of why it came to be demonized.)

This should all sound quite familiar, since these are all theoretically the same values that “Democracy” is supposed to represent, but which it fails to in any tangible sense for most countries today. This is partly because what people call “Democracies” are more specifically “Representative Democracies”, which are a brand of Republic, not Democratic at all.

The lie of modern “R-Democracies” is further made transparent by the judicial and legislative branches committing crimes, such as insider trading, while being treated as though they were immune to the very same laws that they create and claim to “enforce”. In the US, both sides of the political coin commit these crimes even while they are closely monitored, as they fear no reprisal from equally corrupt government structures of selective legal enforcement.

In the context of this modern reality, it becomes the most extreme of ironies that the term “Anarchism” carries with it such strong negative associations, being that the meaning of the word is still largely synonymous with actual Democracy, as it focuses on moral and organizational matters. Rather, the abominations that the term “Democracy” has come to be associated with today merit the very strong negative associations now applied to Anarchism.

“Admittedly, this flies in the face of the popular image of anarchist as bomb-throwing crazies opposed to all forms of organization…” - David Graeber
