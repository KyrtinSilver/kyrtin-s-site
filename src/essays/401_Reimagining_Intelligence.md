Earlier this week I had a good chat with Francis Gorman, now published on “The Entropy Podcast”, discussing AI, Cybersecurity, current events, and where society can go from there.  
[The Entropy Podcast](https://open.spotify.com/episode/135ZVCzJxYUy8JH13WPqQh)

We also discussed questions related to several of my previous posts at greater length, and I can highly recommend the “free-flowing conversation” format for these discussions, which the world could use more of. My previous appearance with Lasse Rindom followed the same format. From a methodological perspective, this approach seems to work best for knowledge integration when exploring novel concepts at the frontiers of technology, as well as other domains.

Everyone has these questions that come to mind, but the questions aren’t one-offs, they are dynamic sequences. To apply a quote:

“Uproot your questions from their ground and the dangling roots will be seen. More questions!”

To answer a single question is unremarkable, but to answer a dynamic sequence of many questions allows a much more robust understanding to form, across any or all of the angles that need to be formed. This also builds trust, being an iterative, interaction, and responsive learning process, since trust benefits from the removal of uncertainty over the angles of those subsequent questions.

Take the old metaphor of the three blind men and the elephant, for example, each describing what they feel and trying to extrapolate to what the entire elephant must be like. If any one of them were to iterate across the elephant, rather than being satisfied with a single static answer, then they could form that much more complete picture of what the elephant is truly like.

People who seek such a complete picture are often worth giving this opportunity to do so. In contrast, people who content themselves to extrapolate from the answers to canned questions absent such iteration are often worth little or nothing to anyone. Think of every form filled with such canned questions, recognizing that they can only highlight specific things, often those that were recognized in advance, while remaining perfectly blind to most of the rest, giving strong Survivorship and Confirmation Biases.

Humans (and various other creatures) learn through iteration, as do truly human-like systems, so this dynamic iteration where one question’s answer leads to new questions, each explored in turn and producing their own branching questions, offers one of the best opportunities for learning. You might compare it to “open world” games, as opposed to strictly scripted “theme parks”, because the freedom to explore the space in whatever ways are needed or wanted is a big part of the process.

I can highly recommend anyone who gets the chance to speak with Francis that they accept his invitation.
