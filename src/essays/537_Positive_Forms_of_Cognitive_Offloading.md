Not all “Cognitive Offloading” is inherently harmful, much of it functions as a trade-off. One example is that of the “Google Effect”, a cognitive bias where memorization is sacrificed when the knowledge is rendered available on-demand, such as via a search engine, like the cognitive bias’s namesake.

\*Note, if the data is at risk of corruption or deletion, this becomes maladaptive.

Another similar trade-off can be made with human working memory, where information is laid out and structured externally, allowing subsets of that data to be more easily re-examined in new combinations. This allows for structures that couldn’t otherwise fit into human working memory to be polished and refined.

One example of this trade-off to extend human working memory is carried out every time I write a research paper. As information is integrated into that document the contents grow beyond the bandwidth of human working memory, with the added benefits of both structure and citations. This process examines a given subject through the lens that such structure imposes, while also rendering portions of it more accessible to expand and reflect upon.

Examples of utilizing these additional opportunities to expand and reflect include for humans to read written content aloud, causing it to be processed through different regions of the brain, as well as comparing and contrasting the contents of a paper with other cited works.

Another form of cognitive offloading that can offer some benefits is the bounded automation of tedious (and in bureaucracies often entirely pointless) tasks. There is also subset of these cases where there is some benefit for a human to perform them a few times, but the human quickly decreases in output quality under the repetition of tedious tasks.

One such example of this kind of offloading was my use of an LLM to sample the probability distribution of related concepts, given some term or limited body of text. A human can carry out the same task while maintaining quality a limited number of times before quality decreases, but an LLM sampling that probability distribution to generate potentially related metadata is able to hold steady at a performance of roughly 20-30% high quality metadata, 50% mediocre metadata, and another 20-30% of poorly or unrelated metadata, the latter of which may be removed by the system during operation.

Another kind of cognitive offloading is specialization and delegation in general, where cognition and related decision-making is handed over to people, groups, or systems that are better specialized for the subject matter. This form of offloading is ubiquitous within modern organizations and society as a whole, with countless examples, both ideal and deeply flawed, aimed at this purpose.

One of the commonalities between viable methods is that they typically offer some potential for improvement over time. The Google effect frees up more cognitive bandwidth for higher cognition. Extensions of working memory do the same, while offering additional opportunities. The bounded automation of tedious tasks compensates for human declines in performance over time, while also reducing some forms of statistical noise. The specialization and delegation form of offloading is perhaps the most ancient and ubiquitous, even though it remains far from perfected today.

One of the common patterns across evolutionary history for the past ~1.5 billion years has been that increasing levels of complexity are paired with increasing levels of cooperation and specialization, via various forms of endosymbiosis. Cognitive offloading in general is an adaptation to some niche within a larger ecology, which itself may be an organism or metaorganism. Like evolution, not all adaptations are successful, but the subset that do succeed are more often passed on to flourish in future generations.

The producers and consumers of today’s “GenAI” brain-rot slop, those who encourage, enable, and themselves embrace the negative forms of cognitive offloading may have no place in the future, and that may well be for the best. A part of autonomy is that some people will choose self-destruction, but Population Dynamics means that as time moves forward, those who don’t tend to advance.
