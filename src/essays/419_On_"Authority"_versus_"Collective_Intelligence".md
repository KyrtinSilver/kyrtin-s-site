The concept of “Authority” is an amalgamation of Cognitive Biases in action, convenient lies and simplifications, mental short-cuts, most of which tend to either boil down to “I’m afraid of (…)” or “It is too much bother to resist (…)”. The irony of this is played out in the modern study of archaeology, where recent decades have peered into the lives of ancient cities where no such “Authority” existed. Like any concept, there was a time before the invention of it.

This butchering of reality by the lazy simplification and apathy of Cognitive Biases also plays out in how people perceive some speaking in any given field or industry to speak with “Authority” in it. Blatantly absurd titles, such as the “Godfathers of AI”, or the prestige of running companies which a given individual’s executive actions had little to do with the success of, are common proxies by which people assume individuals to be competent, even when they’re often anything but. More damagingly, such individuals are associated with some exceptional kind of competence.

Within my personal library of many books on research spanning a variety of fields, there are a fair number of individuals who’ve argued about the other authors. While several of the pop culture authors who I’ve read are objectively terrible, saying easily debunked things, like Yuval Harari or Steven Pinker, most are quite reasonable, like Kahneman, Graeber, Sunstein, Sinek, Taleb, Grant, Voss, or Barrett. Some of these names are thought of as an “authority” within their respective fields, but the study of their works within a much larger and more diverse corpus is always going to be a “higher authority” than any one of those works in isolation.

That is the nature of Collective Intelligence, which describes any process by which the cognitive biases of a group are systematically reduced through diligence and diversity.

It is both reprehensible and understandable that many people bow to such fictions as “authority” in the world today. The world is hyper-complex, dominated by levels of complexity that humans very simply can’t handle, and thus frequently “can’t be bothered” to deal with. Increase the complexity, absent viable systems of mitigation, and you increase the reliance on Cognitive Biases in general, and often “authority” crutches specifically.

Even in smaller nations today, elected and selected officials rarely hold sufficient real expertise, they employ and contract advisors and consultants, with each added step steeply degrading the process as a whole. The problem is that these are not Collective Intelligences, they are hierarchies, a generally degenerative structure. Ironically, such degenerative structures often strongly attract “degenerates”.

Hierarchies built to enshrine “authority” are a convenient lie, another “useful, but wrong” tool for the toolbox. Collective Intelligence systems are just the opposite, where improvements over the baseline are virtually unavoidable for any working system.
