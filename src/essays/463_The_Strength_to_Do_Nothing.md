Sometimes the hardest thing to do is nothing at all. This is one of the great ironies of modern life, as we’re constantly inundated with ways in which all of our time and attention might be flushed down one digital toilet or another.

The emotional pull of “the search for the next shiny object” is strong, often constant, and for many, the default. It is a collection of cognitive biases that drive humanity every day, exploited to great effect by the “dark patterns” baked into virtually every app and platform online today, from “newsfeeds” with infinite scrolling, to the red numbered notifications that emotionally demand your attention.

For people like myself, and many others, these problems are made more acute by the ways in which our investments of time tend to bottleneck. Everyone is subject to bottlenecks of time to some degree, whether you’re stuck waiting on a friend to get back to you, a colleague to complete their part of the work, or some larger event in the world to take place.

Each time humans hit such a bottleneck, that search for the next shiny object comes calling loudly and clearly. The most cognitively debilitated individuals may be recognized by the fidget spinners on their desks, or TikTok and Twitter usage on their phones.

This is where a busy and distracted mind sharply diverges from those who learn, grow, and develop their cognitive and emotional capacities. Part of that search for the next shiny object is to distract, to feed a neurochemical addiction to stimulation, but it also frequently draws on the emotional dislike of being bottlenecked in the ability to continue doing a thing, be it by known others or the world at large.

Part of overcoming the downward cognitive spiral that such fermenting dislike offers is a matter emphasized by Stoicism, to recognize where you end and the world begins, doing what you can, working with what you have, and leaving the rest to others. This is easier said than done, as it is easy to logically walk through and accept, but emotional acceptance and integration of the same takes time and effort.

Personally, I grew up having to get the same work done in half of the time given to others, which I did. In my adult life, I’ve quickly bottlenecked countless times because of this, making it absolutely vital to learn that lesson, which I eventually did.

Reaching that point also offers stronger benefits today than ever before. The number of dark patterns and cognitive vectors of attack are still growing quickly, and getting worse with time. AI-induced and AI-aided psychosis are being distilled down to a malevolent parody of science, as the “attention economy” explicitly aims for cognitive decline at scale.

Go for a walk, get away from your digital devices, learn some form of meditation, reflect on your life and your actions, or simply enjoy the silence.
