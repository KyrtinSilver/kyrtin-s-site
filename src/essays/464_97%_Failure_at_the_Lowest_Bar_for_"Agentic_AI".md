Once, people had “careers”, then came “jobs”, and then “outsourcing”, and finally “gig economies”. While each still exists to some degree, “gig economies” are a collection of methods now commonly deployed to pay the least amount of money, offering the least benefits and protections, in exchange for the lowest quality of work. Even at that lowest bar, “Agentic AI” still fails on 97% of THE LOW-BAR TASKS.

[https://www.zdnet.com/article/ai-failed-test-on-remote-freelance-jobs/](https://www.zdnet.com/article/ai-failed-test-on-remote-freelance-jobs/)

Again, this is 97% failure rates on the tasks that are thrown at gig economies, where expectations are already firmly cemented at their lowest point, scraping the bottom of the barrel. Most of the meaningful work with high impact on companies never comes close to this level, unless severe and systematic errors in judgement have snowballed into desperation.

Just as jobs and the work that people assign to them may be usefully divided up in this manner, according to pay scale, difficulty, expectations, and trust, so too may the people discussing the AI Domain. I won’t attempt to simplify this into a linear scale, but several categories clearly emerge upon examination:

-   Grounded: Researchers and Engineers who stick to the facts, check sources, and resist the tides of social engineering and exploitation with antifragility. They engage in skepticism paired with the ever-present opportunity, and invitation, to prove them wrong.  
      
    
-   Socialite: Anyone whose perceptions and beliefs overcome the grounding force of demonstrated fact. This group is peppered across society, forming a sort of social glue in many contexts, as common personality archetypes frequently favor some balance where perceptions and beliefs are allowed to uproot facts.  
      
    
-   Rodent (the overly polite variation of the proper term): Anyone who intentionally misleads the perceptions and beliefs of others in the pursuit of personal gains. These individuals often specifically target Socialites for manipulation, and Grounded for discrediting. The net result of this combination often involves using Socialites as cannon fodder against Grounded.  
      
    

There are a variety of Litmus tests which may be applied to check for Rodents or trace back to them. One example is that any group claiming to work or “collaborate” with OpenAI or Anthropic is run by Rodents, as no credible group is going to brag about working with the world’s most obvious frauds.

Another litmus test to check for contaminated Socialites and Rodents is the repetition of debunked tropes, such as the FOMO of “Adopt this technology, or someone else who does will replace you”. This is not to be confused with a competent person telling you precisely how a given tool can and cannot be used, as the difference between that and naïve adoption is night and day.

97% of the lowest bar tasks can’t be automated today, an outcome which was purely predictable over 3 years ago with regard to the technology in question, and of the remaining 3% any pre-trained Magic 8 Ball could probably handle at least 1/3 of the tasks. The bar is that low.
