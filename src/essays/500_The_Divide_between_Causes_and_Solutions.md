“Spotting (and figuring out) what is wrong” is a popular activity, and some are more talented than others in this pursuit, but it is also distinctly different from “Correcting what is wrong”.

To spot and figure out the causes(s) of a given problem is a combinatorial problem, recognizing which factors come together to cause a problem and how they do so. Not all combinatorial problems are created equal, but it is a “hard” class of problems in general, which is one reason why human cognitive biases tend to exert a dominant influence over approaches to them. Cognitive Biases act as an effective, unconscious, and default means of overcoming such combinatorial problems, as they too are combinatorial, as well as cognitively low-cost.

However, “Correcting what is wrong” takes the original combinatorial problem and compounds it against another, producing an explosive increase in the complexity of solving it. While Cognitive Biases often aren’t completely terrible at spotting problems and figuring out their approximate causes, their levels of accuracy take a short dive off of a tall cliff when combinatorial problems compound.

One common method of overcoming this great divide is specialization, as the baseline cognitive capacities of a specialized individual may accomplish the first phase of spotting and figuring out causes without invoking any strong influences from Cognitive Biases. This allows the remaining issue of “Correcting what is wrong” to be addressed without cognitive bias induced flaws from the prior phase compounding upon this second step.

Most of the Internet’s countless “armchair quarterbacks” discuss the first phase of spotting and figuring out causes without the faintest shred of higher cognition factoring into the process. Instead, it is treated as a social form of entertainment activity, which may be applied to virtually any subject that people extract entertainment value from.

This form of recreation in turn creates “popular opinions” and “hot takes” alike, but the problem remains that absent higher cognition the downstream byproducts of this form of social entertainment offer virtually no real value beyond it, except to bad actors seeking to extend it or ride upon it.

Part of correcting problems across all domains is recognizing when and where higher cognition is lacking or entirely absent, often replaced by corporately curated defaults that drones may blindly carry out to satisfy some naïve KPI. Best practices may be necessary, but also not sufficient. If the first phase of spotting problems and figuring out causes lacks the necessary higher cognition, then nothing that follows can realistically solve the problem.

Of course, AI “agents” are demonstrably worse at all things combinatorial, even struggling on some of the most heavily contaminated and cooked benchmarks, many billions of dollars later.
