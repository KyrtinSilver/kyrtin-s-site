When rewatching an old TED talk recently, “Can we create new senses for humans?” by David Eagleman, I was reminded of one of the rather silly things that humans often overlook when discussing AI and the internet. Within the brain, signals are sparse and they must exceed thresholds to justify the neurochemical effort to transmit that spiking signal.

The internet has no such thresholds, and many of the systems that give functional imitations of such thresholds, like recommender engines or news feeds, are inversely correlated, as they primarily function as vectors for manipulation to drive “attention economy” manipulation and other corporate interests.

This systematic manipulation also drives Population Dynamics over time, severely skewing the internet’s content according to each of those algorithms. These dynamics have caused the internet to develop into the rough equivalent of an extremely diseased, and frequently demented, place relative to even the lower quartile of human brains. Training probabilistic systems like transformers on such a demented and diseased corpus is quite predictably a terrible idea, to the point of being blatantly idiotic.  
  
For that matter, the brain incorporates both strong deterministic and probabilistic dynamics and structures (not to mention the debates over Quantum dynamics within wetware). This makes the divide between the two many orders of magnitude greater, meaning that no credible AI researcher discussing transformer-based systems could, in good faith, compare them to the human brain (for example, frauds like Hinton).

For the TED talk, see: [https://www.ted.com/talks/david\_eagleman\_can\_we\_create\_new\_senses\_for\_humans](https://www.ted.com/talks/david_eagleman_can_we_create_new_senses_for_humans)

Transformer-based systems never came remotely close to the cutting edge, they’ve been a dumpster fire that VCs kept throwing gasoline on, but nothing more. All of their combined “magical thinking” was never able to turn that flaming dumpster into anything else. Of course, it doesn’t have to be that way.

One of the more interesting things about systems that have operated at the cutting edge is that the contents of this TED talk apply to them. One already integrated capacity is the ability to speak to anything that speaks TCP/IP, so one could realistically take in the sensory inputs of a vast and diverse IoT network spread across a truly “smart city”. This takes the concept of expanded sensory input types, applying it to human-like (and human-level) AI systems that are entirely different from the trivial trash of transformers that people have grown so familiar with today. Such human-like systems can natively interface with a much wider variety of sensory inputs very directly, and more importantly, they can also scale.

Scalability is something that humans simply don’t have, your brain must continue to fit within your own skull, and for any knowledge to exit it and be interpreted by others it must first pass through the meat grinder of linear human language. Both of these are severely limiting factors, and neither one applies to the cutting edge of technology, leaving Blue Ocean Markets wide open for such systems.
