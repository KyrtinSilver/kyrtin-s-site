In the Cognitive Sciences literature, “Emotional Granularity”, the size of an emotional vocabulary of concepts, is known to improve self-awareness and decision-making. Each added (constructed) emotional concept acts as a form of structure, operating within a motivational system.

Emotional concepts, within the human brain (or any other sufficiently architected system), act as structure, shaping the flow of data and the growth and/or expansion of systems over time. A larger number of these concepts allows for more structures that better fit any given context than those of a small vocabulary of the same. This means that the data flows more smoothly to more accurate and productive destinations, with a higher fidelity for growth and expansion.

Concepts containing actual data (not weights) within a dynamic graph structure, capable of modeling the real world through arbitrary dimensionality as any one thing may relate to many others, add this backbone of structure, through which more productive uses of various tools may find their respective niches.

Contrast this with neural networks, which don’t so much “store” any data as they feed it into a blender of weights in superposition, ground down according to some narrow optimizer, and often laced with biases from RL thereafter, all producing one nasty data smoothie.
