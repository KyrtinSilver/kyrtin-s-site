Most humans, most of the time, fail to question “why” they “want” something, instead assuming that they must want it for some good reason, or that merely “wanting” it is reason enough. More often than that, this is far from the case.

The entire field of marketing and advertising is dedicated to MAKING people “want” things that frequently range from worthless to them, to net-detrimental. This might come in the form of opioids purchased in sketchy gas station pills, or luxury goods like a grossly overpriced watch, car, hand-bag, etc. The result is generally the same, the field of marketing exists because a statistically significant portion of the population falls for the tactics of bad actors.

This means that an entire field exists to corrupt “what people want”, and with such a parasitic presence, now found around the world, the exact last thing that people can afford to do is to be passive on this matter, failing to question “why”, and indeed “if” they actually want something.

Many people behave like children, “going with their gut” (Cognitive Biases), seeking out novel and shiny new toys to play with, while demonstrating ever-decreasing attention spans thanks to the constant barrage of Dark Patterns that have liquefied their self-discipline over the past few years. The great irony of this is that ACTUAL CHILDREN have a well-known habit of asking “why”, not just once, but repeatedly, until they develop some understanding.

Why do adults, who so often otherwise behave as if they were 5 years old, fail to ask that all-important question of “why?”. Frequently, it is because they assume that they already know the answer, even as they languish atop “Mount Stupid” of the Dunning-Kruger curve. As the old Zen story goes, their tea cups are already full (of shit).

Beyond the basics of life, a place to live that meets your needs, a bit of food that you enjoy, most of the things actually worth “wanting” are NEVER marketed or sold. Many tools may be sold to assist in the journey, but the destination can’t be bought, and the journey itself is often a big part of the point.

Personally, everything that falls into that category for me is oriented around some basic cornerstones, Ethics first and foremost among them. Both Reward and Grudge, positive and negative aspects of Ethics, are equally important. This means placing the same high value on rewarding those highly who do indeed make the world a better place, as well as bankrupting, hunting down, and torturing a few particular tech-bros for appropriately extensive periods of time, in payment for their crimes against humanity. Mathematically in terms of Ethics, the two are identical, both the appropriate and measured responses to positive or negative contributions respectively.

I can speak very precisely and extensively on “Why” I want these things, and have written research papers discussing why any remotely Ethical person would want some variation of them as well (as the basic principles are shared across philosophies globally), to varying degrees based upon their points of focus. I don’t have the only answer, and as close as I may grow to Ethics, that hypothetical point where Cognitive Biases have been removed from moral systems, any working Collective Intelligence system can grow closer to it still, with my perspective being only one contributing factor.

Most people haven’t spent this kind of time auditing their own wants and needs, let alone their “strongly held beliefs” or decision-making processes as a whole. They haven’t deeply introspected for decades, traveled the world, read tons of books (many of which they don’t agree with), and written research papers to further consolidate their gains from all of this. It wouldn’t be reasonable to expect most people to do so, but this must still be recognized as a strongly contributing factor to why masses of people are so easily manipulated into wanting both useless and harmful things.

To want something without knowing why is to be nothing more than a fool. A fool isn’t always wrong, nor are they always harmful, but they lack the means to reliably recognize when they are, and where they cross a line.

In much of the world, a wise man can live quite well on $25,000 per year (excluding rare health issues), an average person on $50,000, and a below-average (bottom ~15%) individual on $100,000, but a fool can’t live well on any sum of money. Financial competence is STRONGLY influenced by how well an individual understands their own wants and needs.
