One of the silly assumptions that people are often encouraged to make is that what they consciously perceive, and what they unconsciously perceive, are the same thing. One example demonstrating when this becomes very obviously false involves an extremely low frequency sound, below the typical cut-off for conscious hearing, known to evoke an intense sense of dread in those nearby.

This frequency can be hit with massive pipe organs where the pipe’s length exceeds around 40 feet, but another example of it was also traced back to a faulty fan motor within a more enclosed space. The theory tied to why humans respond with intense dread to this particular pitch associated it with certain large predators that hunted humanity’s ancestors, before the world’s mega-fauna predators were effectively wiped out.

At the higher end of the sound spectrum, string instruments have been shown to evoke more intense reactions from listeners when the actual instruments are being played, even if they aren’t seen, as opposed to recordings of those instruments being played through speakers, since the speakers are typically incapable of reproducing the complete spectrum of sound that the instrument itself produces.

My own prior research in psychoacoustics exploited this difference between conscious and unconscious processing of audio signals within the brain in a different way, multiplying the unconscious focus placed on an audio signal.

The human brain is also much more heavily biased in favor of visual data processing than audio, but the overwhelming majority of this data is still processed unconsciously. The same principles of unconsciously processed sensory data strongly influencing humans remains for visual data, such as magnetic ballast fluorescent lights causing migraines due to flashing at an interval faster than the human eye’s normal conscious perception.

Despite the extreme oversimplification (lie) that many were taught in school growing up, humans have more than just 5 senses, and sensory data may be processed unconsciously, consciously, or alternating between the two. The same principles apply to all of them, including sensory signals from the vagus nerve.

This divide between conscious perception and unconscious responses causes several curious effects in humans. One of these was first demonstrated with pigeons (B. F. Skinner, “Superstition’ in the Pigeon”), where food pellets were rewarded to the pigeons 100% randomly. Despite the rewards being entirely random, this led to each pigeon doing more of whatever it had been doing at the time of the reward, mistaking correlation for causation.

Like the pigeons of that study, humans don’t consciously experience the causes of these responses to unconsciously processed stimuli. Instead, they often latch onto that which they perceive consciously at the time. Some even take this many steps further to gradually build complex belief systems and rituals around the experience of what often amounts to otherwise simple unconsciously processed sensory information.

Much of what has often been pointed to as “Sixth sense” or “extrasensory perception” amounts to no more than this, unconsciously processed sensory data that the conscious mind indirectly perceives through spotting subtle bodily, mental, or emotional changes taking place within them in response to that unconscious process.

A simple and potent example of this is a visceral and instinctual fear response that triggers in lab rats and humans alike when they are placed in an environment where the carbon dioxide level is rising rapidly. They aren’t able to consciously perceive the carbon dioxide level that they inhale, but their subconscious processes go absolutely nuts, evoking a potent and rapid conscious response.

In my own prior research related to audio signals, I discovered that the greatest benefits of some methods applied at the unconscious level also immediately vanish if the stimuli is pushed into conscious perception. This means both that great benefits may be gained through the careful design and optimization of unconsciously processed sensory data, and that those benefits may only exist while it remains unconsciously processed.

However, the difference in processing is generally biological in nature (across the full array of senses), not based on conscious knowledge, so informed consent can always be granted without it impacting the efficacy of any known related methods.
