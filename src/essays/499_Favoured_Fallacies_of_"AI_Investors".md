Popular Fallacies in AI investment:

1.  An investor expects you to produce a business that turns a profit, while also demanding to dig through your IP.  
      
    Any founder incompetent enough to fall for this one couldn’t realistically run a profitable business anyway. Theft and corporate sabotage are the norm today, with numerous opportunities for “plausible deniability” when shielded by the legal departments of major tech companies. (See Nvidia’s piracy of Anna’s Archive or Anthropic’s infamous book burning, both in court documents, for examples of how overt this illegal activity has become.)  
      
    Outside of such illegal activities, such “investors” themselves frequently have the higher cognitive capacities of a small spoiled child, at best, and their “advisors” frequently exhibit lower competence (but far more prolific use of jargon) than the average high school student.  
    
2.  An investor expects you to commercially deploy systems pre-funding that exceed the performance of major tech companies.  
      
    This effectively makes said investor worthless, as their only point of necessary value exists when funding is needed, not post-deployment for any competently designed systems. While the term “profit” might rarely appear for “AI companies” today, that is a testament to the rarity of competence.  
    
3.  An investor expects benchmarks designed for completely different systems and purposes, including those such as SWE-bench Verified, which have already been thoroughly debunked.  
      
    This is like judging a software developer by their cooking skills (wrong benchmarks) as well as their horoscopes (debunked benchmarks). As predictors go, this approach ranks somewhere below Prof. Tetlock’s famous “Dart-Throwing-Chimpanzees” for accuracy.  
    
      
    
4.  An investor cites “averages” for investment amounts and equity...despite 75% of VC-backed startups failing, making the “average” values point to failure.  
      
    In reality, major investors rely on a small number of 10-100x+ returns, meaning that they aim for the long-tail of the probability distribution, where values averaged over the rest of the same distribution are less than worthless. Once more, this mistake offers a red flag signaling that you’re speaking with a “Dart-Throwing-Chimpanzee”.  
    
5.  An investor conflates ROI with “control”.  
    
    10-100x+ returns have no actual connection to an investor buying their way into controlling shares. These are two entirely separate matters.
    

It is never safe to assume that a wealthy individual (or worse, a manager of other peoples’ wealth) is competent. Statistically, that correlation is negative.
