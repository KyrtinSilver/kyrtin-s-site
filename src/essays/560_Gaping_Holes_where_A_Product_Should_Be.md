Many markets have their own massive holes where a product should be, but isn’t. Often times these holes exist explicitly for the purpose of screwing people over, such as selling them a more expensive product, when the same need could be more easily met through a different approach, while also enjoying significant performance gains.

For example, if a PC motherboard was manufactured that utilized two consumer-grade CPUs, as is commonly seen on server motherboards that cater to insanely expensive specialty CPUs, then a large segment of those customers buying flagship CPUs could instead buy a motherboard with two or more consumer-grade CPU sockets. The benchmark test figures paint a stark contrast between the two options:

-   AMD Ryzen 9 9950X3D **Score: 70,167** Price (MSRP): **$679.95\***  
    
-   (Individual CPU) Intel Core Ultra 7 270K Plus **Score: 68,765** Price (MSRP): **$319.99\***  
    
-   (Hypothetical Dual-consumer-CPU) Intel Core Ultra 7 270K Plus **Score: \*~137,530** Price (MSRP): **$640\***
    

\*This naïve doubling of the score is optimistic, but even a strongly pessimistic estimation where 50% losses were predicted would still result in a score of **103,147**, a 47% improvement over the higher priced CPU.

Two is also by no means the limit, as even three or four CPU sockets could potentially fit onto motherboards with an ATX-E or EEB footprint. Two is however the easiest to fit on such motherboards, prior to creating a new class of high-end consumer motherboards that might make use of full-tower and super-tower class cases, like my previous monster of a desktop case (and personal favorite), the Cosmos II.

Taking this approach could reduce, but not remove, the demand for the highest-end flagship model CPUs, as no matter how much performance you can get, some people are still going to aim for the top and attempt to max it out, trading value-per-dollar for peak performance. This actually means that the needs of ~95% of people could be better served with multi-CPU consumer systems, allowing for even more streamlined “Economies of Scale” on that ~95%.

Those seeking the peak of performance have a demonstrably lower price sensitivity, having always traded value in pursuit of the peak, and although they may pay marginally more per CPU (think single digit price increases by percentage), they also gain SIGNIFICANTLY higher peaks to strive for. Instead of a single flagship CPU that they attempt to overclock, they might have 4 flagship CPUs, all liquid cooled and overclocked, doing far more than one ever could.

This means that ~95% of people could get far more performance for a far better value, even assuming a $100-125 price increase for building one more CPU slot on the motherboard, while those seeking peak performance might get a 4-CPU motherboard and fill it up with flagship models. It also means that people in the middle could apply liquid cooling to multiple CPUs, and get higher wattage PSUs, benefiting those markets as well.

This is just one example of a gaping hole in the market where products should exist. The need is very obviously there, and while there are some technical considerations for handling the compute traffic of multiple CPUs versus a single CPU, it is nothing new, and it requires nothing particularly rare or expensive to accomplish. Similarly massive holes exist across numerous industries, just waiting for the first chimpanzee to figure out how to bang those rocks together, creating a significant improvement on the available tools.

As to why many of these holes exist, you can often trace it back to a few greedy idiots that aren’t nearly as good at math or logic as they think they are. For example, this approach could EASILY encroach upon the lower end of performance for workstation CPUs:

-   AMD Ryzen Threadripper PRO 9975WX **Score: 105,017** Price (MSRP): **$3,899.00\***
    

The motherboards for hosting such chips are also generally over $1000. Such systems are typical for the maximized financial incompetence of corporations, where they might spend $25,000+ on a “workstation” build, and they’ll likely remain just as financially incompetent tomorrow as they are today, only slowly changing over a period of years. Just because you could match or exceed the same performance while **spending 5 times less** doesn’t mean that they would.

I also use CPUs as an example here because it is a market without a clearly superior option. AMD and Intel trade off their advantages one to the other, more Cache per CPU and long-term socket compatibility for AMD, better general benchmark performance per dollar spent for Intel.  
  
In contrast, the GPU market is entirely one-sided today, with only a single AMD GPU in the top 20, but if AMD started aiming for multi-CPU consumer motherboards, working with manufacturers, they could do to Intel in a couple of years what Nvidia did to their GPU market share over the past 15 years.
