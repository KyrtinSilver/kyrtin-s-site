Another under-appreciated factor governing “Black Swans” as Taleb puts it, is that increasing levels of complexity also inherently increase the number of such unexpected, rare, and highly potent events. This is because more complex confluences of compounding variables are fundamentally harder to predict and mitigate, and when they go unmitigated, they predictably become that much more potent.

The world has been rapidly increasing in complexity, while the human cognitive capacity hasn’t budged, and narrow AI offers virtually nothing of real assistance in this matter, only the illusions of narrow optimization over some naïve, blind, and often net-negative KPIs.

This is part of why my team specifically focuses on a completely different kind of AI architecture, specifically built to handle hyper-complexity, dynamically, across any and all domains where it may be needed.

The mainstream of “AI” has grown notorious for flooding the internet with Slop, greedily consuming all of the hardware (like RAM) that they can’t actually afford, and offering paper-thin illusions of value that are quickly debunked, like any of the overtly fraudulent and/or easily gamed “benchmarks” that major LLM-providers commonly cite. They reliably fail to handle non-trivial levels of complexity, and even minimally complex combinatorial problems they can barely even appear to compete on while cheating.

In contrast, our technology, built on the Independent Core Observer Model (ICOM) cognitive architecture:

-   Completely avoids “Hallucination”, and offers precisely zero Slop
    
-   Isn’t inherently hardware-greedy, able to scale up and down as-needed
    
-   Demonstrates value in real-world complexity
    
-   Resisted attempts to break or breech it for 2.5 years “in the wild”
    

The difference is fundamental, a night-and-day contrast. We bootstrapped ICOM from a Technology Readiness Level 1 to 7, but bringing it to 9 will require proper funding, mostly just to pay full-time engineering staff. That is what we’re working on now.

Ironically, the past 3 years of industry-scale investments in Slop Generators (or “Sloptimizers” as one AI engineer put it) have also created new use cases for our technology, as they fundamentally can’t clean up the mess, but our technology can. It will be a long process, since a large part of the population evidently never took the phrase “don’t sh\*t where you eat” to heart, but it can be done.

ChatGPT was certainly a “Black Swan” in the sense that most of the industry wasn’t expecting obvious fraud to take center-stage for 3+ years and suck up over a hundred billion USD in funding. What we can predictably expect of the status quo is more complexity, producing both more and more severe “Black Swans”. However, we can also deploy technology to avoid that fate.
