One of the highly counter-intuitive and repeating patterns that I’ve observed over the past decade is that the most multi-talented (polymath) and innovative people also tend to be more humble than the average person. This was the case for my co-founder David J Kelley, who systematically understates his work when speaking with most people, as well as with my friend Cosmo Inclán, who systematically dives deep to discover new fundamental insights that cross the borders of domains.

The two take on different forms, one in large blocks of C# code, and the other often appearing as massive messages, but the two are functionally equivalent in terms of innovation. In Cosmo’s case, he can very much code as well, having built an app based on some of my older Psychoacoustic research for Android, PC, and iPhone, as well as various other projects.

For context, David won Microsoft’s Most Valued Professional (MVP) awards for 8 consecutive years, and 3 Microsoft CEOs gave demos that he prepared. Needless to say, much of the code that Microsoft relies upon is his, he wrote it. In Cosmo’s case, his career has a smaller footprint (as he is also much younger than David), but he is ultimately no less capable.

At the intersection of a passion for solving problems, constantly improving understanding, and the systematization of hyper-focus, there seems to be a sweet spot where innovation is maximized jointly with humility. Each individual also tends to recognize talent in others clearly enough.

This particular pattern struck me today as I read through some post-NDA technical documents, where by all appearances I may have to add another name to that list of multi-talented people. One innovation pattern documented in research and books has been that the “hard problems” of one field are often easily solved by visitors from another field, as seen on “innovation platforms”. This appears to be the case for John Murphy and his work, building what I referred to as the “Grammarly” of consensus validation.

While plenty of people across the AI industry have beat their heads against the wall of “Hallucinations”, a feature of transformer-based architecture, they never seem to produce sound methodologies from those millions and billions in investments. John took a different approach, realizing that consensus could be measured by building out a graph structure and comparing those components in context to any given corpus, giving references to content that confirms or contradicts any given statement.

The result is something not just of value to “GenAI”, but to fact-checking efforts, public policy, consulting, legal process analysis, and the study of humans more broadly. This process may also run on edge devices, being very compute-friendly, with the potential for deeper analysis via API.

John was very humble about all of this, while also realizing the significance of it, and planning for that significance. His work has my attention now, and perhaps it should have yours.
