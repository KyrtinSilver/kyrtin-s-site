Today I’ll use an intentionally comical/absurd and subsequently more memorable example of Supply and Demand (S&D), as the world tends to forget such basic lessons in many contexts. I routinely speak with people on both sides of the S&D spectrum where their romantic pursuits are concerned, both too much attention, and too little, by their respective measures, and I’ve had to point this out to both sides.

Any data point that may either be directly observed or expected via correlation is going to be subject to such principles as S&D, some as matters of preference, others for practicality, and by still others merely seeking rarity and/or novelty.

Consequently, the people that I mentioned above all follow this pattern. One who is a rarity, in low supply locally, receives far more attention, while those who remain in their default and common local environments, often where they were born, receive comparatively little, being in abundant supply.

Much of this stems from cognitive biases, like people failing to consider many variables within their own lives which they merely take for granted as orbiting around some arbitrary default point, such as where they were born. The blindness to and subsequent failure to question such flaws leads them to find some local optima within what is often one of the worst possible environments, where they hold the least value.

The same principle holds true for expertise, matters of appearance, demeanor, interests, and a long list of other factors.

Much as the optimal placement for an ice-cream shop is unlikely to be at the North Pole, most people optimize their lives locally within the worst possible placement for them, where people look, live, and think the most like they do. The world has MANY better options, particularly for objectively terrible countries like the US.

He does seem to be consistently picking fights with the top countries on this list, which may be my favorite new spurious correlation.

All of this doesn’t mean that it is impossible to find a global optima in a place where you are common, but in statistical terms, it will be extremely unlikely.
