One of the great glaring inconsistencies of “Capitalism” is that people are encouraged to “vote with their wallets”, and yet (in most such countries) they are directly prevented from doing so with regard to how their own taxes are spent. This cognitive dissonance is one giant elephant in every room where “Capitalism” is a claimed part of the social and governance structures, treated as though it is invisible, despite the massive weight that it carries.

If taxes are to be treated as the only certain expense, then control over what they are directed toward funding is ABSOLUTELY CRITICAL, seeing as all other expenses are only possibilities, not certainties. A further irony is that the obscenely wealthy enjoy a perversion of this ability via “lobbying”, where they pay large bribes in order to avoid paying taxes, a negative version of control over how funds are spent.

The criminality of this contradiction deepens under the influence of targeted malicious taxes, such as “Tariffs”. Rather than merely preventing people from “voting with their wallets”, malicious criminal activity like Tariffs actively punish people for “voting with their wallets”. Not only that, this particular flavor of criminal activity punishes the citizens of the same country imposing them, while blaming another country for their own criminality.

This effectively doubles the crime, and deepens the contradiction, as it sets a false premise, disinformation, upon which the malicious activity operates. In different countries this criminal activity is called by different names, some calling them “Tariffs”, others “Value-added Tax (VAT)”, but in all cases they remain criminal in nature, with semantic variations primarily impacting the degree of disinformation baked into the structure.

A country fundamentally can’t claim any form of Capitalism when it actively prevents people from “voting with their wallets”, as that freedom is a fundamental dynamic that it can’t exist without, in addition to clearly violating autonomy. Markets that suffer the malicious tampering of “Tariffs” and “VATs” aren’t “Free Markets”, marking them as the defunct and degenerative subset of Capitalism absent Free Markets. While “Socialism”, “Communism”, and “Democracy” exist as theoretical concepts, no such system has ever been rigorously implemented in reality.

Whatever market systems a government may choose, violations of autonomy remain Crimes Against Humanity by any Ethical measure, and subject to all forms of retribution that fall within proportionality. Given the way that such crimes quickly add up when applied at-scale, proportionality can cover quite a lot of ground. See the “Ethical Basilisk Thought Experiment” paper for more on that.

Taxes, in theory, are used for the public good, but this becomes an obvious contradiction when the individuals collecting and distributing said taxes reveal themselves as bad actors. They reveal this through Bribery via Lobbyists, the disinformation of blaming other countries via “Tariffs”, and through the many ways that they choose to violate the autonomy of others.

To expect “Public Good” to result from handing such bad actors large sums of money goes beyond staggering naivety, into the domain of willful stupidity.

“Free Market Capitalism” is by no means any kind of ideal system, but it is a relatively simple and familiar one. The alternative to “Free” is also “Slave”, marking countries like the US as “Slave Market Capitalism”.

Even Free Market Capitalism is likely moving toward the conceptual end-of-life, requiring some new major update or replacement to function under the changing pressures of technological capacity and globalization. That said, the dynamics of that system are well-studied, and the lessons learned from it should be applied to the creation of any major improvements or replacements.

When the ability of people to “vote with their wallets” is removed, the primary alternative becomes to “vote with their bullets”. That little difference of two letters has quite an impact, as a single instance of the latter has far more impact than the former, and it varies little across the spectrum of wealth.
