With so-called “agents” grabbing up all domain and company names tied to mythology, common descriptive terminology, and value-signalling, almost purely with the intent to “re-sell” them, humanity is already well into the next incarnation of Ye Ole “Dot-Com Bubble”. In effect, the name space is massively saturated, and dominated almost exclusively by “Scalpers”, one of the most malevolent and consequently lowest forms of life on the planet.

Scalpers are a type of bad actor who buys up some limited (or unique) commodity, exclusively for the purpose of leveraging those limited (or unique) properties to massively inflate the price tag. This has been seen for many years across all domains, from live concert tickets to Nvidia GPUs, where some evil little human-shaped parasite buys up a huge quantity of some limited resource, to the direct detriment of everyone else.

\*Note, a similar name-space dynamic was also observed in years previous with the flood of nonsensical company names on markets like Amazon, most of whose products were obvious copies of 10 other companies with equally nonsensical names. The problem has only accelerated since, hitting more domains increasingly hard.

There are also many second and third-order knock-on effects from this sort of activity, beyond directly damaging the price, supply chains, and general availability. In examples like the flood of Amazon copycat companies, the knock-on effects include “Choice Overload” related damages, where many low-quality duplicates flood out the space, requiring additional time and work for all non-malicious parties to filter out the added noise.

The increased uncertainty for all impacted factors exacts a cost, and often a very high one. Companies that release new products in quantities too limited to overcome the problem also suffer long-term reputational damage, even as they attempt to create anti-scalper measures in the purchasing process.

Of course, the overwhelming majority of such measures today fail, so it often just further negatively impacts those attempting to make the ordinary purchases in practice. However, this is usually due to a lack of methodological competence in the design of countermeasures. For example:

-   Bad Actor A uses bots and/or gig workers to order 1,000 of some limited quantity product at release, with the intent to “re-sell” them at a 100%+ markup promptly after the supplier runs out of stock.  
    
-   These products won’t be delivered to 1,000 different locations, the delivery locations will form one kind of pattern or another, making them extremely obvious to any decent anomaly detection system.  
    
-   Although this pattern will only appear after many such orders have been aggregated, for such new product launches that sell out in minutes or even seconds, a delay in order processing completion of the same value can aggregate the orders, detect anomalies, and rapidly escalate a tiered series of countermeasures against such scalpers.  
    
-   While a wide variety of escalating countermeasures may be applied in sequence, a subset of those countermeasures may extend beyond a single company, such as publicly marking an individual as a Scalper.  
    
-   Much as pedophiles are required to be tracked in a public registry in some countries, any remotely ethical company can’t BOTH detect such scalpers AND fail to alert others to such bad actors once they’ve been spotted. This can require any prudent steps to confirm the activity, but both the failure to confirm it once detected, and the failure to disclose it once confirmed, mark any offending company as an accomplice to the crimes in question.
    

Likewise, variations upon the same process could easily be applied to the Scalpers hoarding various web addresses and company names. While some companies exist explicitly to anonymize the owners of web addresses, those shielding companies become accomplices to the crime in this case, placing a target on their back and giving ample reason to breach their databases. Fortunately, when the trail leads directly back to a shield, that shield may be broken, and the bad actors behind it exposed.  
  
Regardless of the limited (or unique) goods being scalped, the malicious intent remains the same, which the punishment should reflect.
