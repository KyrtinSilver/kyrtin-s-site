The IBM 2025 “Cost of Data Breach” report is one of the rare corporate and consulting domain documents that I can offer some praise toward (and I have no affiliation with them). While statistics are often used to lie, this document provides a few that are more helpful, and some that raise eyebrows in ways that IBM doesn’t stand to benefit from. Let’s look at some of the highlights:

-   Global average cost of a Data Breach $4.4 million USD (US companies hit a new record average of $10.22m)
    
-   Average Days it took to identify and contain a breach across various environments: 276 days
    
-   76% of organizations said that their recovery time after a breach was longer than 100 days, and 65% of those surveyed said that they were still not fully recovered from a breach.
    
-   97% of organizations reported AI-related security incidents
    
-   86% of businesses breached experienced an operational disruption of their services
    
-   45% of organizations raised their prices post-breach, passing those costs on to their customers, with 30% raising their prices by more than 15%. (See above)
    
-   37% of organizations are paying ransomware attackers, and fewer are reporting those attacks to the authorities, down from 53% last year to 40% this year.
    
-   At least 69% of AI security incidents involved unsanctioned tools being used (Such as Samsung employees infamously feeding their confidential documents to ChatGPT back in 2023)
    

Link to the document: [https://www.ibm.com/downloads/documents/us-en/131cf87b20b31c91](https://www.ibm.com/downloads/documents/us-en/131cf87b20b31c91)

Cybercrime is the third-largest market in the world, standing at over $10 trillion annually, meaning that if it were a country it would have a global GDP just behind the US and China, and more than double that of the 3rd highest country by GDP, Germany. No industry has benefited more from “GenAI” than Cybercrime, and these statistics help to give us a more detailed picture of how that is impacting companies, and how those companies react.

As “agents” are fundamentally worse in terms of Cybersecurity vulnerability, a topic discussed by a variety of noteworthy white-hat hackers who routinely break them, these numbers may be expected to get measurably worse as more “agents” are deployed and linked to other systems.

Applying Cybersecurity best practices to any new technology is a hard requirement, and vulnerable-by-design technology like LLMs and their “agent” derivatives require far more diligence in this. The costs of failure are both high, and obvious. The consequences for companies, if anything, should only grow more severe over time, from regulation, public backlash, increasing attacks, and subsequent financial ruin.

I won't be endorsing any of IBM's services, of course, but until viable technology is properly funded, cybersecurity options will remain limited.
