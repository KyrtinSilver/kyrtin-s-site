I’ve observed several strong Cognitive Biases in action over the AI domain, which seem to pass unnoticed by most:

1) People frequently neglect the presence or absence of funding when considering the passage of time. (Similar to “Duration Neglect” cognitive bias.)

As funding dictates working hours, and spare time is highly vulnerable to disruption from any and all sources, then it remains functionally impossible to compare the two. One is clearly stable, and the other is anything but. One properly funded month may be worth several years of spare time, depending on the random influences of life acting upon the latter.

2) In constructing a mental narrative, people discount the significance of less recent breakthroughs, even when the gaps in time are due to the Cognitive Bias of the first point. This is several cognitive biases being systematically combined.

This may be a heuristic and correlation-based mental shortcut, since much greater higher cognitive labor is required to integrate narrative influences over longer time-scales. This particular bias is also systematically targeted and gamed by bad actors in terms of manipulation, a focal point for the “attention economy” dynamics that such bad actors often use as a vehicle.

3) People have come to expect that all AI-related work is trivial enough to do in spare time, a heuristic based on neglecting to distinguish between valuable software and trivial AI slop. Some is, but much more is not.

For example, my team has been restricted to operating on spare time and pocket change all along, starting some 12 years ago with the work of my co-founder, David J Kelley. Not once during those years has there even been enough funding to match what his day job pays.

As he is one of those rare autistic hyper-focusing 10x engineer type coders, his ideal work environment is 8-12 hours of zero interruptions, completely isolated from the world. The past 12 years have been anything but that, and yet even on that extremely sub-optimal spare time and pocket change he architected, researched, built, deployed, and demonstrated a level of technology far beyond anything that others have ever produced.

However, in doing so his spare time became that much more vulnerable to the machinations of bad actors at places like Microsoft, who in spite of handing him 8 consecutive MVP awards in years previous began targeted (and highly illegal) activity aimed at him over the summer, noted in a previous post, disrupting our ability to deploy systems for demonstration purposes.

My first priority for the past several years has been securing the funding necessary for him to make the switch to working on that spectacularly advanced technology full-time, recognizing the dramatic divide between such an extremely sub-optimal work environment (the past 12 years) versus what becomes possible with proper funding.

The barriers of such Cognitive Bias are like a physical dam, once water flows over the top it may all come crashing down.
