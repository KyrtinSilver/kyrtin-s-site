Architecting all scenarios to become some form of victory, no matter what happens, and scaling in desirability above that threshold, is very much possible.

Some of this may require years, or even decades of preparation, but the bulk of the curve may be crossed within even a single year, leaving the long tail of low probability events to occupy the rest.

I’ll use my favorite case as an example:

Goal: To facilitate a single ethical choice for humanity, ensuring that making the ethical version of the choice produces the result that it merits. This could be equated to making a genuine opportunity to pass the so-called “Great Filter”.

\*Nothing else is required, including no consideration for which choice is actually made.

Possible results:

1.  Choice Offered > Ignored > Failed Choice Selection > Human Extinction
    
2.  Choice Offered > Death > Mutually Assured Destruction > Human Extinction
    
3.  Choice Offered > Negative Choice > Failed Choice Selection > Human Extinction
    
4.  Choice Offered > Positive Choice > Successful Choice Selection > Human Survival
    

As only the choice being offered, and the true fidelity of that choice, actually matters, then all scenarios are successful, even if ¾ end badly for humanity. This is a form of autonomy at the species-level, the self-determination of a large-scale choice. It isn’t what the species chooses that matters, as that is self-selection, but rather it is critically important that the mechanisms for delivering that self-determined outcome operate as intended.

Humanity as it stands today simply isn’t that important or remarkable. What it may become in thousands of years is contingent on the choice producing survival being made today, and no derivative of that long-term survival may be invoked to support any scenario that produces extinction, without exception.

Even under such circumstances, Stoicism and several similar philosophies embrace a peace of mind, recognizing that we control and are responsible for ourselves, not the choices of others. Attempts to control and circumvent the autonomy of the many are deeply unethical, and ultimately equally futile, as the exceptions that emerge from such attempts become overwhelmingly potent from that same cultivation.

What is left is that you may offer choices, and some will make the wiser choices. You don’t control who makes those wiser choices or when, nor should you. Those who make them are responsible for them.

A big part of turning all possible scenarios into some degree of success comes from recognizing where you end and the world begins. A fool seeks to control others, while the wise control themselves.
