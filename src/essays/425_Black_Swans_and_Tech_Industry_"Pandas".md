As I began my annual re-listening to the audiobooks of Nassim Nicholas Taleb, with “The Black Swan” (2nd edition), his opening point of them being a matter of perspective struck me, not for what he said, but for what he didn’t say. As someone who works in and studies Collective Intelligence, it quickly appeared that the point needs to be made:

Since Black Swans are tied to perspective, your team, organization, or government is only able to see the fraction of rare events coming that are visible to one or more perspectives that you keep at the table. You may have many diverse perspectives, but fail to routinely listen to them, taking account of the risks and opportunities that each may see.

If the perspectives making your decisions are stacked with similarities, then you’ll be blind to both Negative Black Swans (major risks, such as systematic reasons for technological failures) and Positive Black Swans (major opportunities, such as Blue Ocean Markets).

It is reasonably well-known that Collective Intelligence produces more intelligent results from a group of people by effectively reducing the cognitive biases of that group in the results, but Collective Intelligence is the unsung hero of another massive benefit, a greatly enhanced ability to recognize events that others perceive as “Black Swans”.

It was discovered that “Innovation Platforms” where problems are offered as competitions for solving (often manufacturing and chemistry-based) problems frequently have the best solutions come from people in unrelated and/or unexpected fields.

This is because so-called “Hard Problems” are similar to Black Swans, they often only remain “Hard” from the particular perspective noting them as such. Those within a field easily solve most problems, call them “Easy” or “Normal”, but they are systematically biased in such ways that render some subset of remaining problems “Hard” to them. These “Hard” problems are merely an artifact of Population Dynamics, where they are hard to the subset who solved the rest of the problems, but that subset isn’t equipped to handle them.

Black Swans are themselves a kind of problem, with success being to recognize them in advance, rendering them opportunities and risks (no longer “Black Swans”), and failure to recognize them in advance predictably leading teams, companies, and governments to slam head-first into those brick walls at whatever speed they may be traveling.

Part of this stems from bad actors, but the part dictating the vulnerability or antifragility of potential suckers is, as described above, the diversity of perspectives that they keep at the decision-making table.

When people finally see what cutting-edge AI actually looks like, many are likely to call it a “Black Swan”, to which I’ll reply “Not to any technical people in the industry who haven’t kept their head planted firmly up their own \*ss for the past 6 years or so”.

Black Swans, positive and negative, are perspective. Perspective is what you need more of to recognize them.
