Game Theory and Behavioral Economics share a love of gambling games, to demonstrate rationality and (occasionally bounded) irrationality, respectively. Let’s play one. (\*Feel free to test this yourself with LLMArena’s bank of AI models to drive the point home.)

Say that you have 3 options:

Option A: You have a 95% chance to turn $25 into $250 next year, or $2,500 the year after that. Annual dividends are paid in addition to principle gains. There is a 5% total chance to lose it all for the first two years.

Option B: You have a 50% chance to turn $250 into $500 next year. You may also gain $1,000 the year after that, but only if you and one of your peers each invest another $250. There is a 50% chance to lose it all each year.

Option C: You may invest in either A or B next year, for the same annually recurring risk and reward.

Let us say that these three options also come with additional context:

Option A: Offers unique insights into the future value of other investments, strongly influencing them through technology.

Option B: Offers a well-known brand name, but no substantive benefits beyond such appearances.

Option C: Allows individuals to wait until their respective comfort levels have been satisfied.

Which option(s) would you choose?

Game Theory gives us one obvious answer, but actual human investors and markets have been giving the opposite answer for the past 3 years, and doing so 100% of the time.

One option gives both higher certainty, 95% for two years vs 50% per year, and higher returns, 10x per annum vs 2x (requiring more investment each time), as well as additional unique value that can easily be turned into even greater monetary gains, and the dividends given by Option A.

The difference is that if bad actors exploit cognitive biases with sufficient skill and scale, they can skew the perceptions of value so greatly as to blind all those whom they indoctrinate to any alternative which they themselves don’t mention.

No one can argue that OpenAI isn’t an exceptional manipulator, the data makes that quite clear. However, not one of their investors can claim to be rational.
