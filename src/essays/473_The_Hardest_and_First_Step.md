One of the hardest things for many people to do is simply to ask for help, and this is doubly true when that ask is sent out into the void. I imagine this is probably a big part of the emotional appeal that people attach to the concept of AI “agents”, the thought of being able to accomplish a wider variety of things themselves while still effectively remaining secluded from the world and its many uncertainties, except for whatever windows into it people choose to crack open.

I’ve observed this problem in my family, in myself, and in many whom I’ve met and been friends with over the years. It can feel like a tall and foreboding barrier stands between the need for help and the asking, promising pain in the attempt. This is emotional, not strictly rational, but even the awareness of it doesn’t make this feeling go away. Adding people to any project means introducing new uncertainties and dimensions, which supports this aversion.

This problem is also compounded when shame and other irrational forms of conditioning become entangled with the subject matter. It can further compound where corporate sabotage, or other rational factors, become risks. There are many negative emotions and dozens of cognitive biases directly supported and governed by them, which may come into play in countless combinations and contexts.

For virtually any project worth doing, it takes more than one person. Statistically, the more worthwhile, the more people with different skills and specialties are required for each to focus on their niche for any effective and efficient delivery.

One of my core niches lands at the intersection of software architecture, research, and data science, but I know full well that I’m not a database architecture expert, deployment and integrations expert, or a full-blown mathematician. Even if I did opt to multi-task outside of my niches, it would be a hard and slow slog.

Whatever mixed drugs and Kool-Aid many in AI might be on, “agents” can’t reliably fill those roles either. An expert in their niche may benefit from using such tools, in their niche, but “agents” are no substitute for the expertise that one lacks.

I do see some potential for building a small team project in AI to exceed the performance of currently deployed commercial systems, without infringing on my startup’s ICOM cognitive architecture IP, but I’m also quite sure that I’d need at least two other specialized teammates to put such a project together. I know a few people who might volunteer to fill such roles, one of whom even likes one of the activities that I hate most, setting up testing and deployment environments. It doesn’t make asking any easier.

The project idea is fairly simple, demonstrating a more advanced form of motivational system than the RL and SGD. The concept is compatible with “agents”, but still a far cry from the full ICOM-based technology necessary for any non-trivial definition of “AGI” to be met.
