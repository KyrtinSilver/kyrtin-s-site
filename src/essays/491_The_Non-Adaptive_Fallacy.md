The Non-Adaptive Fallacy is a commonly invoked assumption that linear expectations may be applied to a dynamic system, particularly those populated by entities who adapt both independently and collectively, or any time that “popularity” is involved.

You can find examples of this quickly and easily in most fields, when you look for it, such as applying linear expectations to any system where humans, or to a lesser degree many other forms of life in general, play a significant role. Life exists today because it adapts, constantly and dynamically.

The more dynamic elements are present within a given system, the greater the fundamental potential for adaptation, and the more environmental options are available, the better fit the results may become.

This is further compounded by collective actions, where each individual entity within a collective may exert influence over specific aspects of an environment, specializing themselves as part of a larger whole, much as mitochondria did within the Eukaryotic cell some ~1.5 billion years ago. Since that time, the predictable pattern of increasing complexity being paired with increasing levels of cooperation has emerged time and time again.

Lastly, “popularity” is a far more chaotic form of adaptation, highly error-prone (and vulnerable to bad actors), but also highly potent, transient, and collectively activated.

The presence of any one of these factors throws off linear expectations with almost clockwork regularity, and the presence of all of them tends to utterly shred such expectations, excluding limited windows where “popularity” may suffer systematic abuses to carve out some limited and transient space of predictability.

We see this play out in politics, financial markets, and other global systems every day. It is one of the reasons why many pundits and forecasters are infamous for demonstrating the accuracy of “Dart-Throwing-Chimpanzees” as Prof. Tetlock once put it. (see also “Inverse Cramer”)

A big part of the problem is underestimating (or outright failing to recognize) the flexibility of dynamic systems (relative to the target point) as one of several layers of interacting uncertainty. Within these layers of uncertainty, you also have both benign and bad actors, meaning that some will intentionally seek to throw the system off target, and they frequently can. Some of those bad actors also abuse the mechanics of “popularity” to greatly increase the damage caused and subsequent gains that they may reap.

Humans are incredibly bad at tasks where degrees of separation are involved, rapidly branching out into second and third-order effects, the breadth of which often increases by orders of magnitude, safely exceeding human cognitive bandwidth. When that happens, the Complexity versus Cognitive Bias trade-off is invoked, and the systematic nature of those cognitive biases carves a subset of predictable paths for further errors and abuses.

Consequently, many bad actors also overcomplicate problems specifically so that they may better invoke these cognitive biases. Life adapts, parasites included.

\*For another example of this familiar to scientists, ask yourself “When was the last time that \[you\] read every paper cited in the references of a new research paper?” (1 full degree of separation)
