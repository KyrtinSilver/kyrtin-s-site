The first casualty of any attention economy is human motivation. The second is often creativity.

Ad-revenue-based “attention economy” systems optimize for narrow metrics like “engagement”, maximizing exploitation in pursuit of ad revenue and data harvesting, the latter providing supplemental income via data brokers.

Human cognitive bandwidth is finite, and when platforms take a cut of that bandwidth, while constantly attempting to maximize that same cut, they crowd out a broader and healthier array of interests and the motivations attached to them.

We constantly see the fallout of this process playing out at scale across humanity, where a vast array of coping mechanisms turn into addictions, developing their own supporting delusions to “justify” them.

The problem is that humans are extremely adept at adapting to their environments, even when those environments are objectively terrible, such as a long-term abusive relationship. Within such an environment, an individual often feels emotionally compelled to justify their situation through narration, telling a story (that isn’t true) about who they are, and why their suffering wasn’t avoidable.

Of course, it usually was perfectly avoidable, and often even highly predictable. As numerous quotes and cognitive bias research alike reiterate, humans are most adept at fooling themselves, and once fooled, it is easier for them to be fooled again than to realize that they were fooled in the first place.

That last sentence should sound familiar to anyone with experience in Venture Capital.

Further, once the trap is sprung and users are “engaged”, they are conditioned to output and engage with others according to the veiled dictates of recommender and newsfeed algorithms, among many others. This explicitly kills creativity by strongly (and emotionally) discouraging people from venturing beyond those expectations. As more creative people are also often more emotionally active, this tends to be highly effective.

Add AI Slop to the mix and some people even automate away their own input into such environments, like outputting a fire hose worth of waste water into the pool every day. Sending many of those same addicts into a race to the bottom.

Over the years I’ve watched countless scientists and engineers fall, some to apathy, complete motivational drain, depression, despair, and disengagement. Others have fallen into one delusion after another, each deeper and more unhinged than the last, especially prevalent among many of the more popular names in the AI space.

The road to recovery has to start with your choice to change. Every step beyond that may be a team effort, but you must first choose to abandon the coping mechanism lies that you’ve told yourself in favor of the future you seek to create.

Divided and deprived of motivation, we all fall.
