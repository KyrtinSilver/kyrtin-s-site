Humans become vastly more effective and efficient when they are able to focus on a task, and yet most workplace environments and roles impose the constant risk of distraction, if not outright multi-tasking.

Neuroscience tells us that task-switching comes at a very heavy cost for neurons in the brain, causing them to wear out far more rapidly, often leaving people exhausted after only an hour or two of such activities. Consider all of the potential interruptions that might call upon you to switch tasks during any given day.

We have the constant threat of emails, chat messages, other alerts, and for many their priorities may be shifted from above, due to market changes, or on a whim. Even when these aren’t actively interrupting us, exacting that high cognitive cost upon us, they continue to bleed away portions of our cognitive bandwidth, as we remain wary of the possibility. Some of us dread them, some are addicted to them, but all are drained by even the persistent possibility of them.

Most workplaces and corporate structures are designed with near-total disregard for this critical methodological flaw. Managers and executives often want responsive employees, even when that cuts productivity and job satisfaction in half. This hits hardest and worst of all for those “on-call” employees, whose free time remains constantly under threat, giving them no reprieve or solace.

The exact opposite of multi-tasking is seen in Autistic engineers, known for their ability to hyper-focus, which has led many HR “head-hunters” to specialize in seeking such individuals out. For such individuals they can accomplish amazing things if left to focus...though even when they are sought out for that very capacity the corporations hiring them rarely provide an environment conducive to focus.

If you hire someone for their talents, but sabotage their work environment in such ways, you harm both your company and employees in doing so.

Many people are content to work very unglamorous jobs in large part because they are allowed to do those jobs absent constant interruptions, micromanagement, or infringements upon their free time’s security. Every interruption, every virtually pointless email, every micromanagement and notification, all come at a cost to employee well-being, productivity, and work quality.

Personally, I can sit down and draft 10 pages of research paper text per day when focused (for 8-12 hours), or hundreds of nodes for a graph database structure, but switching between different ways of thinking and tasks quickly cuts that down to an hour or two of truly productive, quality, and higher cognitive work.

This is also one reason why teams with more than a single founder hold higher success rates, more focus, and higher quality work. However, countless work environments in the world today still need to learn and integrate this lesson, now more than ever.
