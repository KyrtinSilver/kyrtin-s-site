The question that should precede any discussion is if that discussion is likely worth the effort to move through it to some equitable conclusion, to avoid “Base-rate Neglect” (Cognitive Bias). For some topics, any equitable exchange of time and effort is unlikely to reach such a conclusion, particularly within the context of groups and/or cultures conditioned toward one extreme (or absolute) belief or another.

A related question is what your time and effort are worth, both in general and at the present moment. For some people they quantify this by the hour, like hourly pay rates, though a more accurate figure would be cognitive hours, at least for any discussion that requires a few brain cells firing in higher cognition.

You can work a full 8 hours and only engage your brain in higher cognition for half of that time, or even less depending on the job, the person, and the circumstances. A serious discussion is going to place demands on that higher cognition, because if a discussion is worth having, it is worth actually thinking through. Instead of any quality of time, we’re now talking about a conditional and much more finite quantity, in competition with the rest of life’s demands.

The specific value of high quality (conditional) time may be broken down further. In one drafted paper from many years ago I used the example:

{2.5} over {24} × {5} over {7} × 10%=0.744% \\( Maximum Peak\\)

In this example, you have a human with 2.5 hours of optimal performance per day, working 5 days per week, and in their optimal environment for that work 10% of the time, all coming together to produce less than 0.75% of peak optimality.

That peak optimality time is a good example of a precious resource, one of the highest value chunks of time, far more so than the rest of any given day. It also typically follows predictable circadian rhythms and habits, like the hours after waking up and caffeine for “morning people”.

A discussion which requires that peak optimality time to reach a desirable conclusion, but that doesn’t receive it, isn’t worth having. However, if that peak time is dedicated to a book that millions of people read on the subject, then it may well reach that worthwhile conclusion at scale, hitting both those who do and do not dedicate their own higher cognition to it.

It is also critical to note that it isn’t a one-sided or single-entity process. If one side dedicates higher cognition, but the other doesn’t, then you have a parody of the Prisoner’s Dilemma playing out, where one side sabotages the other.

Discussions where both sides engage, with higher cognition and mindful intention, can cultivate collective intelligence. Discussions where one or both parties fail to do so are merely wasting the time of both parties, while pre-souring future discussions.
