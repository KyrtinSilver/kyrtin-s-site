Many, if not most problems in the world today are combinatorial problems that have been grossly oversimplified, and subsequently failed to meet any reasonable or effective solution. Most tend to just be accepted as though they were unavoidable, or overlooked entirely.

This is frequently tied to the absence of an appropriate data structure, usually a form of graph structure. To continue the discussion of my last post, take the example of a game world environment. There are several different approaches to the task of creating such a thing, like a spectrum from fully hand-engineered level design to fully generative approaches. However, at the software architecture level the generative approaches tend to be a mashup of simple variables, highly predictable, and anathema to any sense of immersion.

This makes the typical spectrum from hand-engineered design to generative no more than an illusion, as the generative approaches are inherently feeble, fragile, predictable, and of little overall value (relative to what is overlooked). In contrast to this false value trade-off, a graph structured system can add cumulative value on top of any hand-engineered efforts, rather than zero-sum attempts to replace them with hot garbage like Nvidia’s new “Sloptracing” (DLSS 5).

This cumulative value was demonstrated with the previous ICOM-based research system, where a seed of data (largely hand-engineered efforts) serves as the starting point for the growth of a dynamic graph-native structured system. That system started at less than 1 GB in size, and grew dynamically to over 1.6 terabytes, developing new and even unexpected capacities in the process.

In the same way, when hand-engineering efforts of human origin are applied to create such seeds of graph structured data, they serve as the starting point for growth within arbitrary dimensionality and context. In a game, this can serve such a function for every single ability tree, NPC AI, mob AI, environmental controller, crafting system, loot system, progression system, and generative environment spawner, within the context of games alone.

Taken in a broader context, this could just as easily be applied to create entire legal, financial, trade, logistics, and governance systems. Each could be built in a vastly more coherent, interoperable, and architecturally sound manner than the “bureaucra-slop” layers of incoherent political and corporate ramblings and meanderings found in a majority of such systems today. Once again, the poor structures and methods were applied, often over very long periods of time, to create systems that only marginally work, if they can be said to “work” at all.

The higher the complexity, the more likely this problem is to occur, and the more severely it is likely to be when it does. All of those topics that people get excited about the “future potential” of, like “Quantum” things, “Web3”, Industry 4.0, robotics, metamaterials, and even the politics of most countries safely fall into the “extremely high complexity” category.

If you aren’t satisfied with sh\*\* results, driven primarily by cognitive bias or slopomation, start with the right data structure.

\*\*Note, even the term “graph” has come to see heavy abuse over the last few years, with people referring to things as graphs that clearly aren’t, especially in AI. Be sure that you understand what people mean when they use this term, ask questions.  
  
For example, if you ask someone using the term “graph” if each node contains a weight, a string, or both, and they respond with anything other than “it contains whatever it damn well needs to, it is a graph”, then pushing them off of the nearest roof top is a perfectly acceptable follow up.
