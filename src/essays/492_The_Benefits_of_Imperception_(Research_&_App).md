Some of my prior Neuroscience research has just re-entered the public domain in a new form, via an app in the Apple App Store: [https://apps.apple.com/app/neurima/id6758975201](https://apps.apple.com/app/neurima/id6758975201)

The method was simple, overlooked for the better part of two centuries, and discovered back in 2015. Since then I pioneered some research into it, released my findings, and used it myself for more than the past decade.

A good friend of mine built a prototype app to automate the process, and he recently got around to rebuilding a more advanced version (still free) for release to Apple’s App Store (Android next). No accounts, no advertising, no data harvesting, no subscriptions or in-app purchases.

I originally pursued that research as a method of stress reduction, enhancing meditation, and reducing migraine frequency. Many additional benefits were revealed in the course of the research, with some of the more advanced and potent forms of it requiring specialized hardware, but this app is built to cover the portion that software is sufficient to deliver.

My friend behind the app, being very familiar with all of it, broke down the story in short form here: [https://tryneurima.com/story/](https://tryneurima.com/story/)

Even more briefly, the basic idea is tiny audio sample level deviations in timing, as opposed to the frequency deviations found in “Binaural Beats”. The former is processed entirely at the subconscious level, whereas the latter produces consciously audible (and quite irritating) noise. Keeping the entire process subconscious allows a person to “feel” the difference, rather than hearing noise.

I went on to Cognitive Bias and Cognitive Architecture research after that, but I’ve kept using it (and benefiting from it) for all of these years. ~90% less migraine frequency, more persistent benefits from meditation, more focus, less stress, and no doubt much the same neurogenesis benefits that were associated with binaural beats.

One day we might get around to exploring more of the advanced versions of this, like the testing that I did that induced synesthesia using bone conduction, traditional headphones, and TDCS over both auditory cortices, all combined. The potential research direction related to bio-photon research remains far more advanced still.

The same principles could also be applied to visual hardware, provided that the person designing it actually understood how the brain processes such signals. It is that gap in understanding that caused such an obvious subconscious processing function to be overlooked for such a long time that slavery was still popular in the US during the time of the initial discovery by Heinrich Wilhelm Dove.

There is still low-hanging fruit in scientific fields, you just have to know where to look. If anyone working at Spotify, Apple Music, or a similar streaming audio service sees this, consider reaching out to my colleague who built the app.

Try it for yourself.
