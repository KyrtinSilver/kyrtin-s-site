The Behavioral Economics and Game Theoretic literature frequently argue, but one thing that the years of research have made abundantly clear is that humans will gladly and predictably learn to cooperate when the advantages to doing so are made clear, when guarantees are made, and when punishment frequently follows “free riders”.

However, each of these factors frequently fails to hold true under real-world conditions. Most of the time, people have little or no idea what benefits they may gain from cooperating with those around them (from both uncertainty and perceptual blindness), and it takes a lot of hard cognitive effort to even put together a reasonable collection of guesses, let alone rationally evaluate what insights those guesses may offer.

Likewise, guarantees frequently aren’t fully honored, if they are honored at all, and white-collar criminals engaging in dishonest ventures rarely face any punishment capable of preventing them from repeating the same. Often times, even settlements to cover the small fraction who sue over these violations are simply billed as a “cost of doing business”.

Where punishments are too weak, unreliable, costly to the victims, or even completely absent, “free riders” (human-shaped parasites, or “jerks” as Thaler puts it) are cultivated, as they freely iterate, refining one form of criminal activity after another. The research has also shown that the presence of even one such unchecked parasite within a group reliably destroys any desire for cooperation therein.

Humans only reliably cooperate to the degree that they understand the benefits of doing so, with assurances made and parasitic activity harshly punished.

Most corporations, governments, and even individuals within small groups today stand to benefit orders of magnitude more from cooperation than they yet realize. This is largely because calculating those benefits quickly runs into the territory of hyper-complexity, where it is unreasonable for even groups applying effortful higher cognition to fully solve them.

For ~1.5 billion years of evolutionary history, since the first mitochondria joined the Eukaryotic cell, the pattern of increasing complexity being paired with increasing cooperation has repeated. That pattern shows no signs of stopping any time soon.

That is what my team builds for, systems to handle hyper-complexity, and it is where we’ve stood alone at the cutting edge for 6 years and counting.

For more on the larger body of research mentioned, I recommend reading the revised edition of “The Winner’s Curse”, by Richard Thaler and Alex Imas.
