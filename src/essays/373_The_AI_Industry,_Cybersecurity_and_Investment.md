The AI industry from a Cybersecurity perspective:

Every LLM and LLM-derivative “agentic” system on the market is still the same laughingstock that they have been for years, which no amount of “guardrailing”(fraudrailing) can ever solve (see Wittgenstein for why). While many things may be summarized in meme format, in Cybersecurity reality lives up to the meme. There can be no net-improvement on the current trajectory, even with little improvements being made, as major detriments are landing new damage just as routinely as those little improvements.

At this point, AI-integrated Cybersecurity has become so bad that you could genuinely turn it into an Angry Birds (of AI Cybersecurity) game, linking each game mechanic to a cyberattack method or vector, and seeing what happens when people play it.

The blocks to be knocked over are the different parts of a system’s attack surface, the different birds that may be hurled at it different methods of attack, and by combining the human player with an advanced attack-model on the back-end, executing those cyberattacks, you could gamify Cybercrime (or red-teaming) much as “Foldit” did with protein folding.

As Cybercrime is the third-largest market in the world (not even counting examples like the one described below), and given the high volume of such games made today, that might not be such a far-fetched scenario as it may sound.

The AI Industry from the Investment perspective:

OpenAI gave a non-binding agreement to a future deal that they had no way of funding, for hardware that the other party didn’t have, resulting in a treasure trove of die-hard morons boosting Oracle’s valuation by hundreds of billions, turning the money from those suckers into an investment to buy the hardware, and an investment in OpenAI that would pay for it.

Three companies suckered the market into a circular investment bubble, about the shortest and most obviously fraudulent scheme possible from an accounting perspective. The first two times I read about it, I thought that someone was giving a hypothetical Idiocracy-worthy scenario, but only as it continued did I realize that it wasn’t a joke, or rather, that the joke was real. Personally, I keep very low expectations for humanity, based on experience, but that was a new low.

Combine the two perspectives, and you have the AI industry of 2025 in a nutshell, a joke from most any perspective not inebriated with their signature Kool-Aid. Personally, I’d love to see the Cybersecurity of 2026 turned into an Angry Birds game where players have no idea that they’re punching holes in Microsoft’s attack surface.
