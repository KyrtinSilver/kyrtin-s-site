Popular quotes are also often fallacies, such as “Eliminate the impossible, and what is left, however improbable, must be the truth” (1896, Sherlock Holmes). They are popular because they are simple and sound profound, but upon critical examination they quickly fall apart. That particular quote is also known as the “Holmesian fallacy”, because it tacitly assumes that all alternative explanations were actually recognized and considered.

The Holmesian fallacy is also the sort of thing that one may quickly find, should you look for it, in everyday life. Finding or designing good options is hard cognitive labor, making it one of the least likely things to be carried out to any exhaustive degree. This ensures that unexplored alternatives are virtually always present, even in domains and environments where that flaw is rarely recognized.

Another example of a popular, profound-sounding, and equally flawed quote is “Extraordinary claims require extraordinary evidence” (aka “ECREE”), which “is commonly invoked to discredit research dealing with scientific anomalies, and has even been rhetorically employed in attempts to raise doubts concerning mainstream scientific hypotheses that have substantive empirical support.”, as quoted from the paper “Do Extraordinary Claims Require Extraordinary Evidence?”.

Carl Sagan, who popularized ECREE, never actually bothered to define “extraordinary”, which is the scientific equivalent of sh\*tting on the kitchen counter before dinner. Rather, the origin of that sentiment is traced back to Hume, who did define “...an extraordinary claim as one that is directly contradicted by a massive amount of existing evidence.”. This is a far cry from how the phrase is popularly invoked today, where the status quo is usually assumed to represent “a massive amount of existing evidence”, even when there is no substantial or certain evidence to speak of.

The abundant examples of such popular quotes that fall apart under scrutiny is itself an example of Cognitive Bias, neglecting the Population Dynamics whose selection pressures decide which quotes become popular, before Survivorship Bias takes hold. Humans like witty-sounding and easier to remember quotes, and those quotes echo across any place where they may be spoken at proportionately greater frequency, further reinforcing their persistence in the memory of populations over time.

Many of the most popular quotes are also severely warped in the re-telling, such as “Power tends to corrupt, and absolute power corrupts absolutely” (Lord Acton, 1887) itself being “corrupted” into the more common version that loses the word “tends”, with the “absolute” part being hypothetical, and thus of far less importance than the former statement.

You also get frequent mis-attribution of quotes, such as Gandhi being mis-credited with the quote “first they ignore you, then they laugh at you, then they fight you, then you win” despite there being no record of him ever saying it, and even with the known and verified origin being that of Nicholas Klein, a Union leader in 1914.

Quotes are memorable, and useful, but they are tools of communication. Take great care before attempting to apply them in scientific processes, and if you naively invoke “ECREE”, prepare to get your \*ss handed to you.
