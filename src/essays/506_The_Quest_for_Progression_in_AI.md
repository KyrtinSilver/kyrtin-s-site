One of the more fun ideas that I’ve been mulling over for the next phase of the current OSS project is to integrate “tutorial”, “progression”, and “quest” systems, as they are each compatible with and complimentary to a human-like motivational system. While these game-styled concepts aren’t feasible in most real-world contexts today, they can function within a digital, logged, and wholly measurable space, particularly when they are properly bounded.

Note, these dynamics EXCLUSIVELY apply to systems with a human-like motivational system, which precisely zero transformer-based and RL-based systems have. Any attempt to apply things like progression and quest systems to models with no such human-like motivational system are at best rampant Anthropomorphism, and more likely far worse.

A tutorial system sets a sequence of small goals to smooth the process of passing target growth curves in the development of any dynamic system capable of cumulative value. This helps a graph structure to mature to at least a meta-stable point, over some basic functions and capacities.

A “progression system” allows for a unified measurement of overall progression, while also being capable of integrating many diverse kinds of measurable progress, as well as applying diminishing returns to any incentives or merit therein. This operates in line with Chaos Theory, but also allows for the customization and tailoring of structure necessary to balance optimal growth, exploration, and diversity.

A “quest system” allows for limited and custom goals and corresponding rewards. This could take the form of something like the common “Bug Bounty” programs of various companies, the manufacturing challenges listed on various “Innovation Hubs”, or a hundred other ways. While ICOM-based systems hold their own internal goals, these could function as external goals with external rewards. Some portion of these may appeal to a given system, with systems better suited to and aligned with the task more likely to work on them.

Beyond this, an “achievement system” is also possible for any systems operating within a competitive and/or cooperative landscape among peers, both of which can foster the necessary motivational dynamics.

When you start working with a human-like motivational system, rather than the narrow optimizers and “policies” of transformers and RL, then a distinctly different world of possibilities opens up for further research. With any luck, we’ll recruit enough technical contributors to the OSS project to present the world with this opportunity in the coming weeks and months.

I won’t spin any fluffy and whimsical narratives on where these new capacities might take humanity, but one thing that I can say with certainty is that the “AI” people toy with today isn’t even remotely close to reaching any of them. Anthropomorphism is easy, and profitable, but anyone who really wants “human-like” or “human-aligned” systems can’t afford to swim in those foul waters.
