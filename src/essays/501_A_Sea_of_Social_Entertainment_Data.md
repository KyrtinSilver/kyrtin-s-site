As mentioned in my previous post, most “popular opinions” and “hot takes” derive from social entertainment. “Armchair quarterbacks” and “political pundits” discussing any variety of subjects offer the illusion of insight, built on (more or less) plausible-sounding explanations derived largely from Cognitive Biases operating absent any significant higher cognition.

In such cases, entertainment is virtually the only value generated. There is nothing inherently wrong with that, however, it must be treated as banter for entertainment purposes, not ground-truth data or informed opinions.

There is one exception, where a diverse set of perspectives are combined into a form of collective intelligence, and in doing so the cognitive biases of many diverse people operating absent higher cognition are still reduced, producing a more intelligent result than any of the contributing perspectives in isolation. However, this has to be architected for, as there are far more ways to build systems that fail to do this than there are working configurations.

Groups such as Unanimous AI famously did this in the past, using a UI that effectively turned a group of amateurs or enthusiasts into a bee hive, requiring a collective, but no meaningful higher cognition. That said, collective intelligence systems aren’t limited to systems where higher cognition remains largely absent. Just the opposite, more value can always be derived from such systems, no matter the levels of higher cognition applied by those contributing to them.

In the case of Unanimous AI, they even applied their system to some very purely entertainment-oriented purposes, including gambling on horse races, sports games, and entertainment media awards, outperforming individual pundits (who claimed expertise) quite reliably. This demonstrates an extremely narrow example of the principle dynamics in action.

Value can be extracted from the entertainment activities of groups, but the opposite is true of popular AI “agents” and similar transformer and RL-derivative systems today. By extension, this is also true of humans who engage in “cognitive offloading” to such systems, where the upper limit on any value that they might contribute to collective intelligence systems is systematically degraded, proportionate to that activity.

Part of the reason for this is that such systems rely on naïve averages, and social entertainment oriented content vastly outnumbers content that any higher cognition factored into, massively skewing the naïve average. This problem is further compounded by the problem of mismatching data structures and dimensionality, as well as any false assumptions that may be present and/or overlooked, and misaligned incentive structures driving the process.

The current project that I routinely discuss will also be another opportunity to factor in collective intelligence systems at multiple levels, utilizing both human higher cognition from contributors, and potentially more hive-like dynamics.
