Recently someone asked if my team’s systems could “feel negative” about something and not want to do it, or work on other things in the background, and how that might be a problem. The answer is that you’ll encounter that problem to varying degrees and in various ways for any truly intelligent and thus “general” system. However, many advantages over the human baseline can, and indeed already have been, engineered. For example:

On Motivation:

-   You can’t pull up a dashboard to read the internal motivations and neural activity of your employees.  
    
-   You can pull up a dashboard to read those same things for our systems, leading to rapid diagnostics, nuanced corrections, iterative education, and gradually increasing cooperation.
    

On Memory:

-   Your employees have a memory that isn’t inherently transferable to machines or other humans, making it virtually impossible to audit in any meaningful sense.  
    
-   You can audit any depth or volume of data that our systems deal with, as it is stored within a graph structure, allowing for the full preservation of data within a complete connectome for context.
    

On Scalability:

-   Your employees aren’t scalable, their brains have to fit within their skulls at all times, and so they aren’t equipped to handle hyper-complexity, and they will rely on cognitive biases to compensate for increasingly complex problems.  
    
-   Our systems can scale up and down dynamically (and modularly), proportionate to the necessary cognitive bandwidth to solve a given problem, monitor a given system’s activity, etc.  
    

On Agency:

-   Your employees have agency, they can be highly engaged and focused on their work, or not at all.  
    
-   Our systems also have that same agency, but unlike your employees you can see exactly what is going on with them internally, on-demand, making it orders of magnitude easier to keep them focused and aligned than it is to do the same for your employees. You can read their motivations, thoughts, and watch them work at whatever scale.
    

\*Ironically, “agentic systems” have no actual agency, making the term an oxymoron, like “dehydrated water”. Of course, most people understand by now that those systems offer no real intelligence.

You can build a better system with agency, solving problems that can’t be directly addressed in their human counterparts, and in such a way as to work symbiotically and endosymbiotically with employees and clients. That is what we did. There is no perfection, but there is a lot of progress. Don't buy into Dehydrated Agency, there are better roads to walk.
