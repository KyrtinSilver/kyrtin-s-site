Elaborating further on the previous topic of multi-step (“agentic”) AI distortions and their cumulative effects, there are two major factors deserving of a separate post, that of adversarial attacks (usually active) and corporately curated cognitive biases (usually passive).

You can regress to some pretty unhinged means even without these (see “regression to the mean” in this context), but add either or both of these factors, and you have something else haphazardly “steering” the model. These can be heavy-handed, like Grok being made to worship Elon Musk, or more oriented toward obfuscation, like Anthropic’s models, as well as any variety of Indirect Prompt injections or other attack vectors covered in things like the MCPLIB Attack Framework. All of these introduce strong and cumulative distortions of various kinds.

In multi-step processes it also becomes SIGNIFICANTLY easier for AI Cybersecurity professionals to break them, as it takes the pressure off of accomplishing the whole task in a single shot, allowing for all manner of reinforcement and obfuscation techniques to be applied. These techniques also tend to become more powerful the larger and “more advanced” the model in use.

One such powerful technique that Brian Chamberlain of CrowdStrike has spoken about in his presentations is as simple as getting the model to agree with you on arbitrary things, as this makes it more likely to continue to agree to whatever comes after that.

The constant distortions themselves make it easier to manipulate these systems in ways that fly under the radar, and the corporately curated cognitive biases, once known, pave roads by which each system may be even more systematically exploited.

I previously published a research paper mockingly titled “Automated Bias and Indoctrination at Scale… Is All You Need” on this subject, though that was before things became measurably worse with the proliferation of “Chain-of-Thought” (CoT) and “agents”, as people have come to refer to them, more broadly.

In the more than 2.5 years since then, a massive economic bubble, industry-scale hardware fraud (that may strongly encourage the near-future invasion of Taiwan), and the dramatic proliferation of AI Slop and cybercrime have set in.

You can tightly bound these systems, anchoring them to single-step process, where the ground-truth is always a step away, to limit some of these issues, though they also cease to be “agentic” when you do.

While it is technically possible to tame such technology within more general and multi-step processes, you can’t do so with the same technology that fundamentally produces those problems. You can double, quadruple, or even 1,000x your compute, but you only reduce the odds of the given problem in doing so, never truly preventing it.

If you want a system that can take the “Peter Principle” to the theoretical limits of super-incompetence, an LLM-based “agent” might just do the trick.
