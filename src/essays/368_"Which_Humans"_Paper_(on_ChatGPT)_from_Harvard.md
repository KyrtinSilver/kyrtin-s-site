A recent paper from Harvard named “Which humans?” analyzes the question of which places in the world ChatGPT’s responses actually correlate with. You can see the results at a glance, how even on trivial tasks it makes for a poor substitute for humans many places in the world, but this is a “research direction” paper, not an insights paper. [https://scholar.harvard.edu/sites/scholar.harvard.edu/files/henrich/files/which\_humans\_09222023.pdf](https://scholar.harvard.edu/sites/scholar.harvard.edu/files/henrich/files/which_humans_09222023.pdf)

Asking “How well does this correlate with (the average) of country (X)?” is only trivially useful, as it is the average of the highest and most muddled level of abstraction. It implies things for future research to explore, but tells you nothing of substance.

The problem of “Averages” in particular was stumbled upon by the US Air Force back in the late 1940s, when they measured more than 4,000 pilots on 140 dimensions and selected the 10 physical dimensions believed to be most relevant to designing a fighter pilot cockpit for the “average” pilot (middle 30% value range). “Out of 4,063 pilots, not a single airman fit within the average range on all 10 dimensions.” Even 3 of the 10 resulted in less than 3.5% of pilots matching the results.

Designing a fighter pilot’s cockpit is also many orders of magnitude simpler as a problem than aligning general-purpose and knowledge-based systems to specific human cultures, philosophies, etc., meaning that instead of 10-140 dimensions you have thousands or millions of them to deal with (or, more likely, fail to deal with).

The “average” human response of a given country is the same, it fits precisely no one.

To overcome this problem you have to get MUCH more specific, asking specific cultures questions on specific topics, tightly bounding the limits of what is examined. Otherwise, your charts are just going to be “lying with statistics”, as it will remain blatantly obvious that the responses “fit no one” when you try to average a country along poorly bounded dimensions.

Overcoming the problem of correlation being used as a poor substitute for causation is tied into this, as establishing causation in a poorly bounded space suffers from many of the same or similar exponential losses.

Again, while the chart is “thought-provoking”, the correlation of the average of entire countries isn’t terribly useful.

All LLMs and derivative systems are fundamentally misaligned with humans at the architectural level, which no tweaks to said architecture can change. Much as a toaster isn’t a viable tool for doing your taxes, LLM-based systems aren’t viable for alignment with humans, nor did they ever have the possibility of becoming viable, as every credible researcher has known for years.

So please, ask more useful and specific research questions, stop trying to make systems built to regurgitate averages look useful for things they can’t do, and avoid averaging correlations unless you’re aiming to publish a new “Spurious Correlations” book.
