One of the great ironies of the modern age is the difference between “(paid) work” and “games”, as many now experience the two:

Activity A: An individual engages in a tedious process, often for 8 hours or more per day. They are assigned tasks to complete, rewarded for doing so, and frequently suffer some variety of abuses and indignities in the process. The compensation and difficulty periodically increase. Some of these tasks are only feasible for teams, or even teams of teams, but they may promise even greater payoffs.

Often times people are graded harshly on their performance, even harassed, facing these hardships thanks to rivals and colleagues alike. Numerous “Dark Patterns” are built into the systems that they use daily, shaping what they do, when they do it, and how they think about it, often to the tune of an addict’s routines.

Some may favor challenging problems that play to their skills, while others favor nonconsensually attacking those attempting to solve said problems.

Activity B: Exactly the same as above, except that people get paid to do it, and may add it to a resume.

The two activities, games and work respectively, have grown to be virtually identical in terms of what people do and experience within them. The differences tend to be that games are much better at building a narrative, with all hardships and tedium therein playing to said narrative, while also cultivating an artificial sense of “progress”.

Consequently, these also frequently better play into the other emotional “pillars of meaning”, including “Purpose”, “Belonging”, and “Transcendence”, which are themselves strong factors within narrative storytelling.

For many jobs in the world today, it would be perfectly possible to turn them into literal games, even to the point of obscuring the work that is being done under a fantasy and/or sci-fi narrative. That could itself be turned into a Black Mirror episode to be sure, but there are no shortage of non-dystopian uses of the same.

The irony goes much deeper than this as well, since you can build roughly merit-based systems into a game. This stands in stark contrast to the distinctly anti-merit-based systems commonly found in the world’s countless “Peter Principled” companies, where executives sit comfortably at their respective levels of incompetence today.

It is also vastly easier to achieve interoperability and equivalence between two merit-based systems than between two anti-merit-based systems, meaning that merit within one company translates much more directly and clearly to merit within another similar company. Much as there are “vastly more ways of being dead than alive” (Dawkins) there are also vastly more ways of being incompetent and/or corrupt, as found in anti-merit-based systems, than there are in actual merit-based systems.

The final point of this irony is that people fantasize endlessly about this very thing, dedicating entire genres of fiction to it...And yet, no one has yet delivered that which people demand to the workplace.
