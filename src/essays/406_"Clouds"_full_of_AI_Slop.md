Virtually all “Generative AI” is possible to detect with high fidelity and absent the absurd false positives (yes, even “nano banana”), just not with the same sad technology that was used to create it. The same is true of Cybersecurity, which requires an extremely high bar of detection, as well as additional countermeasures and potential remediation.

When attempting to use one instance of a given technology to detect and/or counteract another of the same, in either domain, the best that anyone can do is to stack a House of Cards, trivial to build, but equally trivial to break. “Scaling” and “More Data” do “Jack-Shit” to mitigate this, as the dynamics are derived from the two systems operating in the same fundamental manner.

So long as the same underlying structure and flow within such brute-force compute algorithms remains the same, such as a transformer architecture with various bells and whistles duct-taped to it, then it produces both local and meta-pattern artifacts which may be detected.

However, if another system operates in the same way then it is blind to at least some (usually very large) portion of the patterns and artifacts that it produces, as it operates according to them, and the more similar the two systems are the larger those blind spots become.

In the case of the current “AI Slop” problem, the problem has been maximized by training on “Internet-Scale data” (much of it illegally), as this produces a much greater diversity of local and meta-patterns which may be produced.

In the case of image (and video) generation, for example, these systems are optimized for fooling humans, not pixel-level fidelity, where local and repeating patterns within luminosity, saturation, and specific color tones may give them away. They are further biased directly by human feedback, causing these pixel-level patterns to skew even further from those seen in real environments and images.

There is also the photography hardware, which often produces distinct effects, such as certain f-stop values for focus, which are strongly connected to brightness relative to the environment. While these effects are emulated in such “generative” systems, that emulation has no fidelity, as every single token can skew even a specific hardware emulation being invoked, for example, based on the probability that Robert Downy Jr. was photographed with an f-1.2 lens more often than an f-1.4. The end result can be made trivial to detect, given sufficiently different (and capable) technology used for the purpose.

“Short-form” AND “one-off” writing is about the only exception to this high level of detection, as the volume of data is too insignificant to make a determination with high levels of certainty, in many cases. However, people using such systems for short-form interactions usually have MANY such interactions, and the patterns across those interactions very rapidly cross the necessary thresholds for detection.

\*Note, these patterns also include forms of meta-data, such as when and in response to what those interactions occurred, not being limited to the direct contents of those interactions.

In the case of images, they’re virtually always “data-rich”, because every single pixel contains multiple types of values, giving even a thumbnail image more than many text outputs. In video, this adds a further dimension of pixel transformation over time, as well as the numerous cues of any generated audio that may be present.

Sadly, I’m sure this will be a major use case for my team’s systems, as the cutting edge of AI will be necessary to clean up this garbage dump.

The alternative is obvious.
