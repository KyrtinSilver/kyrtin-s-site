Most people are highly adept at overlooking the obvious, even to the point of seeing the opposite. For example, if a company spends 50% of their budget on marketing, that means that the goods they sell are overpriced by at least 2x. That figure was previously noted for a particular soft drink company, whose name pretty much everyone knows, and whose overpriced toilet bowl cleaner sold as though it were a drink, you’ve probably had a taste of at some point.

The same is true of “Luxury” goods, with the word “Luxury” translating directly into a thousand wholly accurate and negative statements about the absence of financial competence which the buyer demonstrated through their purchase.

“Luxury” brands, like Apple, are known for profit margins that have hovered around 66%, and a few brands are significantly worse offenders than that, with the degree of incompetence scaling accordingly. Apple also adds some value to mitigate part of that premium price tag, whereas others are the same exact leather on the same stupid bag, just with a price tag 10 times higher than it should be.

Yet, many still naively associate these things with success. These are treated as signals of financial competence, when the exact opposite is true. The amount of money that a wholly incompetent individual can burn is infinite, and “Luxury” goods are a constant reminder of that bottomless pit of human stupidity.

Warren Buffet, prophet of profit-seekers, was the exact opposite. He did like the taste of that toilet bowl cleaner soft drink if I recall correctly, but he was known for keeping his price sensitivity despite becoming quite wealthy from a long and competent career.

Warren didn’t fall prey to the common Cognitive Biases that lead so many career professionals to keep ramping up their cost of living every time their income improves. In the US, there are even worse common trends, as many are suckered into increasingly extreme forms of debt, leaving them ever more vulnerable to bad actors and bad luck alike.

For example, when I had very good access to the financial data of many tech employees across the Seattle area (from just about every major company except Apple or Nvidia), I saw this pattern repeated over and over again through the combination of knowing the income and equity compensation of each person, alongside the credit cards that each held and the sum of their debts. $160,000 per year salary for a middle manager, with over 30 credit cards and over $300,000 in debt.

A half-wit who can’t live comfortably on $160,000 per year without a trace of debt shouldn’t be responsible for managing anyone, but you find major tech companies full of such glaringly obvious incompetence. An individual can live quite comfortably while traveling the world on a $25,000 per year budget, or even less, as I personally demonstrated for several years following my years in Seattle.

The next time you see luxury goods or advertisements, try to recognize them for what they are.
