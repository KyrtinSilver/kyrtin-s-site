There are three primary approaches being used to “advance” AI today:

1\. You jam some kind of structure into the model, like a graph, RLHF, CoT, MoE, more “system prompt”, “guardrails”, GOFAI output filters, etc.

2\. You change what gets fed into the model (in training and/or usage), like prompt “optimizers”, GOFAI input filters, intentionally biased training data, or (worst of all) “synthetic data”.

3\. You build structure around the model to contain it, dictating what roles it fulfills, while giving it no unnecessary, unvalidated, or insecure data, points of contact, or control.

While all three are compatible concepts, only the first two are used in practice by a majority of companies today. In Cybersecurity terms, the first two are also roughly the equivalent of a screen door on a submarine without the third in place.

People have joked for many years that most of AI/ML has been a thinly veiled process of throwing spaghetti at the wall to see what sticks, which options 1 and 2 are compatible with, leading to their popularity. Option 3 on the other hand is due diligence in design and implementation (actual work), Cybersecurity and solutions architecture, which is why it has become so incredibly rare today.

Just as a coffee mug thrown into a lake doesn’t contain or control the lake, tossing a line into the system prompt, a chunk of RLHF into the training, or looping outputs back to inputs so that a system can eat its own tail won’t contain or control the lake either. When you build the structure to contain it, then it is both secure and may optimize within that secure environment to fulfill specific functions.

When you don’t have containment on that metaphorical body of water, you also get contamination, proportionate to the size, scope, and exposure of it. “Internet-scale data” and consumer markets mean the maximum contamination and scope, with size being the fairy dust fueling “Magical Thinking” like the long-debunked “Scaling Hypothesis”. The net result is that your coffee mug of structure is now floating in a large and growing body of raw untreated waste water.

Option 3 is the only method that has shown real promise in terms of viable deployments, where there is some hope of ROI and some measure of actual Cybersecurity. All models marketed as “general purpose” are devoid of it, with no real hope of security or long-term commercial viability. They jam everything they can into their growing pools of untreated sludge in order to look marginally better on naively designed benchmarks, but that is an infinite road to nowhere, except breaches and bankruptcy.

Most of the tech industry’s vocal CEOs currently have a lot in common with RFK Junior, in that they like to go swimming in waste water, and when they do they turn it into a family activity. Before you swim out into the middle of that lake, know that nothing floating in it is going to make it any better.
