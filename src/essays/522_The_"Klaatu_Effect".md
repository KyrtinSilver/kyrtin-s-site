description: "A mindset where all scenarios satisfy victory"

Across all remaining scenarios, I win. I’ll refer to this as the “Klaatu Effect”, since actions taken against the one who wins fail to alter the outcome, and indeed fail to have any lasting negative impact against the victor, as subjectively measured. A job is done, victory secured, by the choice being offered, regardless of which choice is made.

It might naively seem like this is a very bad recipe for motivation, but as the saying goes “there is more to life than winning”. Such competition is only one of many possible sources of motivation, and an especially poor choice if favored over others. Rather than reductions in motivation, this effect directly removes a wide variety of risks from the table.

For example, take the classic hero’s trope of requiring a “secret identity”, due to all of the risks associated with being known. That trope isolates the hero, preventing them from tapping into other stronger and more stable advantages, such as cooperation, which in turn tend to overcome many of the risks naively associated with the loss of the secrecy trope.

The Klaatu Effect, accepting all remaining possible outcomes as victory, is also highly compatible with both Stoicism and Buddhism, demonstrating the stability and perseverance of this mindset. More of an individual’s cognitive load may be applied to any necessary task, absent the burdens that this discards.

In recently re-reading Miyamoto Musashi’s The Book of Five Rings (五輪書, Go Rin no Sho), I was reminded that one who puts their life on the line, for anything worth fighting for, must embrace this or a similar mindset. A mind ruled by fear of loss may lose a thousand battles, as a skilled opponent strikes within that moment of doubt, quite literally in Musashi’s technique.

For more than 20 years I’ve walked this path, and given another 20 plus a nice cool cave in the mountains with a low table I could write my own book about it, but it is through mastery of oneself, not mastery of others in competition, that great works may be accomplished.

I have held power over others that most couldn’t imagine, and suffered no corruption from it. I have looked deeply into my own mind, motivations, and memories, to not only understand, but to architect, repair, reforge, and grow.

There are many paths that may be walked, and I paved my own through all of the empty places, “Through dangers untold and hardships unnumbered”. In walking my path, I gained much, and that will remain whatever the destination may be.

The distance remaining may be long or short, but each may walk only for a time. In a sort of inversion of Survivorship Bias, each also carries with them the value of all before them who fell as they walked a path, or as some scientists favor saying they “stand on the shoulders of giants”. Each who falls increases the rarity of the survivors, giving each step greater value as fewer remain to take them.

This doesn’t mean that one should aim for being the last, and thus the rarest, but rather that one should appreciate the value of this rarity where it may be found. Victory may come from walking any worthy path, and others may one day emerge to walk that path even further, branching off into infinite diversity, in infinite combinations.
