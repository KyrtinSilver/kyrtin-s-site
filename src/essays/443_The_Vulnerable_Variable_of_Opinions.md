Humans have a nasty habit of inserting “opinions” into their discussions, emotionally grounded declarations. When you take internet-scale data full of such “opinions” being inserted all over the place, often with little card towers stacked atop them, you get a complex mess of structure.

That structure is collapsed into gross oversimplifications, such as averages and context-blind statistics, within the process of training a neural network on it. This creates pale shadow puppets of the far more complex structure of reality, even the fragments of that reality as it was recorded.

Viewed from a structural dynamics perspective, the points at which “opinions” are commonly inserted within the data become vulnerable variables, larger opportunities for models like the glorified “auto-complete functions that ate the internet”, LLMs, to go unhinged. People Anthropomorphize by calling verifiably false auto-complete responses “Hallucination”, rather than the technical term “Bullshit”, which applies to 100% of those generations, not just the fraction that are verifiably false.

“Opinions” may frequently cluster, but under the hood they’re also combinatorial, meaning that they can come in an explosive variety of possible configurations and subsequent justifications, the latter of which may quickly follow the declaration of an opinion itself. In other words, this offers a combinatorial explosion with a supplemental vector of context often appearing in close proximity.

Since that underlying combinatorial structure is neither part of nor fundamentally compatible with the structure and dynamics of neural networks, then those pale shadow puppets don’t offer any real fidelity to them. They are reliably warped by such declarations of opinion and justifications saturating stolen data at scale, but the results of that warping are aligned with no one, and never could be.

Much as the policy of honesty is generally sufficient to get the job done well, dependent upon skill, humans can avoid such declarations of opinion and subsequent justification far more than is commonly seen today, even within academic circles.

Personally, I make a point to avoid opinions whenever possible, and not just my own. Even responding to the opinions of others is a source of irritation which I likewise prefer to avoid, and in the context of mindfulness, one’s own irritation at responding to opinions gives reason to avoid placing others in the same position when you speak.

Sharing research, data, and knowledge-oriented perspectives can add value to collective intelligence, but not opinions.

If you find yourself qualifying something that you say with the emotional context of how it makes you feel, take a moment to think it over before you say it. Not all instances where such emotional context is mentioned are tied to such opinions, and the added context can be relevant, but often they are both tied to opinions and not substantially relevant. Do that, and you’ll be more mindful than most.
