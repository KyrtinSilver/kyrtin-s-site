In the research literature, conditions and prerequisites for something are often described in terms of being “Necessary, but not sufficient”, meaning that while they are required, they are required in combination with some other factor(s). These two are themselves only part of the broader spectrum of the same, spanning Unnecessary, Necessary, Sufficient, and Optimal (local or global).

The first three tend to be fairly universal for a given problem, where the same factors are unnecessary, necessary, or sufficient for any people or groups approaching them. This reliably changes beyond the point of sufficiency, where optimality can depend on the specific details of the individuals, environment, and circumstances. In some cases, it also changes at the level of sufficient, such as some particular environment having deficiencies requiring some additional necessary factors.

For example, most environments might not have a chronic deficiency of water, but if a task dependent upon some quantity of water must be carried out in the desert, then a new necessary factor is added to the list for reaching a sufficient state.

Reaching optimality may also include the relocation of activity to a more suitable environment. A real-world example of this is the sadly common practice of building datacenters that require water cooling in the middle of a desert. At face value, this is an utterly moronic decision, but many still make it for the sake of getting cheaper land, since they’ve historically been able to avoid facing the long-term consequences of such actions.

Much as the problems of business may have their components placed across this spectrum of combinatorial factors, the psychological aspects of an individual mind or group can as well. In fact, if even 50% of the unnecessary elements bogging down the average individual or team were swept away, predictable improvements could be observed. For some opportunities “Spring Cleaning” of the unnecessary is a hard requirement, such as becoming a Digital Nomad requiring distilling one’s possessions down to what can fit in luggage.

For making any change of non-trivial consequence in the world, especially positive changes, it is necessary to overcome fear, because historically there has always been a bad actor with the incentive to attack sources of such change. Overcoming fear is necessary, but to reach the level of being sufficient then actions much be taken, walking the path necessary to deliver those positive changes.

With sufficient practice, then local optimality often comes fairly easily. Moving from local optimality to global optimality often requires retrospective and introspective examination, finding the flaws in one’s own methods and ways of thinking, and overcoming them one by one.

In practice, many people tend to learn that some portion of what they’ve carried with them was unnecessary as they walk such paths, and removing those extraneous factors helps them to move from local optimality to global optimality. In this way, unlearning is itself a necessary, but not sufficient, part of learning mastery.

For unlearning along the road to global optimality, an old Zen proverb comes to mind:

“(On this particular day, a scholar came to visit the master for advice. “I have come to ask you to teach me about Zen,” the scholar said.

Soon, it became obvious that the scholar was full of his own opinions and knowledge. He interrupted the master repeatedly with his own stories and failed to listen to what the master had to say. The master calmly suggested that they should have tea.

So the master poured his guest a cup. The cup was filled, yet he kept pouring until the cup overflowed onto the table, onto the floor, and finally onto the scholar’s robes. The scholar cried “Stop! The cup is full already. Can’t you see?”

“Exactly,” the Zen master replied with a smile. “You are like this cup — so full of ideas that nothing more will fit in. Come back to me with an empty cup.”)”

Some cups must be emptied for such a journey to be completed.
