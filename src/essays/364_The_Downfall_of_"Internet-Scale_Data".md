Does your daily life look like the daily news headlines, or your newsfeed? If the answer is “No”, then best to think twice before using LLMs.

What people talk about online and what actually happens aren’t the same thing. If anything, they are nearly opposites. People talk about novelty and exceptions far more than anything else, things that stand out in a sea of the mundane reality of life.

Worse yet, misinformation and disinformation were found to spread ~6 times further and faster than accurate information in a famous study by MIT in 2018, meaning that 6 times more people are 6 times more likely to discuss novelty and exceptions that never existed, as though they were true or real, on the internet.

Even some conservative back-of-the-envelope calculations can illustrate why this is so damaging for the kind of AI that so many billions of dollars have already been poured into. For example:

Let’s say that 90% of what people talk about online is discussing novelty and exceptions, such as rare events that get news coverage. In actuality, those events might constitute 0.1% or less of what makes up reality, which could be bumped up to 1% when considering second-order effects and context supplied when discussing them.

This means that 90% of internet-scale training data is statistically skewed toward 0.1%-1% of reality, at a proportionately extreme ratio.

Factor Misinformation and Disinformation into that, and you have a 1:6 ratio of true and false material being discussed, meaning that 77.14% of your 90% is pure fiction presented as fact, some part of which is also malevolent (disinformation). Most of this also flows through “Influencers” today, so the problem has likely grown worse, as many of them are easily bought.

So that 90% extreme skew is now divided into 77.14% fiction and 12.86% skewed facts.

The Internet isn’t a reflection of reality, it is closer to a rumor mill, gossip factory, advertisement, or a meat grinder. Training on all of that data doesn’t get you intelligence, it gets you confident ignorance. You can’t get a “World Model” without the right ratio of reality, and without purging the high volumes of pure fiction.

In the process of someone telling me how one “reasoning” model gravitated toward extreme conclusions with nearly every interaction, even where I found it trivial to see far simpler and more mundane explanations to the same material as they went over it with me, I realized that this needs to be said.

The statistical average of the internet is rarely a good answer, and corporately curated bias fudging those statistics (via RLHF and “guardrails”) only degrades them, causing them to no longer even fit to the curve of that data.

The human mind is antifragile, making it far more capable of handling these kinds of harsh and even extreme data hazards, but if a human were fed an exclusive diet of the Internet’s data they’d undoubtedly go clinically insane 9 times out of 10. Personally, I wouldn’t want to meet number 10 either.
