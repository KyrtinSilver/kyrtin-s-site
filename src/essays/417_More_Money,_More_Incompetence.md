Baseline “Financial Competence” is one of the rarest skills in much of the world today. This is increasingly the case over time, as the success rates and number of attack vectors for manipulating people into financial incompetence have steadily grown, activity which may predictably continue under the status quo.

One of the common problems in US and similar cultures is the tendency to increase overhead costs to whatever level that income may rise to (absent any real reason to do so), while also neglecting to buffer for unexpected events. Both are utter nonsense, and strongly contribute to this problem. When auditing the finances of tech industry employees at virtually every major firm with a strong presence in Seattle (Microsoft, Google, Amazon, Facebook….basically everyone except for Apple, at the time), this is why I’d see things like a Microsoft PM with $160K base salary and 33 credit cards, with over $300K in card debt.

Cognitive Biases frequently encourage people to associate those who make more money with greater financial competence, but the exact polar opposite is usually true in practice. This is also a strong contributor to the problem of grossly incompetent investors investing almost exclusively in their own grossly incompetent friends (and the obvious scammers who feed on them, like Altman and Amodei).  
  
The higher people rise in the tech industry, the more financially incompetent many of them appear to become in practice. That is based on empirical and first-hand observation of the industry in Seattle, up close and personal.

That was also governed by the “Peter Principle”, before “GenAI” came along to empower those already at their own “Level of Incompetence” to rise further to their respective levels of “Super-incompetence”. The damage from this has neither noticeably slowed down nor stopped, even if the hype train has lost steam.

One of the reasons why it is frequently difficult to get a team of veteran coders bootstrapping is because of this problem. They have technical competence with code, but many don’t have a shred of financial competence, even while many of them do have mortgages to pay and families to support. Financial matters aren’t really taught in the classical education settings, and so many reach such points in life without ever developing those skills, leading to death by 1,000 financial cuts. It is trivial to do better, with even a bit of education.

For example, I can, and do, live a better life on less than 1/10th the income of some people in Seattle, while avoiding debt, which they do not. This is perfectly possible given grounding to actual needs and costs of living, since goods marked as “luxury” may as well read “scamming any moron who’ll buy this crap”.

Combine this severe and pervasive financial incompetence with countless algorithms aimed at maximizing exploitation, over a population that is so docile that it has failed to overthrow an openly malevolent and violent regime, and the result can’t reliably even be called a “nation” of “humans”, as both terms cease to be true beyond some threshold. Where to draw that line is open for debate, but the existence of the line is not.

Beyond raw biology, what is it that makes someone “human”?

Most of the answers I’ve heard are things being destroyed in the above case.
