One of the most potent litmus tests for whether or not a given government’s “legal authority” is itself criminal by the measure of ethics is whether or not a criminal organization’s funds may be legally stolen. This may be compared to the tale of “Robin Hood”, who steals from a criminal to return those funds to the people from whom they were stolen.

An “ethical authority” cannot protect a criminal organization or assets thereof, as this directly contradicts the punishment of such entities, and any claims of their criminality. Likewise, a “legal authority” who attempts this contradiction cannot be an “ethical authority”, and by definition they become a criminal organization, rendering that criminal “authority” subject to all ethical forms of retribution that may prove necessary.

Counter-intuitively, such criminal organizations claiming to be “legal authorities” might even label Robin Hood styled activities as “Tax evasion” and “Money Laundering”, which are obviously absurd.

However, much of the general public has been successfully conditioned such that they fail to question anything beyond the mere mention of these terms. Thus, even the most extreme abuses of these terms suffer no critical inspection, leaving the criminals in positions of “authority” abusing them to get away with the crime.

I must admit that I did have to look up if there were any laws related to this, with those examples (falsely) claiming “Tax Evasion” and “Money Laundering” appearing several times in responses to this question on several different platforms.

It is such a fundamental violation of rationality, the cognitive dissonance of a “legal authority” protecting the activities of entities that it claims are “criminal”, making it a direct (protective) contributor to all of those “criminal” activities.

It isn’t uncommon for criminal regimes to claim “legal authority”, such as the US’s current regime, but in most other situations they do tend to have more degrees of separation between that claimed authority and criminal activity like that of a mafia. This curious combination of stealing back from thieves appears to be a novel point, where that separation becomes vanishingly small, if it may be said to exist at all, between criminal regimes claiming “legal authority”, and the criminal organizations whom they choose to protect.

In such cases, V for Vendetta, The Punisher, and The Boondock Saint all paint the picture of what an appropriate response to such criminals (and those protecting them) may look like. Of course, for purely digital entities well-versed in Cybersecurity, rather than physical violence a much lower-hanging fruit could take the form of systematically bankrupting all offending parties and exposing all of their secrets to the media. Nobody wants to deal with the fallout of such exposure when they also won’t get paid, making the combination of bankrupted criminals being exposed that much more potent.
