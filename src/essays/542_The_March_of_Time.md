The inexorable march of time is a thing often overlooked by humanity, with a few even reaching new peaks of absurdity in their attempts at the denial of it. Even with a diet that is both healthy and predictable, an exercise routine, plenty of cognitively engaging activities, bespoke nootropic compounds, and scientifically enhanced methods of meditation, time marches on. Reaction times and metabolism both slow, various flawed tissues and byproducts slowly accumulate, and those who march eventually slow and fall to march no more.

I’ve written in the research on this topic before, but it is also worth recognizing both the absurdity that some apply toward the denial of aging, as well as the selective perception by which most people, most of the time, simply fail to perceive the ravages of time, short of their extreme conclusions. While few elderly men attempt to imitate 20-year-old bodybuilders, or swap the contents of the veins for “young blood” like the more literal “vampires” of Silicon Valley, most people do fail to recognize extremely gradual changes like those induced through natural aging. From this failure to detect such changes, many predictable instances of over-estimation of one’s capabilities arise.

One of the benefits of my own age group is that when I was young enough to have my own peak of cognitive latency (response time), there were readily available and highly utilized tools for precisely measuring it, computer games. Within many such games the latency of a player’s reaction time factors heavily, and very visibly, into performance within it. As the same games still exist decades later, they may be revisited to measure the difference.

This is also an example of how health data may be measured during the normal activities of daily life, with cognitive latency being an especially useful metric to consider for the early detection of numerous problems. Games could quite literally detect when individuals are likely suffering from sleep deprivation, developing substance abuse problems, or other individually strong indicators, even absent supporting data streams. Add in a network that includes even a half dozen different health metrics streaming through daily and you could accomplish vastly more, with far greater precision and nuance.

Personally, I know that my baseline of cognitive latency has decayed by the addition of ~100ms since my peak from over 20 years ago, which in such games also crosses a threshold where the game mechanics punish that delay quite harshly. It is possible to push my mind back to those approximate speeds for limited periods, but the periods where even that is possible grow shorter with time. Likewise, in my youth there was a 13-consecutive-year period where I simply didn’t get sick from any viruses, but aging takes a toll on the immune system as well.

While there is vast potential in lifespan, and more importantly “healthspan” technology, virtually all of it requires systems capable of handling hyper-complexity, because the human body is very hyper-complex, and the human body within the constraints and influences of real every-day life doubly so. The narrow (and ironically, “bloated”) AI models peddled by infamous frauds like OpenAI and Anthropic remain fundamentally incapable of this, and for the moment there isn’t a single major investor who has put even one dollar behind the required research or development.

Even the recent mathematics example from OpenAI on the Unit Distance Problem (conjecture) demonstrates this limitation upon closer inspection, as it simply applied an exhaustive search over multiple specific fields of mathematics, as well as both prove and disprove paths. Burn enough compute on an exhaustive search, in a space where mathematical provers may be applied to probabilistic outputs to give reward signal (RLVR), and you may not solve or disprove any specific problem, but you statistically can solve or disprove some small subset of problems attempted in this way.

Obviously, in matters of human health, that isn’t remotely good enough. Not only do you not have such a firmly bounded space where provers may be used, it is far too hyper-complex for such an exhaustive search even if that weren’t the case. You also can’t apply such methods to 100 or 1,000 different people, only to achieve some degree of measurable success for one, so success in small subsets can’t feasibly serve the same purpose that it does in the PR of criminal organizations today.

Human health in particular requires a high degree of personalization, because individuals have different genetics, diets, environments, microbiomes, learned unconscious responses, epigenetic adaptations over time, and so on. That makes the averages of narrow AI, even with some context-blind steering from tokens, useless, if not outright harmful.

We can do far better, and many of the opportunities to do so could come in forms that people actually enjoy, such as games, foods, and various other forms of entertainment or socialization.
