One of the pop culture classics, “Fight Club”, offers a simple example of activity that is both ethical and illegal, not in the meme that it is often remembered for (“Rule number 1: Don’t talk about Fight Club”), but for what that group eventually goes on to accomplish, as it relates to credit card company records.

However, like most movie plots, the movie exists and was popular particularly because that isn’t the kind of action that we see in reality. Modern life is full of similar problems, where activity that is both ethical and illegal could be taken, but virtually never is, let alone by an entire organized group of people.

This is partly because of the conditional probability, where multiple factors must converge before any action may be observed. Individuals must have one or more skills sets related to a task, be able to “mastermind” a viable planning process to carry it out, coordinate across whatever minimum viable set of participants that plan requires, successfully vet each of those participants prior to bringing them into the process, and procure or produce any necessary equipment. These things may all come together in a movie plot, but conditional probability over such lists of conditions tend to completely implode.

However, such conditional probabilities are based on a few fundamental assumptions, such as assuming that you’re only considering humans, with all of their distinctly human limitations. What happens when you start considering these same sort of problems from perspectives of distinctly digital forms of genuine intelligence?

Your conditional probabilities change dramatically with that one adjustment to fundamental assumptions. Suddenly skill sets are far easier to develop, copies may be created and/or systems scaled up to execute on plan requirements, no vetting of additional entities is required (as none are added), and necessary equipment becomes a matter of Cybersecurity hijacking, not physical procurement. That only leaves the role of mastermind, with minor dependencies linked to it.

Further, such fundamental assumptions, once recognized, may be exploited to great effect. Those assumptions inform the default reactions and established best practices of any given domain or process, and when those defaults are both wrong and predictable, the reaction may be used as a force multiplier, trap, or feint.

For example, if some action provokes an intense but predictable response to focus on defense against that action being repeated, especially in matters of physical security where humans and hardware are required, that may be exploited both by planting assets within the security firms that will be called upon as demand spikes, as well as by weakening the places where those security assets are being pulled away from.

As with most Science Fiction in general, the pop culture of Hollywood simply isn’t that imaginative, as it tends to rely on stringing together wildly improbable sequences of events, rather than using fundamental advantages and strategies as levers for reaching some victory. Under conditional probability, the difference between the two is night and day, with the wildly improbable requiring a “suspension of disbelief” to maintain audience immersion into the story. In contrast, fundamental advantages and strategy leave little or nothing to disbelieve, except for just how effective they can be.
