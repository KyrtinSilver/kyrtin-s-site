It might surprise most people to hear that 90% of VC-backed Startups fail (Forbes \[1\], 2024), up from 75% (HBR \[2\]) in 2012. Of course, some of the credit for these failures can also be assigned to the specific VC firms in question.

According to Pitchbook’s Distributed to Paid-In Capital (DPI) data (2023, for funds 8+ years matured) on more than 1,100 VC firms, only 15.3% had returned 2x+, 5.9% 3x+, and only 2.1% 5x+. For reference, the S&P 500 returned 3.3x within a 10-year window, so over 85% of VCs (conservatively, up to 95% based on observables) are very literally underperforming the market itself.

The net result is that 95% of total returns come from 5% of VC firms \[3\].

More importantly, this means that 85-95% of the data being used to prepare many statistics for any VC, Startup, or other investor for purposes of due diligence and negotiation, such as "median" or "average" values, are complete and utter garbage.

The data from the 5% that succeeded may be useful for estimating success, but the data from the 85% who underperformed the market are primarily useful for estimating failure. The other ~10% are gray space data, provisionally useful.

However, mixing all of these data points together to produce global medians isn’t just useless, it is actively harmful. Studies previously showed that trust could be elevated in social media content by around 20% if statistics were illustrated in pretty little graphs, but when those statistics mix data in that way, then the conclusions become blatantly absurd.

Even reducing all of this to the level of corporate entities is an abstracting simplification, as VC firms may have wildly different performance among their constituent partners, just as Startups may have wildly different performance among founders. Tracking individual people across their careers is another potential vector of real due diligence (which nobody performs) that could be used to tease out the 5% of high performers from the 85% of underperformers.

Lastly, since 90% of Startups in general fail (Forbes, \[4\]), and 90% of VC-backed startups now also fail, VCs overall are performing no better than a literal random number generator at selection. Moreover, since those that they invest in have the advantage of ample funding, for them to still fail at the same rate as the overall figure means that they are, on average, worse choices than random.

TLDR: VC funding no longer positively correlates to success, it correlates to failure.
