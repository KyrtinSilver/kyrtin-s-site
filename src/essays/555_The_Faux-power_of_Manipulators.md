One of the stranger abuses of modern language that humanity has developed is encompassed by the terms “Soft power” and “Hard power”, neither one of which is truly “Power”. Rather, “Soft power” and “Hard power” are both forms of persuasion (or coercion), one of which focuses on social pressure, and the other using economic and/or military methods.

Bad actors often specialize in one or both of these forms of faux-power, as both are methods of manipulating others, both in positions of “authority”, and at scale. However, those specialized in such methods tend to have no True Power at all.

True Power is that which an individual can accomplish without the assistance of, or even in spite of, anyone and everyone else. It belongs entirely to the individual in question, requiring no persuasion, coercion, or even “compliance” from others. It merely is.

True Power is also extended by the individual entity’s ability to overcome any and all barriers and/or hostile forces, which can take on countless forms of methods and strategy. This may be measured as the fragility or antifragility quality of that True Power, which in turn may tell you how long it may be expected to last under various conditions.

Another striking point of contrast between faux-power and True Power is the difference between those who seek one or the other. The famous quote claiming that “power corrupts” is specifically a reference to faux-power, the ability to manipulate others. This also highlights why “absolute (faux-)power corrupts absolutely”, because it is merely the absolute ability to manipulate others, not to be confused with the real thing.

This often leads those with no True Power to seek out and “climb” their way up through hierarchies as they seek to accumulate ever-greater faux-power.

In contrast, those who focus on developing True Power often ignore hierarchies, steamroll through barriers, and deny corruption by giving it no place to sink in. A skilled hacker, a Buddhist monk, a researcher, or an inventor may cultivate such power, as they develop, enrich, and extend their own capabilities across the passage of time. While they may earn the respect of others who use their eyes to see (a thing which is often sadly uncommon today), they have no need of hierarchies to climb, or people to manipulate.

To those with True Power, hierarchies and the persuasion of others are points of irritation, pointless headaches and tedious games to be avoided whenever possible. Even “Changing the World” requires none of this when ample methods and resources have been applied to the task.

While some people may attempt to use the methods of faux-power for the sake of gathering resources, in doing so the effort often tends to be self-corrupting, and thus self-destructive. Masses may be predicted absent manipulation, but once manipulation takes root then True Power decays.

True Power also holds another critically important property, in that those who cultivate it are vastly more resilient against the manipulations of bad actors who’ve specialized themselves in faux-power (Soft and Hard alike). Another entertaining point made in the research literature is that those specialized in faux-power also make themselves more vulnerable to it, studied in the case of marketers being more vulnerable to marketing. In this way, True Power strengthens defenses, while faux-power weakens them.

Another facet of True Power is that it makes it far easier to cooperate with others, as none of it may be put at risk through such cooperation. This facilitates collective intelligence, where symbiotic and endosymbiotic relationships progressively increase the fitness of all parties involved.

Faux-power also often favors “Majority Rule” on paper, while actually creating a “Tyrannical Minority” via Oligarchies, Feudalism, and through pitting two sides against one another in such a way that a “swing” minority between the two is empowered to maximally exploit both sides, all of which may be observed in the US. This strongly cultivates polarization, but that polarization is itself a strong benefit for the bad actors operating therein.

Faux-power is also often fetishized, and sexualized, leading to a greatly increased degree of overlap with people like Jeffrey Epstein, and the one who was famously “Blowing Bubba” in front of Putin, Donald Trump. Those who focus on manipulating others frequently do so for sexual purposes.
