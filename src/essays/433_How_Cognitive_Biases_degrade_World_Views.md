In Behavioral Economics, it is reasonably well-studied that humans become progressively less anchored to ethical and moral concepts with every added degree of complexity, even when such an added layer is utterly trivial. The famous example to first demonstrate this involved simply paying one group of study participants with a token that they exchanged for the same amount of real currency, within the same room. No added ambiguity, just one extra and trivially simple step, but the participants became measurably less ethical as a result.

Many systems in the world today are designed explicitly not to be understood, full of not just many wholly unnecessary steps, but many additional sources of intentional ambiguity and other uncertainties. Unsurprisingly, the industries where such opaque systems are the norm also tend to be the least ethical, and the most parasitic. They frequently engage in the many flavors of “Economic Warfare” as they exist today.

For average citizens in many “developed” countries, these dynamics have an interesting, and no doubt intended, side effect. They make it far less likely for people to realize when they’ve crossed the line from being a free individual, to a modern slave. Modern slavery needs no physical collars, chains, or work camps, but instead it leverages a vast variety of scams that lure people into debt, as well as numerous other forms of “learned helplessness”, where they are made to feel trapped in a paper-thin system of control.

All of this ties in very directly to problems of complexity and a couple of hundred known Cognitive Biases that the human mind fluidly and automatically uses to cope with such complexity. “Authority Bias”, and related biases, are a common example that plays into this particular set of problems heavily. Many systems of modern slavery leverage governments, the very systems whose only legitimate purpose is to “protect and serve” its citizens, as a cudgel to swing at them, keeping their heads bowed low to avoid it.

Of course, governments have NEVER actually had a “monopoly on the use of violence”, and their citizens could easily overthrow them at pretty much any time, if they rebelled in any reasonable numbers. Yet, this too is prevented within many modern societies, excluding a handful of statistical outliers, by other problems of complexity and cognitive biases. In this case, the root cause is often that a comprehensive and utterly false narrative about how the world works has been conditioned into the general population from all angles.

To get the average person to abandon any belief is difficult, but to get them to abandon a comprehensive world view, however false, is predictably terrifying to them. I demonstrated this in my own life between 2005 and 2015, repeatedly, routinely, and transparently showing an assortment of people that their beliefs, particularly in government sources of “authority” and the cudgel, were a lie. No matter the evidence, they never learned.

I won’t go so far as to assign a “Because”, as I don’t keep a Panopticon inside of their skulls, but it did impress upon me the incredible degree of attachment that ordinary people assign to such world views, stronger even than their religious beliefs, which are a mere subset of that view.

The lesson of this is that unnecessary layers of complexity are often explicitly intended to tip the scales from higher cognition to cognitive biases, where populations are more easily controlled.
