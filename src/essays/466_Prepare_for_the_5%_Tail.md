Prepare for the worst, or you likely won’t survive. Prepare for the best, or you’ll never truly succeed. People often make the mistake of choosing one or the other, but this duality is about mitigating the long tail of the negative distribution, within reason, as well as taking advantage of the positive versions of the same. The two are complimentary, rather than mutually exclusive.

For example, at the start of 2022 my team set a milestone in AI that stands alone at the cutting edge to this day, and a month later the Russia-Ukraine War began. We both began preparing to seek proper funding for the technology, and we began preparing for geopolitical instability from the US, Russia, and China. I permanently left the US behind, traveling the world for several years, to this end.

We prepared for the best, the event of proper funding, viral press coverage, etc., but we also set up our assets and incomes such as to remain stable under the worst of circumstances. Both I and my CEO aren’t financially reliant on our work with the startup being properly funded, and for my part I’ll be just fine up to the point where human extinction becomes a certainty (status quo), no matter the funding or lack thereof.

Which way events flow ultimately has nothing at all to do with rationality, but rather it is a confluence of chaotic factors commonly referred to simply as “luck”. Even if your startup has the technology, people, market demand, timing, research, etc., all neatly lined up, nothing is guaranteed, nor does “better marketing” actually justify the added expense with any regularity. It remains a gamble.

Even walking people capable of funding our work through what they stood to gain (while omitting what it would cost every last one of them should they fail to do so, instead publishing a thought experiment on the subject) couldn’t inspire people to make one of the most obvious rational choices in human history. Rationality is rarely the problem, human stupidity is, as no amount of rationality may penetrate it.

The long tail of negative outcomes often require long-term planning to mitigate them at any acceptable cost, such as the cost in funds and time to uproot and travel the world. The opposite long tail from the positive outcomes distribution is often much more focused on timing, pacing, and short-term preparations, with a lighter and more flexible set of long-term requirements.

The first step toward being prepared for both ends of this duality is the awareness of what each end translates into for your particular situation, the scenarios populating positive and negative outcomes that exceed your current thresholds of preparation. It generally won’t be worthwhile to dedicate serious resources to a 0.1% scenario, but dedicating moderate resources to a tail of scenarios totalling 5% (the default scientific margin of error) can serve this purpose.

Sooner or later, virtually everyone hits the tail end of the distribution, positive or negative. You can be prepared for it, or not. The choice is yours.  
  
Regression to the Mean occurs after hitting the tail ends of the distribution, but even mediocrity has limits, so one tail or another eventually slaps even the most mediocre of people squarely in the face.
