Every year I find one or more topics that hold significant global consequences, each worth writing a 15-30 page research paper covering. Recently, I settled on the topic for my next paper, now in process:

Human Rights and Freedoms, and the role that technology has to play. (I’ll share the actual title once a draft is ready for pre-print)

To that end, I’d like to ask my network a simple question, the answer to which may contribute to the paper. That question is this:

“What Rights and Freedoms do you see as possible, preferable, and perhaps necessary, but that are not yet given to most people?”

I’m aiming to cover a very broad spectrum, addressing the status quo of where we are (and appear to be headed), as well as where else our current and near-future technological capacities could alternatively take us. One of the wonderful things about research papers, at least for those not attached to universities, is that you can cover almost any topic, even politically polarized ones. You can do this, because stupid bobble-headed pundits don’t actually read research, excluding a tiny subset that is force-fed to them.

For example, my most read paper spiked in popularity due to a handful of trolls on Reddit, and yet they never actually bothered me, I only heard about it second-hand from someone monitoring a Discord server where those trolls had built a nest. Short of drawing the ire of malevolent billionaires, which is about as difficult as drawing their favor, you can generally state everything plainly, disregarding politics and other popular beliefs, within the confines of research. For better or worse, practically nobody reads actual research. It is the handful of people who do that may well change the world.

As this paper will be tied heavily into technology, I plan to cover topics such as:

Data Sovereignty (Including the “Right to be Forgotten”),

Freedom from Manipulation (Direct and Targeted, as well as the Indirect Viral Shotgun of Disinformation)

Biomedical rights and technology (including the various forms of autonomy that entails)

Historic forms of basic human rights

Historic forms of anti-rights, practices whose existence runs directly counter to actual basic rights (I’ll be tracing at least one of these back to the Blood Feuds of ancient Athens.)

Enforcement, complete, selective, and absent

Please, let me know what additions to basic human rights you see as possible, preferable, and/or necessary for humanity to progress.
