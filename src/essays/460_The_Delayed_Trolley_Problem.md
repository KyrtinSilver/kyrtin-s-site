Delaying decisions to much later dates, as well as shifting the responsibility for making them across bureaucracies, frequently plays out in a manner similar to the Trolley Problem variation below. In this variation, the cost of making the hard decision increases every time that decision is delayed. The increase may be linear or exponential, but it reliably increases in either case.

This dynamic should sound familiar, as it is frequently observed in the bureaucratic systems of governments. People are happy to take credit for their successes within systems where they may rise through a hierarchy, but holding them accountable for their failures often proves unreliable, at best.

Just as people often attempt to “dodge” responsibility for their failures (or snatch credit from others), that activity is also time-sensitive. Move quickly and precisely enough and those human reflexes for dodging responsibility won’t cut it. Likewise, countermeasures prepared in advance often conform to recognizable patterns, which become increasingly trivial to recognize and bypass with experience.

Even relatively simple systems aimed at anti-corruption showed remarkable success over the past 10 years…so much so in fact that people in charge panicked and shut them down in places like China, where some 8,000 likely corrupt officials were flagged by one system in years previous. In 2019 the shutdown of this “Zero Trust” system was covered in many news outlets, such as the South China Morning Post saying “Despite being restricted to just 30 counties and cities, artificial intelligence system has already helped snare 8,721 officials”. Remember, this was in 2019.

Much as corruption demonstrates many recognizable patterns, so too do attempts to dodge responsibility or systematically delay decisions. While attempts to dodge responsibility may differ from corruption in terms of statistical legality, from a system dynamics perspective they are virtually identical, making it just as easy to detect such activity at a baseline.

However, attempts to dodge responsibility, on average, are also going to be much easier to detect in practice, due to Population Dynamics operating over that statistical difference in legality. That means that more effort goes into preventing corruption from being detected than the dynamically comparable activities of those seeking to dodge responsibility.

Clear-cut examples of both corruption and systematic delays were demonstrated by individuals such as Mitch McConnell in the US, delaying the appointment of judges, then ramming through as many as possible during his window of control, particularly as that window was closing in 2020. Each such example of the two being successfully paired together and going unpunished also dramatically increases the odds of the same occurring again.

The current mega-scale AI Bubble was entirely avoidable, but there is no avoiding the global financial damage now, many delays deep.
