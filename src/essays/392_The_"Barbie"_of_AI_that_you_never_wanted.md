To borrow a term from Cass R. Sunstein, most consumer “AI” today is a “Barbie” to commercial entities, meaning that it is something that they wish didn’t exist, but are expected to buy into since it does.

People don’t “need” a new iPhone each year, but they can easily be exploited into buying one when release schedules reinforce this social expectation and frame it as reflecting some aspect of identity.

We see the very same process taking place in the commercial domain, where companies are told that “if you don’t adopt AI now, you’ll fall behind in the eyes of your customers”. This exploits Cognitive Biases related to Peer Pressure, Loss Aversion, and sense of identity.

The claim, of course, is objectively false, but if these Dark Patterns successfully exploit corporations and/or their customers, then the damage is done despite the claim being a lie. Effectively, those peddling this particular lie are attempting extortion and coercion, with unfortunately high rates of success.

Apple was one of the few to resist this, for a time, but they eventually caved into it, even while publishing research clearly showing that the claims being peddled were false.

A lot of people are actually looking forward to the next AI winter now, as a potential end to this extortion and coercion. This is part of why even those who understand just how catastrophic the current bubble popping could be often discuss it with a sort of reserved glee.

I personally wouldn’t buy any phone or similar electronics bragging about “AI-integrated (whatever)”, being deep enough into Cybersecurity to recognize vulnerable-by-design systems. Rather than being enticed by such claims, I recognize them as traps for the gullible, including those companies gullible enough to integrate them.

Yes, some idiots on Wall Street might undervalue your stock if you don’t integrate these weak kinds of AI and related Dark Patterns, but that is a small loss to avoid the much larger backlash when the bubble bursts. Short-term thinking and easily coerced companies and their funds are soon parted when the market slams face-first into a “correction”.

For more on Sunstein’s discussion of the “Barbie” concept, see his new book “Manipulation”.
