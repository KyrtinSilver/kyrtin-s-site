Today’s comical new example of the “confused deputy” class of cyberattacks (the $50,000 coupon minting noted previous was another) is instructing an “agent” to provide you with a report of its own vulnerabilities. This example comes to us from Michael Blake, a hacker who has raked in some substantial bug bounties over the years: [https://x.com/michael1026h1/status/1946801939641196589](https://x.com/michael1026h1/status/1946801939641196589)

Yes, if you have just enough knowledge to know which report to ask for and the proper n-grams to ask for it (or copy-pasting from someone who does), an “agent” can now assist you in exploiting it.

This manner of attack still requires some knowledge to exploit the vulnerability information that the agent freely provides, but once again the bar for the attacker is substantially lowered as another step of the attack process has an opportunity to be greatly accelerated, if not outright automated. Given that the attack information adheres to an expected report format, this also trivializes the automation of subsequent steps in the same attack chain.

For example, if step 1 is automated and outputs an expected report format, step 2 might be automated up to 80% with fairly low effort and knowledge required, with step 3 automated up to 60%, and so on. This process may branch out in practice, so some specific branches will be more or less trivial to automate away.

Formatted vulnerability information is a higher bar of data quality than many businesses have internally, making it ironic to see cyberattacks doing a better job at automation and interoperability than their targets often do. As cyberattackers also have nothing to defend and no disclosure requirements, they also have more opportunity and incentive to innovate than said businesses or those attempting to defend them.

If you want below-average (AI slop) fantasy, the PR of AI has got you covered, but if you want a screenshot of reality, look to Cybersecurity, the people who break the illusions in the real world. Cybersecurity goes beyond the academic (but still often labor-intensive) debunking of popularized BS, applying those lessons in practice, through iteration, and in combination to pulverize BS into a fine paste, ready to flush.

While industries struggle to justify the absurd funds that they’ve thrown behind “GenAI”, in terms of compute, “talent”, and infrastructure bankrolled, cybercrime and cybersecurity have been the clear winners. Cybercrime benefits directly, while Cybersecurity gains job security.

Meanwhile, “agents” have achieved a new milestone, “Life of Brian” (Monty Python) levels of absurdity, just ask for a report with all of their vulnerabilities and the Crack Suicide Squad provides them.
