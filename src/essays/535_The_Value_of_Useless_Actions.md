While Miyamoto Musashi studied and honed the art of the sword, the lessons that he taught strongly highlight broadly applicable dynamics of strategy. For example, any time a domain benefits from preparing some kind of responses in advance, particularly when a school (or “best practices”) teaching a domain instructs its students to do so, then a set of pre-prepared actions become a meaningful subset of reactions within that domain.

One approach is to counter those actions themselves, which is perhaps the most common tactic. However, one of the strikes that Musashi taught was to spot the fraction of a second pause between an opponent’s movements, the moment where one pre-prepared action ends, before the next one begins. This is called the “Single Cadence Strike”.

Another of Musashi’s tactics was a variation upon this, with a feint intended to trigger such a predictable reaction, then striking at the moment that either the opponent relaxes or that reaction ends. This is called the “The Passing Cadence in Two Phases”.

This is a lesson on the importance of timing, and the opportunities offered by well-timed strikes, especially when a subset of an opponent’s strategies and reactions are known. This lesson also relates to modern concepts like “Zero-Day Vulnerabilities”, as well as MCP server attack exploits, and countless other aspects of Cybersecurity.

The broader lesson of striking with precision, timing, and tactics is acutely potent with respect to any genuinely new capabilities, moves that have never been seen before, or that simply weren’t possible before. With the introduction of more genuinely intelligent software (not to be confused with “agentic” trash), systems capable of cumulative and continuous learning and adaptation, such new possible moves and adaptations tend to outnumber those that came before.

Those adaptations also include pivots that simply weren’t possible previously, like infiltrating a network, spotting previously unknown kinds of vulnerabilities, and writing new bespoke code to exploit them from inside that network, all while operating at machine speeds and potentially running silent in communication terms. This focus on timing in Cybersecurity strategy can exploit a well-timed opening to infiltrate a system, which doesn’t immediately result in victory, but does breach an opponent’s defences, leaving them far more vulnerable to subsequent strikes.

In any domain, humans have both the learned reactions and configurations for defense, like “best practices”, and the systematic influence of cognitive biases, the predictable ways that individuals and groups fail to apply their higher cognition to a task or circumstance. Both individually can produce vulnerabilities, made worse when compounded.

These flaws are often made worse through mechanisms of traditional “automation”, as well as more modern statistical AI. They lack higher cognition and real grounding, usually relying on the weights in superposition of a neural network, a structure that is perpetually vulnerable to any skilled opponent.

Such forms of automation and statistical AI (like LLMs or their “agent” derivatives) can pose risks to even a skilled opponent if they are laid out as silent traps, like a strong anomaly detection system that waits silently, spread across the vulnerable spaces of a given network or system. This is an example of why care should be taken to check for traps, honey pots, anomaly detection, or any other variety.

The origin, code contents, and timing of breaching an enemy network all matter, as do the risks of such traps. For example:

-   If intrusion occurs and a trap is triggered, any intruding code might be picked apart, and the origin targeted by a counterattack.
    
-   The trap might also be triggered on a slight delay, such as systems with a human-in-the-loop, or periodic processing for anomaly detection (such as once per day automatic audits). If this is the case, then precise timing becomes critically important.
    
-   If the intruding code contains any secret information or Globally Unique Identifiers (GUIDs), then even a decoy point of origin could fail to prevent a counterattack. The possibility of all such data falling into the hands of the bad actor defending must be considered as a calculated risk in advance.
    
      
    \*Note, if algorithmic defenses may be triggered in such a way that the triggers are classified as false positives, a “The Boy Who Cried Wolf” effect (aka “Alert Fatigue”) may be triggered, causing a real subsequent intrusion to be ignored.
    

Even an unskilled bad actor may get lucky when defending against a superior force, especially if these are neglected.  

“The Important thing in strategy is to suppress the enemy’s useful actions, but allow his useless actions.” – Miyamoto Musashi
