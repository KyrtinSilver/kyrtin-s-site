The big consumer-facing pitch for much of “GenAI” over the past several years could be summarized in the phrase: “Use simple natural language for UX! It’ll be easy and people love it!”

How those interactions actually look: “You are a diligent and meticulous assistant. You must produce an array of (x) with (y) metadata, for all cases where (z) is true. Make no mistakes or you’re fired. I’ll pay you $1,000 if you get it right.”

Absent severe brain damage or memory loss, people have generally come to recognize that the people treating these systems like coding, with ample knowledge and experience in real coding, are the ones actually making use of them to any reasonable effect. This demented version of coding includes tricks like threats, bribery, the (unstable) use of key phrases, and more traditional pseudo-code descriptions.

At the same time, infamous fraudsters like the guy who first coined the term “vibe coding” (Karpathy) are publicly admitting that they’ve grown disillusioned with it, recognizing that their own skills have (strongly) degraded, as they abandon “vibe coding”.

Behind closed doors, the pitch for the same AI is often oriented around producing more code, or laying off entire customer service departments, with both equally absurd, and severely detrimental in different ways. In coding the systems introduce more, harder to spot, and increasingly severe Cybersecurity vulnerabilities, as noted in numerous examples among my posts.

In customer service attempts to use that same “GenAI”, you frequently encounter a hard disconnect between technical implementation and customer UX. For example, the technical people recognize that so long as it runs an unspecialized model via API that you can’t leave such a system accessible to anyone, or else it will be abused and run up a bill, as it is vulnerable-by-design technology.

However, it can’t serve a large number of customer service functions if a login is required to first access it, such as the all-too-common account problems centered on logins. This places the resulting system directly in conflict with its stated purpose, as it prevents customers from reaching “customer service” at the architectural level of implementation, meaning that the model’s performance or lack thereof doesn’t matter at all, since neither it nor any human customer service may be accessed by those who need it.

Remember all of those increasingly severe and obscured Cybersecurity vulnerabilities being produced by “GenAI”? Guess how those will impact the volume of customer service tickets hitting the above wall of nightmarishly awful customer experiences.

Those kinds of spectacularly awful experiences tend to dominate the memory of a brand for many years thereafter, and they are only the tip of the iceberg for the fallout caused by Cybersecurity failures. Most of the damage tends to hit peoples’ lives far outside of the scope of the company at fault for them.

Both the public-facing “Natural Language UX” pitch and the private “Coding and Customer Service” pitch are absurd to the extreme, but the people pitching them remain far worse.
