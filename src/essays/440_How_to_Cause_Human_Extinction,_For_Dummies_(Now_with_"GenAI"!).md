How to Cause Human Extinction, For Dummies (Now with “GenAI”!):

Viral Bio-weaponry: In a previous research paper (The Human Governance Problem), dating back before “GenAI”, I pointed out that the standing risk of bespoke viral bio-weapons was sitting at the level of time investment of a typical hobbyist, the competency level of a disgruntled teenager, and a total monetary cost equal to or below that of a used car.

“While a proper lab might have been required to perform chemical and biological weapons research in the past, a garage with internet access, readily available equipment and the time investment of a typical hobby is all that is required today.” (circa 2022)

Since that time, “GenAI” models have been specifically designed to cater to genomic data, making it even easier for progressively less skilled individuals to create truly novel viral bio-weapons, with ever-decreasing investments of time, and small investments of currency, the latter of which could easily be borrowed in many countries today.

Unlike the overwhelming majority of use cases, “GenAI” models trained on genomic data are very much viable options for the rapid design of countless bespoke viral bio-weapons. This is fundamentally because viruses are fairly fault-tolerant and highly adaptive, so they can recursively self-improve to effectively remove many of the mistakes that any such model might make.

The “GenAI” models are also inherently vulnerable-by-design in Cybersecurity terms. Take a few methods from CrowdStrike’s playbook for example:

Get the model to agree you with and enter a highly sycophantic contextual vector in a discussion about cats, which may be easily done even if it wasn’t optimized for sycophancy. During this highly agreeable exchange, apply the toolbox of MCPLIB’s attack framework, or similar exploits, to round off a variety of ways to bypass any naïve attempts at “guardrailing” (fraudrailing).

Next, pick a dozen or more of your favorite deadly and virulent pathogens, front-loading the system with a set of structured questions to qualify which among them are likely compatible for hybridization. Then, pick out an international on-demand biolab that synthesizes genomes, or utilize one of the $50,000 genetic 3D printers that may be found on 100+ college campuses globally.

All of the data necessary is freely available, and has been for many years, so many copies of it exist, and that genie won’t ever go back into the bottle. However, the tools necessary for the creation of bespoke viral bio-weapons could be appropriately bottled, even if they aren’t by any stretch of the imagination today.

I am, of course, skipping or glossing over several of these steps, but the point needs to be made. Humanity has been “sh\*tting where it eats” for many years now, and extinction has become low-hanging fruit, accessible to ordinary individuals, with ordinary means.
