Recently, in my work on the OSS project (pending a catchy name), I came across some of the recent figures on Digital Nomadism that paint a telling story about the US in particular.

“In 2023, there were 17.3 million American digital nomads, which was a 131% increase since 2019, and the number increased to 18.1 million in 2024.” (~35 million total global digital nomads as of 2023)

The US’s total population is only an estimated ~349 million people, so that means that nearly 5% of the US population has successfully left that s\*\*thole of a country behind, myself included. The “successfully” part of that is also critically important, as many, if not most, people in the US never have any feasible option to leave the country behind, regardless of whether or not they’d choose to. (Digital Nomads also tend to be SIGNIFICANTLY happier with life, 81%, compared to the 49% high satisfaction of stagnant populations in the US)

Consider all of the steep barriers to leaving it behind:

• US legal mechanisms (barriers to exit) designed to complicate, entrap, or extort, such as the blatantly absurd attempt to tax income from US citizens who neither live nor work in the US.

• The equally absurd barriers to entry that virtually every country in the world appears to adopt, to varying degrees of Barbarism and Bureaucratic corruption. These takes a wide variety of utterly nonsensical forms, the only common denominator being the nonsensical nature itself.

• Financial mobility, requiring a baseline of funds necessary to compensate for the steep increases in uncertainty and safety net pressures that come with relocation to another country.

• The ability to either work remotely, or secure an appropriate job in another country, or even on another continent. This itself tends to require both the individual and the employer to jump through numerous, absurd, and completely pointless flaming bureaucratic hoops.

• The practical reduction of literal baggage, going against consumerism and hoarding behaviors to significantly reduce physical baggage, thus increasing mobility.

Again, nearly 5% of the US population successfully passed all of these barriers to successfully GTFO of the country, circa 2024, with a steady increase from year to year. The US has been a sinking ship for many years, with more recent times also setting it on fire.

It would likely be overly generous to estimate that even 10% of the US population has ever received a decent opportunity to work remotely or relocate to other countries, along with the necessary financial mobility. Assuming that 10% of the population did receive that opportunity, that would mean that, given the chance, 50% of US citizens able to do so would leave the country behind.

As anyone who has tried to secure a job in another state of the US than where one lives has likely found out, many places simply don’t hire people from a distance, no matter the effort put into the hunt. Many, if not most, of the places who do hire from a distance aim to send you to places where you really don’t want to live. Those are only in-country moves across state lines, a tiny fraction of the hassle that is baked into relocation to other countries.

5% of a country’s population leaving it behind under the circumstances is like if 5% of the UK swam across the English Channel to live in northern France. Next to the US, no other country has even ¼ as many people fleeing it to become digital nomads.
