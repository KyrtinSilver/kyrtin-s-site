My appreciation of books is usually directed at research-oriented literature, from the rather small number of scientists who get books published. This leaves gaps in my reading schedule, often filled with works of fiction (beyond the classics) that can provide ample entertainment, immersion, and provoke their own share of thoughts.

One excellent example is “The Quantum Thief” trilogy, by Hannu Rajaniemi, a Finnish biotechnology entrepreneur with a PhD in mathematics. He introduces a lot of pretty advanced scientific concepts into his writing, which can make for a welcome change of pace and imagination. (Just run screaming in the other direction if he tries to write about “Generative AI” again)

Another is the “Murderbot Diaries” series by Martha Wells, which despite the name (which turned me off from reading it for a few years) isn’t really about murderous robots. The dialogue is quite simply amazing, though the stories are fairly short, so you’ll likely find yourself re-reading them.

The most extensive high quality work that I’ve found came from Matt Dinniman, the “Dungeon Crawler Carl” series, which combines fantasy, science fiction, and comedy all in one. By the time I got around to reading the first of this series the 7th book had just come out, and I quickly plowed through all of them, which are especially good in audiobook format.

All are books that I’ve personally read more than once.

Sadly, platforms like Audible (the platforms themselves) remain irredeemable trash, as they don’t allow you to block recommendations for utter garbage, like the writing of Brian Herbert, Robert Greene, J.K. Rowling, or Steven Pinker, just to name a few of their worst offences today. You also see this particular inexcusable offence on platforms like Spotify, where a simple “block (track, artist, book, podcast, etc.)” function could make all of the difference.

Instead, everyone is left to expect Idiocracy-level misalignment to reality from the platform’s recommendations. The recommender algorithm is so bad that I came to expect that it could only recommend total crap, with Dungeon Crawler Carl proving to be the exception to the rule.

More social platforms, like the obvious one for this post, commit the same crimes via “fraudvertising”, such as fraudvertisements for OpenAI. Court cases are now cropping up with regard to a different platform, Meta, and their own revenue stream from overtly criminal sources, so some attention is starting to be paid to this problem.

That aside, while the platforms may be hot garbage, there are some good books (and audiobooks) worth diving into among the many works of fiction that they host. Whether for Xmas or the Lunar New Year, I can recommend taking breaks from family to enjoy a good book of fiction. That can be a better investment in sanity than any food or drink one might consume.

\*Also be warned, if you go with the audiobooks, try to make sure that the same narrator handled all books in a series, as the pronunciation of names can vary painfully between different narrators.
