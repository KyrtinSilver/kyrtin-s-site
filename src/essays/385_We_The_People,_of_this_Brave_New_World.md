Human Rights and Freedoms have been the focal point of countless works enshrined in the memory of large populations around the world. Across centuries and millennia, wars have been fought, and dictators overthrown, to see new rights and freedoms delivered to the people.

Technological acceleration has greatly increased the rate at which new rights and freedoms are required to avoid the countless variations of Dystopia that fiction has imagined, and many that it has not. Still other “Anti-Rights”, lingering vestiges of humanity’s various long-debunked forms of barbarism, have remained entrenched, directly preventing a variety of basic rights and freedoms from being delivered.

My latest research paper has focused on covering this subject, currently in pre-print and receiving feedback. One of my peers reviewing this work has already said of it:

“...(I) noted it as being one of your most accessible stand-alone works to date. Possibly also one of your most important for any one person to read as well, though I suppose that depends on how versed the person is in your prior work.”

That reviewer also turned the entire paper into a short audiobook (AI-generated narration, but of pretty good quality). Given that the narrated copy is an hour and 46 minutes long, I suspect that may prove far more accessible than reading it in text for most people.

As noted in my last post, the default reverence for the various moral and cultural concepts that some hold “sacred” or “taboo” have been systematically stripped away from the paper, by design, as they are a particular form of cognitive biases. This may predictably increase the cognitive bias of many who read or listen to it via knee-jerk reactions as those sacred and taboo concepts are unapologetically flattened, but it does serve to reduce the cognitive bias of the paper itself, and they’ll get over it.

Even the most “civilized” countries in the world today fall far short of even the rights and freedoms that are already both highly visible and urgently required. While I’m tempted to add further discussion on numerous points relating to these rights and freedoms, it will be a sufficient accomplishment if people read the over 18,000 words or 1 hour and 46 minutes of audio of the current form. I still have no intention of moving from long research papers to books, even skirting the limits of it.

I look forward to further feedback, even given the predictable subset of knee-jerk reactions. I also encourage others to expand on the discussion of those rights and freedoms that they care about and see particular opportunities to advance and/or study, publishing papers of their own.

As I’ve pointed out before, you don’t need a PhD to write a paper, you just need to apply the methodologies that one is taught to follow.
