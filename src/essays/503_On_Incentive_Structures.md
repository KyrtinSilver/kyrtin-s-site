Incentive Structures are one of the clearest methods of understanding a system, studying the dynamics motivating action and adaptation. For example, when two forces exist within a system, one focused on the carrot (positive, reward), another on the stick (negative, consequence), but neither infringing upon the other, then the separation between them is likely mere illusion.

The phrase “carrot and stick” itself emphasizes that these are complimentary facets of a single potentially balanced incentive structure. Examples of these operate both passively and actively all around us, every day, from the passive algorithmic manipulations governing the spread of information (or misinformation) across social media, to law enforcement activities against more openly violent events.

Demonstrably obscene and darkly comical examples of malignant incentive structures have also gained notoriety over the past few decades, such as the famous “Peter Principle”, which remains alive and well despite now being older than most who embody it today. Another even larger part of the Cancer upon society’s incentive structures is highlighted in David Graeber’s famous work “Bullshit Jobs”, which should probably be required reading in every school.

Incentive Structures dictate how a system either grows and develops, or decays and collapses, as no matter the starting point, any system with malignant incentives will favor decay and collapse. No matter how well or poorly the rest of a given system is architected, the incentive structure has the final say in what happens to it over time. Systems with poorer starting points but better incentive structures can easily surpass those with the inverse over time.

Another opportunity that Incentive Structures offer is that their influences may be measured and compared to the claims of any parties tied to them, providing a partial litmus test for corruption. For example, any polarized system where two parties allegedly pit themselves against the other, while both serve a third purpose that neither of the two openly voices, is actually a single system posing as two opposing parties. The US political system’s historic pendulum (pre-Trump) is one such example.

A rather comical example of this is found in some religion, where an afterlife is split into the typical carrot and stick of reward or consequence, and some force is assigned to each, treated as separate, despite being two halves of the same unified system. This becomes even more comical when one half is treated as an all-powerful monotheism, from which endless variations upon cognitive dissonance may spring.

Narratives drive many of these forms of cognitive dissonance, and as research has demonstrated, there is no difference between religion, politics, or any other form of belief within the human brain. The same neurons fire. However, narratives are themselves also a part of the incentive structure, as humans are highly motivated by narratives, especially when those narratives are internalized.

For example, when people internalize narrative elements, they become a part of that person’s identity, which may take a purely individual form, or one that indicates their standing within a social group. Incentive Structures that tap into such internalized narrative elements have an exceedingly high aptitude for short-cutting many higher cognitive processes, leading to frequent and highly visible hijacking and exploitation.
