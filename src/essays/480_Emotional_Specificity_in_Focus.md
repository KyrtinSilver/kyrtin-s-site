One of the common simplifications when describing emotions is neglecting to designate the focal point of an emotion, such as “being angry” specifically meaning “angry at X”, “angry at myself”, or “angry at both”, and even “angry at both, but more angry at (myself or X)”.

This is a structural flaw in emotion-based systems that don’t differentiate by dividing the two across graph structure. Some structure is required to separate each possibility from the others, which can take the form of of-type relationships within a graph, emotion concepts that specify self, other, or mixed focal points within them, or any combination of the two.

End-to-end approaches and naïve statistics aren’t your friend in this problem space. That would be similar to using the average depth of a river and neglecting where the fast water and sharp rocks are within it.

Each category of focus or mixtures of the two has distinctly different prescriptive needs and solutions attached to it, and this difference predictably becomes more acute as those emotion concepts grow in complexity and branch out combinatorially.

For example, take that great and common human pursuit to find a long-term romantic partner. It is certainly complex, comprised of trust, curiosity, security, comfort, compassion, empathy, and a number of other complimentary motivational factors, with the influence of just as many negative variations upon the same.

It certainly grows more acute with this explosive increase in complexity and subsequent specificity, and so too the need for greater specificity in communication grows in step. Anyone to hold a healthy relationship for any duration of time has learned the importance of specificity in communication.

When people talk about their systems performing “emotion recognition” in AI, the safe bet is that they’re incompetent, and probably criminally liable. Lisa Feldman Barrett’s research, alongside many others, demonstrate that there are no “emotional fingerprints” to perform such recognition, as the patterns of emotions change dramatically between different people and cultures. This problem is further compounded by the issue above, the failure to designate the focal point(s) of each emotion present at each time step.

As noted in a previous post, “emotional granularity” (Barrett) is the vocabulary of motivational concepts, a kind of constructed categorization process. When the size, contents, and definitions of each motivational concept within such a vocabulary vary at the individual level, then it should come as no surprise at all that “emotion recognition” can only work given focused knowledge of a specific individual.

The next time someone demonstrates this particular failure and attempts to market “emotion recognition” to you, I recommend an educational exercise, where you teach them the importance of focal points and emotional granularity, by promptly punching them in the face.
