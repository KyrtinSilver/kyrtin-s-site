Much as the highest tier of products and services are often never sold, the highest tier of “talent” rarely opts to sell themselves.

While the relationship may only be a strong correlation, the top tier of talent tends to cherish freedom, which happens to be diametrically opposed to turning oneself into a “commodity”. These two things can be held in the cognitive dissonance of a single mind, but the number of such contradictions that a mind can cope with remain finite, each exacting a cost that no sum of money can mitigate. For some subset a value in trade may be roughly “equivalent”, but only as it mitigates some other equally harmful problem.

Some prior research, discussed in a popular TED talk (Emily Esfahani Smith: There’s more to life than being happy), categorized the strongest 4 sources of emotional motivation as “Pillars”, dividing them into:

1\. A Sense of Purpose: The feeling of having one’s own path, where each step along it carries meaning.

2\. A Sense of Belonging: The sense of community, security, and support that any social species requires.

3\. Storytelling: The narrative that weaves it all together in a form that may be easily remembered, refined, and added to. This pulls together both internalized (identity) and external elements.

4\. And lastly, Transcendence: The feeling of being a part of something greater than oneself, similar to a refined mix of the other 3 as a single complete system.

These are things that most companies, even or especially those that pay the most, rarely offer more than the weakest semblances of. There is even a culturally ingrained concept in Western societies, where any activity that is meaningful or fulfilling in these ways is considered something that people shouldn’t be paid to do. Instead, “work” is directly associated with suffering, as a transactional kind of Sadism.

Why would the top talent in any domain choose to subject themselves to such Transactional Sadism? Excluding the truly Masochistic few, they generally don’t, short of existential risks.

Wars for freedom have been fought across human history, and the top talents don’t surrender such precious and hard-won grounds.

Over time, as Population Dynamics play out across global society, a decreasing number of people resist the pull of that Transactional Sadism (most of which are themselves “Bullshit Jobs”, as Graeber puts it), but as the number of survivors decreases, the value of each survivor increases no less than proportionately. Rather, they tend to increase more than proportionately, due to finite constraints where the need for those talents and urgency compound.

These are not matters of philosophy, but rather the observable system dynamics that flow around us and across global society. Few sweeping predictions may be made of them, but many localized fallacies are easily debunked by them.
