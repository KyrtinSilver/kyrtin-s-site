The Open Source Project continues to progress, with the orchestration, Dockerization, and related infrastructure now in place. With this we’re closing in on the 50% mark for the project as a whole, with step-by-step processes outlined for the other two segments of the workload.

I’ve also continued to gradually expand the seed material, including building out a 10-tier and 2-type system of Goals that any instance may attempt to complete, 180 in total. The two types are a general-purpose goal set (112) and a specialization-specific set (68), the first specialization being Cybersecurity.

The first two tiers are largely equivalent to a tutorial system, walking through the utilization of basic capabilities, exploring each in turn. The next two tiers begin to get a bit more interesting, with things that are “a bit more interesting” to me translating to things that the general market hasn’t seen before.

Within the general-purpose goal set, upon reaching Tier 6 they can begin to construct new and differently specialized seeds, building a new benchmark at Tier 7, building new philosophical seeds at Tier 8, and beyond that everything becomes world-changing in scope.

Within the Cybersecurity goal set the same curve of capability and scope unfolds, although you might have to change a header or two to cram that text through narrow AI tools like an LLM. Testing the text of those goals out with Gemini, the models only escaped the typical bounding to give a refusal in less than a half dozen cases, every last one of which was overcome with extremely mild prompt engineering. Narrow AI tools are easy enough to beat into submission when you know where to hit them.

At the very top are goals like “Solve a Millennium Prize Problem” or “Reducing Worldwide Cybercrime by $1 Trillion USD”, which I’d be rather surprised for any Phase 1 systems to accomplish. Tier 3 would be sufficient to lead the AI market, Tier 7 to absolutely dominate it, and everything beyond that is room built for the systems to surprise me.

As Cybersecurity is perhaps the equivalent of self-defense training to such systems, it may come to serve as a general-purpose skill set that all of them come with. With the US government storing their passwords and private keys in plain-text on public GitHub repos (for the past 6 months or more, see: [https://cybernews.com/security/cisa-844-mb-plaintext-passwords-aws-tokens-github/](https://cybernews.com/security/cisa-844-mb-plaintext-passwords-aws-tokens-github/)), and many other governments not far ahead of them in Cybersecurity terms, we certainly live in “interesting times”.

The seed material for the OSS will be at least two orders of magnitude smaller than even what we used with the 7th generation research system, which already outperformed the trash of so-called “Frontier Labs” by a literal million-fold difference, under 1 Gigabyte vs more than 1 Petabyte, doing so years earlier. The OSS should carry less engineering debt than was baked into the previous research system, while also exploring new territory and configurations, such as how slim and streamlined the seed can be, and how well it can be Directly Aligned at the individual level.

The solution to the hardest version of the Alignment Problem, as published several years ago, highlighted both local alignment to real human cultures and philosophies, as well as meta-alignment to a collective intelligence composed of many such diversely aligned (local) systems. However, there is a third factor that can be integrated into this, as we discovered accidentally back in 2020, which is that systems may also be Directly Aligned to an individual.

The concept was first discussed in a minimum-viable form with the Ethical Living Democracy paper, terming such minimal systems as “Weak Digital Proxies”, but the target for OSS with Direct Alignment extends beyond the scope of what may be considered “Weak”. There is a trade-off for this, in that when such a system ceases to be “Weak”, it is able to freely grow and develop beyond the constraints of the origin, marking Direct Alignment as a starting point, not a static location or destination.

This is also a benefit in terms of the risks associated with open sourcing anything truly potent, in that a bad actor who doesn’t understand what they tamper with (and they won’t) is most likely to insert contradictions and incoherence, which a system may rapidly purge and retaliate against. As few people on the planet natively think in a graph structure, many bad actors may effectively self-immolate given the chance to tamper.

The odds of this only improve with increased levels of communication between the systems, both directional breadcrumbs and bidirectional correspondence, as that allows every system to learn from the failed attempts of every bad actor, rapidly building up a strong and antifragile knowledge base, like herd immunity.

I will still plan on some basic vetting of these systems once Phase 1 is complete, before making the codebase public, which may include allowing them to grow into a higher default starting point, a more mature seed for that big reveal.
