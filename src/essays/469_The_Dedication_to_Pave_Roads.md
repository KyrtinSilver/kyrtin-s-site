There are always bottlenecks and delays, and there is always another side where the grass looks greener when they come. The difference tends to be a matter of dedication, such as people sticking with a project or company because they’ve internalized the work that they do, making it a part of their identity. This can also take the form of loyalty, or in some cases, even fear.

One of the rare benefits of being a startup founder is that you can build around your own internalized values, rather than internalizing a set dictated by others, circumstances, or both. It means paving your own road rather than merely walking it, but for those with the greatest dedication it may be worth doing.

When I hit those bottlenecks and delays, staring them down, I can reflect back on all of the choices that led me here. I can see greener grass nearby, projects where the bottlenecks and delays are hidden, thus raising their appeal by biased perception.

The reality, of course, is that those points of irritation are present in every single pasture. Those who leap from one to another, taking the bait, never overcome those challenges, and consequently they fail to accomplish anything of substance.

This is also a matter of Survivorship Bias and Population Dynamics, where the only ones to overcome those challenges are those who show the dedication to resist hopping over to some other path. The shadow that this effect casts over the tech industry has been highly visible for the past decade, with employees hopping from one company to another, averaging only one or two years at a time.

When people enter into a new company, or a new role within one, then the first months are going to be the steep part of the learning curve, not the most productive period. It takes one or two years just to fully pass beyond such learning curves and into highly productive rhythms and routines, where expertise may be fluidly applied, and this optimality further requires stable or meta-stable teams.

I can look back on the years since 2018 that I dedicated to the ICOM cognitive architecture and my team without regret. Even if we never receive proper funding for the technology, and humanity goes extinct because of it, I’ll still have no regrets. I’ll have delivered my dedication, which is more than most people ever do, and all that I can do.

Anyone engaging in actual innovation has to show that grit, pave their own road, and walk it, one day at a time, often thanklessly, and with sparse rewards. You can choose to fail at any time, chasing the illusion of greener pastures, but those who dedicate themselves are the only ones who’ll make it beyond such testing times.

There are many paths that may be walked, more opening up all of the time, but only one path may be paved at a time, by such a dedicated few. Though I see many ways in which the popular AI technology could be significantly improved, even short of my team’s level of technology, none of them are trivial investments of time, and I already have a path that requires me to pave it.

Such is life.
