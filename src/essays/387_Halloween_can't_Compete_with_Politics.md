These days Halloween seems a bit redundant, and watered-down, since the most horrifying things you can come across are generally news headlines on the subject of current events in politics among certain countries, like the US, even when you’re 12 time zones away from it.

The more I read, the clearer it becomes that writing the paper on Human Rights and Freedoms, the role of government, and the role of technology was well-timed. In it, I describe the mandate of government as follows:

“Very simply, the purpose of government is to secure, ensure, and facilitate the human rights and freedoms of all those who dwell therein. Every other function that a government serves is some derivative or supporting method or factor for that core driving force.”

People often realize when this mandate is being violated, such as recent attempts at country-level mass-murder via starvation and deprivation of healthcare on very obviously false pretenses, but most citizens tend to remain reactionary, narrow in their adaptations, poorly organized, completely devoid of strategy, and utterly enfeebled in the face of an entrenched adversary (with their collective complacency being the reason why that adversary was able to entrench in the first place).

Humanity has failed to learn the lessons of history, so those lessons now repeat once more. Technology has changed, and with it the means of manipulation, but all of the old patterns are still there.

I’m still welcoming feedback on the paper, though I’m planning on not adding any new sections to it, just a paragraph here or there as needed. The full-text pre-print is here: https://www.researchgate.net/publication/396891066\_We\_The\_People\_of\_this\_Brave\_New\_World\_The\_Freedoms\_that\_we\_don’t\_have\_but\_could\_and\_the\_role\_of\_technology

My first reviewer turned it into an audio format, 1 hour and 46 minutes in narrated length, found here: https://kyrtin.substack.com/p/we-the-people-of-this-brave-new-world?r=61e3oh

Without action, nothing changes for the better, as nobody is coming to save you from your own apathy, least of all at the scale of a country. Think about your own rights and freedoms, those that you have, and those that are withheld from you. Think about what you can do to change that, not just for yourself, but for those around you, for your children, or even for all people, everywhere.

Think about the methods at your disposal, ones that you accept, ones that you reject, if your reasons for rejecting them are valid, and circumstances that overcome those rejections. If you can’t think of any such circumstances, then you aren’t truly thinking for yourself at all, and so long as that is true, perhaps you need no rights or freedoms at all. You can change that, with a hard look in the mirror at who you are, who you choose to become, and what drives that choice. Will you?
