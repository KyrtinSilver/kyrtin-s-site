One of the more blatantly absurd domains of public policy is that of taxation, due largely to both extreme and extremely obvious inconsistencies. In many, if not most, countries around the world it becomes easier to partially or fully avoid paying taxes the more that someone earns.

Of course, that places an increasing burden on those least able to carry it, as those making significantly more should be paying significantly more, not dodging and exploiting loopholes.

One of the major loopholes, which I’ve personally never heard any proposals for closing, takes the perverse form of tax exemption for “religious institutions”. It is perverse specifically because of a thing called “tithing” that people do to such organizations, a “donation” that takes a percentage of the individual’s income, then applies that money toward some purpose that they have some say in.

In other words, the “religious organization” extracts a kind of voluntary tax (subject to social pressures) from community members, but unlike governments it allows that money to be applied toward some purpose that those giving it actually care about. This can’t so much be called “separation of church and state” as “Church replacing state”, since the “religious organization” does what the government should have done, while also enjoying tax exemption.

This also means that those who join such religious groups have a distinct advantage, as they can far better control what their money goes on to fund. The average tax payer in the US funds war crimes and mass-murder in no uncertain terms, but writing off that tax burden with “donations” can reduce that ethical debt through diverting funds away from it.

I do place “religious organizations” in quotation marks, as there have also been no shortage of such entities that are very obviously for-profit, corrupt, and quite absurd in practice. The so-called “mega-churches” in the US south-east are one such example, with “priests” at many such churches raking in salaries that could compete with tech executives.

Given that research has shown how the same neurons in the human brain fire for both “politics” and “religion”, it should come as no surprised to see many of the same methods being used by bad actors within these domains. Both are simply different flavors of belief, and the two are frequently tied together, as “Separation of Church and State” tends to be more of an “aspirational” statement, divorced from the reality around us today.

A more modern version of “no taxation without representation” could easily take the form of ranking the things that any given individual cares about, and directing the taxes taken from them accordingly. In an age of abundant compute capacities, this is relatively trivial. Some expenses like electrical, water, and transit infrastructure are mandatory, but even for those expenses various updates and reforms could be prioritized.

Others types of expenses may have many distinctly different fundamental models, like healthcare and military, where the models being funded can fundamentally change how that branch of a government operates. The US has been on a “war posture” for many decades, but if that was placed on a tax preferences form, how many would have the stomach to tick that checkbox for mass-murder? Or in healthcare, how many would tick checkboxes for the Mandatory Gambling (on your health) via Health Insurance systems?

Even the simplest implementations of such a ranked-choice voting system for tax allocation could dramatically improve the utter absurdity of how such funds are spent today. People could vote with their wallets, and in doing so take direct responsibility for that which they choose to fund.
