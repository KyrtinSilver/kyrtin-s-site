In compiling all of my ~550 daily posts (and counting) while adding metadata to them, since this work began, I’ve been reminded of just how many more “Laws”, “Paradoxes”, and “Principles” (Dynamics) there are than people have yet assigned (or popularized) names for. Some of the common ones that you may (or probably should) be familiar with are:

-   Goodhart’s Law
    
-   Condorcet Paradox
    
-   The Pareto Principle
    
-   Law of the Instrument
    
-   Brandolini’s Law
    
-   Brooks’ Law
    
-   The Peter Principle
    
-   ...(plus about 100 more)
    

I suspect that while many more have been recognized, few among those recognized have been both formalized and popularized, as each of those additional steps may reduce the number passing it by another order of magnitude. While few among those that are recognized may reach such heights, each one that does tends to be vastly more significant than any “Law” of the legal domain, as each is rooted in system dynamics, unlike their legal domain counterparts.

In fact, a system that takes into account an extensive list of those known dynamics (laws, paradoxes, effects, and principles) could be far more useful in designing legal laws and structures than those attempting to naively enforce semantics. The US Supreme Court routinely behaves like a group of witch doctors tossing chicken bones into the air to “divine” the true intentions behind whatever semantic word-salad is placed before them. As they’ve been a Theocracy for some years now, this belief-based behavior should come as no surprise, but that particular deeply flawed method predates their more recent decline.

The dynamics and architecture of a system determine what it can and cannot do, which no amount of belief may circumvent. Many of these dynamics are commonly observed, and even easily remembered and pointed out once formalized and popularized, but they are also combinatorial. These dynamics are often \[some context(s) + some constraint(s)\] resulting in \[some systematic result\], such as the Peter Principle being:

\[workplace competence and incentives over time + population dynamics = employees systematically rising to their own levels of incompetence\]

This dynamic itself demonstrates the absence of a particular constraint, or multiple constraints, necessary for optimality, meaning that this Principle is a failure mode. The incentive is intended to reward loyalty and work, but it naively undermines this. There are many forms that reward may take, and constraints that increase benefits other than promotions that significantly change job responsibilities are often more optimal paths to explore.

Many of these dynamics are some form of failure mode, and those who don’t learn from them often come to embody them (perhaps reaching middle management in doing so).
