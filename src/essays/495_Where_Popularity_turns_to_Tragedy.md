Popularity itself holds an unpopular aspect, in that it serves as a weak and low-effort proxy for higher cognition, which predictably and systematically leads to poor results (and when allowed to compound via routine “cognitive offloading”, cognitive decline). Perhaps nowhere is this more visible today than in discussions of AI, particularly where terms like AGI, ASI, agents, world models, and other popular “beliefs” are invoked.

The three cats in a trench-coat of PR narratives standing on the shoulders of UX, who in turn stands on the shoulders of statistics are quite a sight once you know what to look for. They are designed and built to exploit the nebula of cognitive biases that encircle popularity, such as Survivorship Bias, Availability Heuristic, Attention Bias, Mood-congruent Memory Bias, Endowment Effect, Sunk Cost Fallacy, Peltzman Effect, Subjective Validation, Semmelweis Reflex, Anthropomorphism, and numerous others.

One method of (approximately) measuring the damage done on a daily basis, with respect to a single facet, is to contrast a post on any newsfeed-dependent platform that lacks the exploitation of rage-bait, click-bait, viral misinformation, or mentions of AI, with posts dedicated to each of the above. What you’re likely to find on most of the popular platforms is an exponential decrease in the spread of content when it fails to meet the criteria of a malevolent post (bait and misinformation) and/or also fails to point to AI.

I’ve personally observed this difference between AI-related and objectively more important posts to range from ~85,000 to ~500 in terms of how far a post spreads, the higher number favoring AI-related 170:1. This also remained true even when a post received considerably more comments and reactions per “impression”.

Beyond the bounds of social media platforms, this Heap of Popularity-related Cognitive Biases is also easily observed in a fallacy that many have faced, where the offending individual states something to the effect of “Well if that is true, then why hasn’t someone else already (…)”.

In lieu of encountering a formal name for this fallacy previously, we can call it the “Why hasn’t someone else? Fallacy”. The core problem is that popularity, not logic, is what it invokes. It creatives an infinite loop where everyone asks the same question, why someone else hasn’t, rather than applying higher cognition to answer the harder question, whether or not they should.

That Fallacy in turn creates a Tragedy of the Commons, as each subsequent individual passes on the same problem to someone else, “turtles all of the way down” into tragedy as it were. Somehow, every offending party finds it implausible that every prior person made the same egregious blunder, even as they repeat the offense.

When speaking of AI and investors in particular, you absolutely will run into this, in both positive and negative forms, and to staggering extremes. The same basic fallacy’s positive form is of course “Well if they (invested so much) then they must have actually (applied higher cognition)!”, which the world has seen on infinite repeat with (anti-justified) funding rounds and valuations for OpenAI and Anthropic, among others.

No one with the faintest shred of higher cognition could justify an investment in OpenAI, or any of the numerous and equally fraudulent spin-off companies that their former employees have produced, nor was it reasonable to do so at any point in the past 4 years. Instead of justification to higher cognition, such infinite fallacies are invoked, producing the glut of overtly criminal activities that we see in the AI domain today.

Question: “Why hasn’t someone else…?”

Answer: “Because the last person also chose not to actually think, and said the same thing.”
