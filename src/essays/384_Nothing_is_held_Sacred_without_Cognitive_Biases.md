There is a fairly simple, and utterly counter-intuitive test for detecting if a document of any length and breadth is still sugar-coated with a thick helping of Cognitive Biases (and/or the manipulations thereof). The shadow of this method can also be seen in the research of Jonathan Haidt, as he describes the tendency within Moral constructs to designate certain things as “sacred” and others as “taboo”.

“Sacred” concepts are subject to a variety of positive and reinforcing cognitive biases, while “taboo” concepts are discouraged from being discussed in the opposite ways, being negatively inclined and negatively reinforced. So, what does it look like when Cognitive Biases are stripped away from these socio-normative bounds for communication?

It looks like you driving a steamroller directly across the concepts that arbitrary moral systems designate as sacred and taboo alike, paying no substantial heed to either one.

Ironically, that may also be the best recipe for maximizing the Cognitive Biases of any perceiving this activity as it takes place, resulting in a predictably high supply of knee-jerk reactions.

A few years back my team learned that when you have a system that passes the Turing Test (back when people still cared about it) that the people interacting with it frequently jump to immediately assuming that they must actually be talking to a human, even though they were not. Success, as it turns out, is often received in the poorest possible terms.

Yes, sugar-coating the discussion and selectively omitting concepts in order to pander to an audience, publisher, peer reviewers, or a university board, are all methods of explicitly injecting Cognitive Biases into the work. These are things that grant statistical advantages, leading Survivorship Bias and Population Dynamics to favor those papers with more Cognitive Bias of these kinds present, not less. This effect is, of course, also significantly worse in peer-review, particularly within the domain of Cognitive Bias itself.

The same basic problem may also be found in the average company, and communication therein. Those who sugar-coat and selectively omit are more likely to rise in hierarchies, following the Peter Principle as they rise to their own level of incompetence.

An extreme example of where that activity may take you is the individual who at least 80% of the Associated Press’s “Fact Check” activity now appears to be dedicated to. In that case, a bad actor imitates candor, but the lie of that is made clearly visible by also imitating to hold things sacred or taboo.

If you want to demonstrate less Cognitive Bias, be prepared to p\*ss off a lot of people, as that is the predictable result of doing so. In fact, I fully expect the paper that I just finished drafting on Human Rights to demonstrate this.
