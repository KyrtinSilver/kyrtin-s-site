One of the curious examples of systematized mass-stupidity, as covered by Anthropologist David Graeber, was the “Myth of Barter”, a potent fantasy famously popularized by Adam Smith, which contributed significantly to the foundations of economics as a field. The funny thing was that even before Adam Smith’s time Native Americans were operating very functional collectives, stockpiling longhouses and distributing goods according to communal will and needs, using neither such a mythical system of “barter”, nor any traditional currency.

\*Note, although those collectives didn’t prove competitive with Europe in matters of warfare, leading to their downfall, that isn’t a fundamental limitation of such systems.

An even greater irony is that such collectives are obviously compatible with systems that use traditional currency, merely functioning as nested systems, where currency is utilized for interactions between the collective and outside parties, but goods and services are distributed by chosen means within the collective. This is endosymbiosis to a collective for any member of that group, with the collective potentially symbiotic to other groups through exchanges of currency.

Many similar examples may be found, where nested systems are presented as an option that is exclusive to something else, when in actually it is fully compatible with them when nested. In many cases, overcoming this particular blind spot may prove critical in reaching optimal solutions for the problems that they influence.

When a nested system is fully compatible with a system operating at a larger and/or broader scale, then it offers a means of localized specialization that doesn’t detract from the larger system. Many such localized specializations may drive further improvements to the larger system as a whole, moving it closer to global optimality.

A further irony of comparing currency-based systems and nested centralized (local) collective systems is that it is almost infinitely easier to tax the collective, due to the flow of goods following distribution, and all distribution being known. Within currency-based systems, only a subset of digital transactions are truly tracked, and physical currency simply doesn’t tell anyone where it has been unless it was specially marked.

Try as they might, even the most corrupt governments today fail to truly control physical currency. The only thing that they’re worse at taxing is the actual system that predated both collectives and currencies, systems of debt (Discussed at length in Graeber’s book, “Debt: The First 5,000 years”).

However, nested systems also exist in part because what works optimally at one scale doesn’t necessarily work equally well at a larger scale, or for that matter when applied to a different group of people or a different culture. Numerous attempts have been made to import policy decisions from one country to another, rather than dedicating any real thought and study toward the needs of the target country and their cultural differences, with predictably poor results.

Like nested systems, government policies can only be imported and slotted into place a fraction of the time, because what works for one group or culture isn’t a silver bullet to solve the problems of others. Each locally optimal system, where specialization takes place in any form, has some kind of niche within a larger ecology. A different ecology might not even have the same niche to fill.

\*While the US may be divided into “State” and “Federal” governments, States are only minimally nested, with extremely weak legal and financial self-regulative capacities for the State governments.

A further benefit of nested systems is that they can have their own policies, and the functions funded by taxes in larger systems can often be partially provided by such nested systems to their members, subsequently reducing the need to tax those members who draw less on the larger system’s services.

Nested systems with digital intelligence open up many “Blue Ocean Markets” of possibility as well, awaiting exploration.
