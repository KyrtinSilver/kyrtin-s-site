The Superman/Disney Fallacy:

A common, popular, and utterly bankrupt fallacy that has been pushed as ideology for many decades in pop culture is frequently connected to comic book superheroes and Disney main characters. This fallacy is itself an extension of Authority Bias, another equally bankrupt Cognitive Bias, which is in turn itself derivative of the drives for “cognitive frugality/offloading” more generally, leaving the cognitive labor to others whenever possible. Of course, those others also tend to try dodging the responsibility for such work whenever they can as well. It is evasion (and often corruption) all of the way down in that stack.

Within the Superman/Disney Fallacy a common trope plays out, where some criminal is caught, at which point the hero/main character promptly and prematurely defers all responsibility to someone else, despite their job being wholly incomplete. This is infamously why comic book villains kept returning in later books, because the hero never actually did their job in the first place.

In many of the more Disney-flavored versions of the fallacy, some random, backlash, and/or environmental effect deals with the villain, which is passively peddled as “karma”, while being wholly reliant on external forces to resolve the main character’s incompetence and unethical activities, such as half-\*ssing their one job.

This all begs the question, “What would this look like if ethical behavior replaced fallacy?”:

Let’s take the classic example of Superman and his infamous nemesis Lex Luthor. When he defeats and captures Lex Luthor then he is still wholly responsible for his choice of action. It is blatantly obvious that his nemesis will eventually escape, going on to do more terrible things. Knowing this, Superman has a choice:

1) He takes Lex Luthor into custody, and in-so-doing becomes directly guilty of every subsequent crime that Lex Luthor commits, as Superman is the one who was responsible for choosing not to put that path to an end.

2) Superman takes actual responsibility for his own actions, not being an overgrown man-child in tights, putting a conclusive end to Lex Luthor on the spot.

3) Using some other means available to him, he takes responsibility, while selecting a different punishment than simply death.

To this day, this particularly insidious fallacy still crops up with some regularity, and the actual “heroes” tend to be called “anti-heroes”, despite them being the only one to take any remotely ethical actions. The deeply unethical, heavily biased, and cognitively lazy path has been encouraged for a very long time now, with countless attempts to hammer it into children.

Not all children are so gullible as to fall for that BS. The ones who don’t may change the world, even as the rest do the cognitive equivalent of eating crayons.
