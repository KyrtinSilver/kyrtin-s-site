Many of the most impactful decisions in the lives of any given person you meet, ruling over their mental health and well-being for years or even an entire lifetime, share a few important things in common. They:

1.  Are usually made based on grossly incomplete information.  
      
    
2.  Don’t occur more than 1-10 times in an individual’s lifetime, limiting or even negating the benefits of experience.  
      
    
3.  Are subject to extreme levels of “Choice Overload”, where there are too many options and hyper-complexity in the variables needed to reach some truly optimal solution.  
      
    
4.  Have entire industries dedicated to exploiting people seeking to make these decisions, reducing the odds that most people will make wise decisions to very nearly zero, as someone else gains a financial benefit when they don’t.
    

For example, take the subject of the previous post, how increasing AI-induced mental illness is leading to dystopian scenarios where people claim to be dating chatbots. To do this, we must look at the thing they’re opting to avoid.

In typical dating, decisions about other potential partners are made under extremely incomplete (and often intentionally misleading) information.

Serious relationships are time-consuming and energy-intensive, making them rare in any individual’s lifetime.

Dating Apps have increased the level of Choice Overload in this domain to a point where entire countries may be searched, with the evaluation of any individual potential partner already hyper-complex.

The Dating industry and various parasitic industries attached to it exploit these problems very well, and as humans have fundamental emotional needs to be met, they always have demand in their favor.

In the short-term, the coping mechanism of escapism into AI chatbot “relationships” avoids this, but the damage festers and grows worse with time.  
  
This begs the question, what would it look like to help solve this problem?  
  
If you have a system with actual digital intelligence, then opportunities increase dramatically. You could verify the details that matter about each individual potential partner, without ever having to disclose private information on a profile. This means that you could have substantially more information factoring into the decision-making process, negating that Choice Overload, without publicly displaying any greater level of information than people are comfortable with.  
  
This can also be used to negate bad actors and parasitic industries, by spotting manipulation, scams, etc., and shutting them down. Many examples of “How to make (X) work for you!” are parasitic to unaddressed problems within an industry, and those can vanish as well.  
  
You could also have the benefit of the sum of human knowledge on relationships recognizing what you’re doing wrong, who you need to meet, and how you might go about improving it all. For such a system there is ample experience to draw upon, in addition to research knowledge, as it can run an entire platform.
