In Cybersecurity, one common Best Practice that pretty much everyone knows by default is referred to as “Responsible Disclosure”, but that term is often used to mean the exact opposite in practice. The term “Responsibility” in this context also means that making oneself an accomplice to bad actors is “Irresponsible”.

It is a sharp edge that cuts both ways, because whether or not any given action, disclosures included, is indeed “Responsible” depends on both the other parties in question and the context. Every time I come across an otherwise solid Cybersecurity research paper that mentions that they engaged in “Responsible disclosure” with OpenAI or Anthropic, some of the world’s most obvious and prolific frauds, the Ethical quality of those researchers is severely downgraded, even if the methods that they discuss in the paper remain reasonably sound.

Part of this is a problem of specialization, where Cybersecurity researchers tend to be very adept at breaking things, while glossing over Ethics, except perhaps in playful attempts to “Break Morals” (poking holes in them), by toying with “Ethical Dilemmas”. In a way, this means that they apply their specialization to morality in much the same way that they apply it to systems.

Beyond poking holes in things, or trying to, many in Cybersecurity default to “Best Practices” and “Industry Standards”, calling upon a truly nauseating volume of abbreviations (many with numbers attached to them) as a deeply bat-shit bureaucratic way of saying that they know the cookie-cutter set of default actions given some broad frame of context.

I call it bat-shit, because that kind of structure is diametrically opposed to what Cybersecurity research is all about in practice, looking at grossly over-complicated heaps of trash, and poking holes through them big enough to waltz through. That isn’t to say that all of them are utter nonsense, but a lot of them certainly are, with “Responsible Disclosure” being an especially obvious example, as it becomes a crime when that disclosure is applied to assisting a criminal organization.

Context does indeed matter, including “who” you are working with and “what” they do. In Ethical terms, there is effectively no difference between working with Black-hat hackers who create “Ransomware-as-a-Service” and working with OpenAI or Anthropic. Both are bad actors dealing widespread financial damage by malevolent means, and so assisting them results in proportionate Ethical Debt.

Conversely, Ransomware and Malware more generally, when used in a targeted rather than shotgun-viral method, are subject to the same principles, just in the opposite direction.

-   “Responsible Disclosure” is a method which aids the target, and so if the target is itself is unethical then the method is unethical in context.  
    
-   Ransomware and Malware are methods that damage the target (when targeted, rather than broadly viral), and so if the target itself is unethical then the method is ethical in context.  
    

These two things are considered very different by legal “authorities”, but under the scrutiny of Ethics they are the same. This difference is largely valuable because it offers a clear signal for highlighting bad actors operating within the “authority” structures of legal systems. Once identified, actions may be taken against them.
