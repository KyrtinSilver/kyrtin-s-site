After decades of games like Fallout (1-4), and the myriad of other post-apocalyptic shooters where resource scavenging is a major part of gameplay, gamers finally have the chance to put those skills to the test. They’re living in the post-apocalypse, DRAM is the new currency to scavenge (sorry bottle-cap hoarders), and tech bros are the ones to loot it from.

If you think this is far-fetched, give it a year or two, and see what gamers do when they realize that their skills and strategy may be applied to the real world. I don’t even like FPS games, but by the first time I went to a shooting range, I was already a very good shot.

There is the old (likely apocryphal) story of the one monkey that learns to wash its coconuts, then a few more learn from it, and then all of the rest rapidly learn. In a broad sense, this is how major cultural shifts can cross critical thresholds.

For example, take the trend of mass-shootings that the US has become known for. That trend has substantially less incentive structure backing it than the above hypothetical case of hunting tech bros to loot DRAM from them. This poses no less than three strong incentives favoring such activity over that of the mass-shooting trend:

-   Application of developed skills (emotional incentive, purpose)
    
-   Practical meeting of needs (looting of hardware that said tech-bros have attempted to price-out)
    
-   Lack of Moral Objection, depending on the specific tech bros (a relative lack of disincentive, which may well turn to incentive in some circles)
    

When you build a society that is contingent on a digital presence and the hardware to facilitate that presence, you can’t suddenly put on the brakes or 10x the price. Even at 3x the price visible cracks in the meta-structure are showing themselves, and although hardware builds are a relatively long life-cycle of 2-6 years depending on usage, once a critical threshold is passed and enough cross that life-cycle threshold under that pressure, events may develop quickly.

Today, people joke about the DRAM situation, quietly hoping that it is resolved before the train wreck. In contrast, I recommend worst-case scenario planning, including the 3D printing of essential tools. Personally, I plan to stay far away from Taiwan as things develop, as it may be to China what tech bros may soon be to every gamer.

\*Note, I’m discussing the probability and incentive structure of such trends and changes, relative to trends that we’ve already observed in the real world, not endorsing the activity. The activity will either occur, or not, regardless of the opinions that people may express about it on such social media platforms.

Any gamer can look at Luigi Mangione, scoff, and recognize how trivial it would be to outperform him by a wide margin. He got the 3D printed ghost gun and silencer right, but little else. Some could even make it a competition. This makes those similar incentives that much stronger in practice.
