Recently, I had a 2-hour coffee with an Australian gentleman who has lived 9 months per year in Vietnam for some years now, and we reminisced about all of the cultural differences between places like the US, Australia, and Vietnam. Combined with my recent reading of “Bullshit Jobs”, and prior research, this helped snap a realization into place.

The US and Australia both have SEVERE drug problems, as well as homelessness and associated substance abuses (like alcohol, nicotine, etc.), and crime more generally, found most acutely in any major city. As we sat in the “Wall Street of Vietnam”, District 1 of Ho Chi Minh City (aka Saigon), we recognized that these problems were virtually absent in Vietnam.

(\*The exception being specific locations that Russians often flee to, in order to avoid being drafted, where cigarettes are heavily consumed)

To get one common delusion out of the way, this is no product of the “Wonders of Communism”, as just like Democracy, true Communism doesn’t exist, and indeed it couldn’t technically exist without a certain technology being adopted, just like Democracy, or Socialism for that matter. Rather, this is largely derivative of differences in culture.

Jonathan Haidt’s body of research plays a role in this, as he highlights the fundamental differences between “individualistic” and “communal” cultures, with the US, UK, and Australia being the former, many “Eastern” cultures in Asia being the latter, and many others falling somewhere in between. These set basic fundamental expectations on every individual, and how they relate to those around them.

One thing that I puzzled over for quite some time was how I see private security officers all over the place in Vietnam, but never a trace of crime. In contrast, I saw plenty of police and security presence in places across the US, where crime ran rampant and largely unchecked. I came to refer to the Seattle City Council as a drug Cartel, though policing practices in the more conservative parts of the US (such as “The South”) were no better.

As others in Cognitive Bias research discovered, expectations are pivotal in understanding this. One example was a study focused on the problem of theft from a Petrified National Forest, where different signs warning against theft were tested. What did they find? That signs telling people not to steal actually increased theft if they were worded to set the expectation that many people steal, but they decreased theft if they set the opposite expectation.

These same differences in expectation, particularly when enshrined in fundamental culture, can have the same effect that we’ve observed on drug abuse, crime, homelessness, and many similar problems. These issues are not truly “problems unto themselves”, but they are rather a byproduct of incompetently (and often malevolently) aligned expectations. Even without anything resembling Universal Basic Income, and with far lower wealth overall, places with a more communal culture fair far better than over-hyped dumpster fires like the US.

Cooperation requires such a sense of communal good, and where it is absent, these problems flourish.
