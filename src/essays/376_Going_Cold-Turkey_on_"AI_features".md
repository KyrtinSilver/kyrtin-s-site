In recent discussions of healthcare, migraine prevention, and treatment, I was reminded of a test I performed back when I was still a teenager, more than two decades ago. I was taking more than a half dozen different “migraine prevention” medications, and without telling anyone, I stopped taking all of them cold-turkey one day. Three months later my parents asked me what had changed that my health had improved so much, and I said “I stopped taking all of that crap three months ago. That is what changed.”

They couldn’t argue with results.

The reason that I mention this today, is that we’ve probably come to that point with “AI integrations” as well. All of those AI “features” that were intended as “improvements” for UX have piled up, the damage of them compounding one against the other, increasing the detriment to a company’s healthy UX.

Set up your experimental conditions, take your “before” metrics, double-check that you’re asking the right questions and focused on the right KPIs (like not confusing the different wants and needs of different types of users, “averages” of multiple groups are usually an idiot’s metric). Once you’ve done that, run an A/B test or two, and maybe try comparing cold-turkey removal of “GenAI” to your status quo.

Google is known for running more than a hundred A/B tests in parallel on their search engine alone, and when it comes to reducing the amount of “GenAI” BS down to the things that at least some specific segments of your users actually want, A/B testing is a useful tool in the toolbox.

One of the biggest moments of applause you could hear in some past announcements from places like Google or Microsoft was the simple addition of a “Dark mode”, a simple UX theme change. The removal of “GenAI” from UX can and will see the same level of positive reaction from many, if not most, segments of users.

The applause will likely be louder among users of software from places like Adobe, where the UX designers deserve to be (...redacted...) at a bare minimum for the big obscenely colored and impossible to remove “Ask AI Assistant” button. There is no excuse for such things, and to say that users “Hate” them for it is a significant understatement.

If the idea of users of your software genuinely and passionately Hating you (with a capital H) seems alien to you, then you really don’t understand users or clients at all. They can, and they do. How much they do depends on you.

If you’re Microsoft, you hire Sadists to design every UX, and if your company was bought by Microsoft (LinkedIn, OpenAI \[49%\], etc.), you try to inject that Sadism anywhere that you can. If you’re anyone else, hopefully you don’t aim for Sadism Maximization, and if you don’t, it is time to start trimming away the “AI features” that your users hate you for.

Failure to measure the hate of your users doesn’t mean that it isn’t there.
