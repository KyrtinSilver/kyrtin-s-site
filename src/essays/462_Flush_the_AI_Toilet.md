Since the end of 2022, the world has seen tech giants and startups do one thing over and over again in the AI domain, double-down on hype. At first, this was largely disregarded as being par for the course with the PR campaigns of most any other industry, but that was a mistake.

Sure, people rapidly debunked absurd claims quickly in the first few months (like the “Sparks of AGI” fraud from #MicroSlop), but those claims kept proliferating far faster than they could be debunked, and bad actors began building upon those debunked claims for added layers of obfuscation.

The difference is exponential, not in the capacities of popular AI technology, but in the exponentially growing divide between our observable reality and the things that people talk up and sell in the AI space, so desperately attempting to indoctrinate others into believing them.

The CEOs of the world’s largest tech companies and startups are constantly contradicting themselves and one another, being proven wrong, making one objectively false claim after another, and spinning financial narratives that are otherworldly delusional.

In the past several weeks:

-   #MicroSlop’s (Microsoft) CEO begged people to come up with a killer app to justify their blatantly idiotic investments in “AI Infrastructure” at DAVOS,
    
-   Nvidia’s CEO preached that businesses should ignore ROI concerns for their AI investments,
    
-   Anthropic and OpenAI are attempting to differentiate themselves via a shallow rivalry, despite being two apples from the same rotten tree,
    
-   Nature.com published a deeply fraudulent article claiming that AI had surpassed human intelligence (not their first dive into fraud by any measure, mind you),
    
-   “MoltBook” has given rise to countless absurdities of hype, fear, comical memes, and mindless investment,
    
-   Oracle has claimed that “Everything is fine”, and that they aren’t financially impacted by Nvidia reneging on their defunct $100 billion investment in OpenAI
    

The rate and intensity of fraud is accelerating, as OpenAI attempts to reach an IPO before the bubble bursts, and as major tech giants hosting the cloud infrastructure jockey for the best position to reduce their losses when it does.

In the 2008 financial crisis, Iceland was a paragon in terms of their response to the guilty parties. They sent the guilty bankers to prison for their crimes. The AI bubble is going to be far worse, and the rate of “AI Infrastructure” projects being delayed or cancelled is already accelerating, up from 16 in December to 25 in January.

There are going to be a lot of guilty parties to hold to account, for a much larger bubble, with much more severe fallout. Those guilty parties are screaming louder than ever right now, as they continue to double-down on these things, even as the opportunities for remotely plausible cash grabs evaporate.
