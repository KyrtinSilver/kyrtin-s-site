Democracy is not equal to Bureaucracy, but rather Bureaucracy is almost the exact opposite of Governance in general, as it epitomizes common failure modes of Governance.

Bureaucracy is perhaps more aptly summarized as the systemization of parasitic absurdity. It leans heavily on the fallacy claiming that “Governments have a Monopoly on Violence”, and across history Bureaucrats have gone to great lengths to make even invading forces dependent upon them, which succeeded more often than it failed.

See David Graeber’s Book “The Utopia of Rules” for an extensive look at the subject. I also highly recommend “Bullshit Jobs”, which is directly applicable, as well as his other exceptional works, such as “Debt: The First 5,000 years” and “The Dawn of Everything”.

Bureaucracy tends to systematically collect (hoard) and enshrine absurdities, whose purpose is, above all else, to give them new distractions, and new excuses for expansion.

Take the concept of “Insurance” for example, as that concept has been made “legally mandatory” in many respects within countries such as the US.

I’m reminded of a moment from Terry Pratchett’s first book in the Discworld series, “The Colour of Magic”, where this alien concept of “Insurance” is being explained to someone. In summary, it is gambling. A bet is being placed on whether or not some event will happen, like a more limited version of social gambling platforms like “Polymarket”.

While it may seem counter-intuitive, the US doesn’t only have legalized gambling, it has legally mandatory gambling, across multiple sectors at that. Much like many casinos “of ill repute”, the insurance industry there, as a whole, is also overflowing with scams and white-collar crime of all kinds, with no small part of those ill-gotten gains funnelling into bribery for government officials. That is to say, Bureaucrats.

Leaving the rest of that rabbit hole of absurdities for comedians and satirical writers, it is worth focusing on one of the highly visible features of many of the most egregious criminal Bureaucrats. They systematically ignore what amounts, in the broader sense, to the public’s “User Experience (UX)”.

A competent system would be designed for interoperability, modularity, and ease of updating. In contrast, Bureaucratic systems routinely increase the burdens of “paperwork” that fall on all other people, even though technology makes it ever easier to decrease, not increase, that burden. (More on this in the next post)

This is a common reason why naïve attempts to automate Bureaucracies fail, because the observable dynamics of Bureaucracy are for them to expand (bloat) and progressively degenerate in terms of efficiency and transparency. The degeneration of transparency is itself often as much a by-product of Combinatorial Absurdity as it is malevolent intentions, that is a combinatorial problem where the options and factors to combine are themselves fundamentally absurd.
