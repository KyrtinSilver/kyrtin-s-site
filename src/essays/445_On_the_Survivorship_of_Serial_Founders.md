Survivorship Bias plays an extreme and unspoken role in determining who rules entire markets. Those who aim at typical “Exits” (IPO, M&A, etc.) for their companies from the start might not fail, but they impose a hard upper limit on their own success.

Many “Serial Founders” rinse and repeat the cycle of peddling one mediocre or niche\` company after another, “exiting” just before the gaping maw of an upper echelon company swallows it whole. They can make plenty of money this way, but they never reach the top.

Over recent years less companies have taken aim at IPOs, with some noteworthy public companies aiming to return to their prior private status. In more volatile markets bad actors hold the advantage, as they have more levers by which they can trigger volatile reactions, including those with no grounding in reality, nor any real legal accountability for pulling them.

In terms of liquidity for the benefit of shareholders, companies turning any reasonable profit can easily manage stock buyback options, as well as paying dividends to encourage holding onto equity long-term. Liquidity has numerous sound options that don’t involve IPOs or M&A, both of which damage the long-term potential of a company. Beliefs to the contrary of this are usually derivative of small sample sizes and luck, or one or more forms of Survivorship Bias.

Publicly traded tech giants are directly subject to economic bubbles, meaning that their stock values are inflated, but those bubbles burst just as frequently, on top of the high vulnerability to random events, such as the numerous instances where a single demo or conference presentation cost a major tech giant over $100 billion in market cap within hours of the event occurring.

Those vulnerabilities only become more acute as time moves forward, since the understanding of how to artificially create such moments improves, and the financial incentive to do so increases. This casts a growing shadow over all such major publicly traded companies, each of whom has a target painted on them.

“Serial Founders” cash out before the risk truly hits them, and they may become wealthy in doing so, but they’ll never become an economic force driving any given industry. This also means that investments in serial founders have a hard upper limit on the returns that may be realistically expected of them, as they are “bread and butter” companies, not 1,000x return cash cows.

Of course, the more ideal investment funds tend to be a mix of the two. You have your reliable “Serial Founders” for lower risk and lower returns, as well as your actual innovators, the ones who don’t fit the typical mold, and by breaking that mold may become the 1,000x return companies to propel larger fund investments over the 3x ROI bar.

Serial Founders don’t tend to give you Blue Ocean Markets, but they are still decent for diversifying.
