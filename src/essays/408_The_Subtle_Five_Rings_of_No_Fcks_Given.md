Someone recently messaged me with some good questions, asking what price I pay for being my unapologetic self, writing what I write, the way that I write it (even in peer review), as well as how I handle rejection. The answer may be worth sharing more broadly.

The price may be split between:

Costs already paid: Applicant Tracking Systems have hated me for at least a decade already, with inexplicable rejection rates (calculated over thousands of applications) back when I was looking. Some of this may be traced back to nonsensical Cognitive Biases baked into them, like a bias against unique names and name changes, as well as those strongly against atypical career paths and favoring (or worse, requiring) worthless college degrees.

Self-Selection: Many of those who the content turns away are the people I’d want nothing to do with in the first place, so this saves me time.

Business: In this we control the deciding technology, so people can walk away for any reason, be permanently blacklisted from our technology, subsequently bankrupted, and held accountable for all of their actions in due time. The choice matters a great deal for them, and very little for us, in a dramatic asymmetry, as a more than sufficient number will apply our technology given the chance, whether they like my phrasing or not.

The benefits in turn are that I can point out the things that I observe and how they connect and interact over time, saying the rare thing or the thing from a rare perspective, which adds greater value for any reader. It adds the most value to my team’s systems in the long run, as it provides a unique and higher fidelity blueprint of my thought process, allowing that process to be made digital and “alive”, operating at speed and scale, with full recursive self-improvement.

As for handling rejection, multiple colleagues telling me “I saw the book The Subtle Art of Not Giving a F\*ck, and immediately thought of you” led me to read that book, which I seemed to embody. Every non-sociopathic human does still technically care about how they are perceived to some degree, but aggressively pruning trolls, the mentally unstable, and other wastes of time is a healthy measure to cultivate a sound mind.

In my case, having a great deal more “power”, or more genuine power perhaps, than people yet realize helps me to simply roll my eyes and move on from various absurdities. By rejection, people often unwittingly harm themselves, thinking that they harm me, which I allow to pass unnoticed via omission.

That asymmetry of perceived and actual power, when allowed to move quietly unnoticed, is another useful strategy. 99.99% of people aren’t going to know what this means, nor will I clarify it for them, as this is not mentioned for their benefit.

In general, Sun Tzu, Miyamoto Musashi, and many more classical sources of strategy may be applied far more often than most people ever consciously do. These number among the seed material for our systems.
