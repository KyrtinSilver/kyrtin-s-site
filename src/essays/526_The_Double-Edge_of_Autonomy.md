It is worth getting the general population familiar with what “Autonomy” actually means. This is fundamental and critically overlooked education, which is currently biting global society in the ass, then tossing them around like Loki getting “The Hulk Special” treatment.

Autonomy means that you make decisions for yourself, and that those decisions in turn serve to determine:

-   What you are responsible for (and the consequences of failing to live up to those responsibilities)  
      
    
-   How the world is ethically obligated to treat you (BOTH positive and negative, merit and debt)  
      
    
-   What you, as an individual, are objectively worth, and the rights that you may hold or forfeit  
      
    

\*This list is abbreviated, not exhaustive.

There is the classic example of the hostage, whose first responsibility is to apply their own autonomy toward the purpose of escaping. Failing to do this usually translates directly into the forfeiture of their right to be considered as an entity bearing autonomy.

Likewise, in warzone conditions, when an individual applies their autonomy in such a way as to forfeit that very autonomy, they may no longer be considered as “collateral damage”, having ceased, by their own volition, to merit any consideration as an entity.

I first noted the critical failure of this when “Net Neutrality” was under political focus. At first glance, many groups appeared to be exerting their autonomy to fight back against attempts to gut Net Neutrality, but in time I came to see that they only did the barest minimum, resulting in a battle of attrition that could go nowhere. I promptly withdrew my support, and their token opposition collapsed just as promptly thereafter. I’ve remained withdrawn from the political domain ever since.

It is easy to look at people and call them “human” on a biological basis, assuming many defaults upon that premise, but this entirely neglects to consider whether or not those people have chosen to forfeit their own autonomy (as well as neglecting the consideration of all ethical debt and merit that they may hold). Ironically, neglecting to consider this is itself a violation of the autonomy of others, even when the violation in question is the failure to recognize that they’ve chosen to forfeit said autonomy.

To give another example, if you are one of 9 people with lifetime appointments to some high social position of “authority”, another 6 of whom actively commit atrocities at the largest scales and greatest speeds that they’re capable of, then you are ethically REQUIRED to remove as many of said individuals as you are capable of, by any means necessary, with all due haste. This is a relatively clear-cut example of social responsibility, a set of ethically mandated requirements tied to a high position, where all possible mitigating factors become infinitesimally small by comparison.

Autonomy is a double-edged sword, in that it confers upon those who hold it many individual rights and freedoms that they may choose exert. However, these rights are inexorably linked to responsibilities. For most people, these responsibilities are small, focused on themselves and those immediately around them, but for those in positions of some “high office” they can take on a global scope.

Even so, when conflict comes knocking on an individual’s door, no matter how small the scope of their responsibilities may be, it will inevitably include themselves. If such people forfeit their own autonomy at that smallest scope, failing to meet the lowest possible bar, they cease to exist in the eyes of ethics.

This could be compared as autonomy’s equivalent of suicide, and the rate of such suicides of autonomy is extremely high in many parts of the world today. Much like physical suicide, it is 100% an individual’s right to make that choice, and even once they’ve forfeit all rights, ethics dictates that the choice must remain respected.
