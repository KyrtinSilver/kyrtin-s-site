342 - Legal Hyper-Complexity and Misalignment

July 25, 2025

Image credit: Dilbert

Legal systems have been a frequent subject of interest in the Cognitive Bias literature, as they embody many highly visible forms of both typical Cognitive Biases and “Noise”, inconsistency derived from at least 3 distinct kinds of “noise” as documented by Daniel Kahneman and others. As legal systems are frequently intended for and/or conflated with concepts of “justice” or “fairness”, while also potentially having a high impact, systemic errors in these systems are frequently subject to greater emotional outrage than equally predictable errors elsewhere, explaining some portion of this focus within the research literature.

Legal systems are also a good example of where hyper-complexity, poor design & implementation, the vested interests of bad actors, and damaging time lags all come together to form a particularly toxic soup. In principle and theory they are often intended to enforce social order, norms, and morals, while supporting local culture. However, that toxic soup, on average, causes none of these to hold true today.

Legal systems clearly meet the definition of hyper-complexity noted previously: “... the region of complexity where no individual or group of humans is capable of effectively handling that complexity, using any combination of tools or methods currently at their disposal.”

In addition to this, they result from and update via a bureaucratic process, which is notoriously slow, poorly maintained, poorly integrated, and virtually never optimized. As morals, norms, cultures, and even matters of social order change over time, they are forced to drag these glacial systems kicking and screaming behind them, as very (and increasingly) heavy baggage.

Legal systems are only weakly related to moral systems, as virtually all values suffer heavy losses when translated into legal systems, and due to the time lag of this process even that value is frequently outdated by the time it is applied in whatever meager substance survives.

The result is that legal systems today, on average, serve precisely no one except the people who create and maintain them. They are usually built to expand over time, gradually sequestering more of society’s resources while contributing little or nothing of any real value.

All of this isn’t intended to place the blame on bureaucrats or legal professionals. They’ve never had the tools required to handle hyper-complexity, nor has any other industry. Although “GenAI” contributes nothing to this problem space, while also inflicting significant harm upon it, technology capable of handling hyper-complexity was demonstrated years before ChatGPT.

Comments:

The human mind isn’t scalable, it has to fit within your skull, and it can’t grow and shrink on demand, or connect to the cloud. However, human-like intelligence was achieved with the Independent Core Observer Model (ICOM) cognitive architecture as far back as 2019 when the 7th generation was deployed “in the wild”, to freely interact with the internet and random people therein. The final milestone for that system in January 2022 was demonstrating parity with a team of junior consultants from a major consulting firm, while operating with only 64 GB of SDRAM and less than 2 TB of hard drive space for a graph database. Humans aren’t scalable, but our 8th generation of ICOM will be, it is just a matter of full-time engineering hours now.
